# Questions to ask before meeting an equity release adviser

Walking into an equity release appointment without preparation is a bit like signing a contract you haven't read. The adviser may be perfectly competent and entirely straightforward, but equity release is a long-term financial commitment and you'll get far more from the meeting if you've thought through what you want to know beforehand. This guide covers the credentials worth checking, what to bring, and the specific questions that tend to matter most.

If you're still getting to grips with how equity release works as a product, our [equity release explained pillar guide](/equity-release/) is worth reading first.

## What credentials should you check before the meeting?

Start here, before you spend any time preparing questions.

Any adviser giving regulated advice on equity release must be authorised by the Financial Conduct Authority. You can verify this in about two minutes on the FCA Register at register.fca.org.uk. Search by name or firm. If they're not there, that ends the conversation.

Beyond FCA authorisation, look for membership of the Equity Release Council. Council members are bound by product standards that protect you: no negative equity guarantee, the right to remain in your home for life, the right to move the plan to a suitable new property, and fixed or capped interest rates on lifetime mortgages. These aren't optional extras; they're the floor for any reputable product. An adviser who recommends plans outside Council standards should be able to explain precisely why, and that explanation should be very good indeed.

Some advisers also hold the Certificate in Equity Release (CeRER), a specialist qualification. It's not legally required, but it signals that the person across the table has done more than the minimum.

## What should you bring to the appointment?

Advisers work faster and more accurately when they have real numbers in front of them rather than rough guesses.

Bring a recent mortgage statement if you still have a balance outstanding. This matters because equity release proceeds are typically used to clear any existing mortgage, and the adviser needs to know the redemption figure. A recent utility bill or council tax statement also helps for identity verification purposes, which all regulated firms are required to carry out.

A rough sense of your income helps too: state pension letters, any private pension statements, details of any benefits you receive. Equity release can affect means-tested benefits such as Pension Credit or Council Tax Reduction, so this information allows the adviser to flag any interaction early rather than after you've made a decision.

Finally, and this is worth doing before you arrive: write down what you actually want the money for and how much you think you need. Not a vague figure, but a specific one. "Roughly £40,000 to clear the mortgage and do the roof" is a far better starting point than "some cash to help out." Precision helps the adviser model the right amount and avoids you releasing more than you need, which compounds over time in a way that matters.

## What questions should you ask about costs?

The costs in equity release come from several directions, and not all of them are obvious at first glance.

Ask specifically: what is the total adviser fee, in pounds, for this transaction? Some advisers charge a flat fee; others charge a percentage of the amount released. A percentage model can create an incentive to encourage you to borrow more than you need. I'd suggest you want to understand exactly how the adviser is paid before you go any further.

Ask about the lender's arrangement fee. Most lifetime mortgage providers charge one, typically in the range of £600 to £1,500, and it's often added to the loan rather than paid upfront, which means it compounds alongside the interest.

Ask about valuation fees, legal fees (yours and sometimes the lender's), and whether there's any early repayment charge should you want to repay part or all of the loan later. Early repayment charges on lifetime mortgages can be significant, particularly in the first ten years. Some plans now allow voluntary partial repayments of up to 10% per year without penalty, which can slow the compounding considerably.

The interest rate question deserves its own moment of focus. A £100,000 lifetime mortgage at 6% compounds to around £321,000 over 20 years. At 7%, the same loan becomes approximately £387,000. That difference matters to your estate and to your options later in life. Ask the adviser to show you an illustration of the balance at 10, 15 and 20 years, not just the initial figure.

:::callout
**On compound interest:** The Equity Release Council requires lenders to provide a personalised Key Facts Illustration (KFI) before you proceed. This document shows the projected balance over time at the offered rate. Read it carefully. If anything in it surprises you, ask before you sign.
:::

## What questions should you ask about alternatives?

A good adviser should raise alternatives themselves. If they don't, ask directly.

The alternatives to equity release worth discussing include: downsizing to a smaller property (sometimes dismissed too quickly, worth revisiting if you're in a house larger than you need), taking out a standard residential mortgage if your income supports one, using savings or investments if you have them, or checking whether you're receiving all the benefits you're entitled to. Age UK's benefits calculator is a reasonable starting point for the last of these.

Ask the adviser: "Have you considered whether downsizing would meet my needs?" and "Am I eligible for any state benefits or local authority grants that would reduce the amount I need to borrow?" A thorough adviser will have thought about both. One who steers quickly back to the product without engaging with these questions is worth being cautious about.

## What questions should you ask about the specific product?

Not all lifetime mortgages are identical. Aviva, Legal & General, More2Life, Pure Retirement and Key Later Life Finance each have their own product features, rate structures and flexibility options, and the adviser should be comparing options from across the market rather than from a single provider.

Ask whether the adviser has access to the whole market or only a panel. A whole-of-market adviser considers all available products; a panel adviser is limited to a smaller selection. Both can give regulated advice, but you should know which you're dealing with.

Ask whether the plan allows interest payments. Voluntary monthly or quarterly interest payments prevent the balance from compounding at all, which can make a substantial difference to what's left in the estate. Not every plan offers this feature; not every borrower can afford it. But if you can, it's worth modelling.

Ask about the drawdown facility if you don't need all the money at once. A drawdown lifetime mortgage releases an initial amount with a reserve facility you can access later. You only pay interest on what you've actually drawn, which reduces the compounding effect compared to taking a large lump sum upfront.

:::callout
**Right to remain:** Under Equity Release Council standards, you have the right to remain in your property for life or until you move into long-term care, regardless of how much the loan balance grows. Any plan your adviser recommends should carry this guarantee. Ask them to confirm it explicitly.
:::

## What questions should you ask about the process from here?

Understanding the timeline reduces stress later.

Ask the adviser how long the process typically takes from application to completion. Six to eight weeks is common; some cases run longer if there are property complications or solicitor delays. If you have a specific date in mind (clearing a mortgage before a fixed rate expires, for example), raise this early.

Ask whether you'll need to appoint your own solicitor. Equity Release Council standards require that borrowers receive independent legal advice before completing a plan. This is a genuine protection, not a formality. A solicitor who specialises in later-life matters will go through the document with you and confirm you understand what you're signing. Your adviser may be able to suggest names, but the choice of solicitor is yours.

Ask what happens if your circumstances change before completion. Life doesn't stop because you've started an application. Knowing what flexibility exists, or doesn't, is useful to know in advance.

## One question worth asking yourself before you go

This isn't a question for the adviser; it's one for you, or perhaps you and your family, before the appointment.

Does everyone who might be affected by this decision know it's happening?

If you have a partner, adult children who might expect to inherit the property, or anyone else with a close interest in your housing situation, the time to have that conversation is before you commit, not after. I've seen cases where families were blindsided by an equity release arrangement, and it creates difficulties that didn't need to exist. The money is yours and the decision is yours, but that conversation tends to go better when it happens early.
