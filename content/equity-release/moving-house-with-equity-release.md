# Can you move house if you have an equity release plan?

Yes, in most cases you can move house without repaying your equity release plan early. The majority of lifetime mortgages sold in the UK include a portability clause, which lets you transfer the loan to a new property rather than settle it when you sell. The catch is that your lender has to approve the move, and the new home has to meet their criteria. If it doesn't, you're looking at full repayment and, very likely, early repayment charges.

Worth reading the detail before you fall in love with a property.

## What does "portability" actually mean?

Portability means your existing lifetime mortgage travels with you to your new home, on the same interest rate and terms. You don't redeem the loan at completion; instead, the security simply shifts from the old property to the new one.

All plans approved by the Equity Release Council must offer portability as a standard feature. That's been the case since the Council's product standards were updated, and it applies to any plan with the ERC quality mark. If you took out a plan before the Council's standards were widely adopted, or through a lender who isn't a member, portability may not be guaranteed. Check your mortgage offer documents, or ask your original adviser.

The mechanics in practice: you notify your lender that you're moving, they instruct a valuation of the new property, and if everything passes their criteria, the loan is formally transferred at the point of legal completion. Your solicitor and the lender's solicitor handle the paperwork, much like a standard property transaction.

## What criteria does the new property have to meet?

This is where moves can run into difficulty. Lenders aren't obliged to accept every property, and most have fairly specific rules.

**Minimum value.** The new property must usually be worth at least as much as the outstanding loan, with a comfortable loan-to-value ratio left over. If you're downsizing significantly and the loan has grown through compound interest over several years, a small flat may no longer provide adequate security. Some lenders set a minimum property value (often around £70,000 to £100,000, though this varies).

**Construction type.** Standard brick-and-mortar properties are generally straightforward. Problems arise with non-standard construction: timber frame, prefabricated concrete, thatched roofs, steel frame, properties above commercial premises, or homes with a short lease. Leasehold properties with fewer than 70 or 75 years remaining on the lease at the end of the mortgage term are often declined outright.

**Condition and use.** The property must be in a habitable condition and used as your main residence. Holiday lets, properties you plan to rent out, or homes with significant structural issues will typically fail the assessment.

**Location.** A small number of lenders apply restrictions on rural properties, particularly those in flood-risk areas or with no mains services. This isn't universal, but it does come up.

If you're buying somewhere that might sit outside these parameters, I'd strongly suggest raising it with your lender or an FCA-authorised equity release adviser before you make an offer. Finding out at the point of exchange is far more stressful than finding out at the viewing stage.

:::callout
**Before you make an offer on a new property,** contact your lender and ask them to confirm in writing whether the property type and location you're considering would be acceptable security. Most lenders will give a preliminary view, though final approval is always subject to valuation.
:::

## What if the new property doesn't qualify?

If the new home fails the lender's criteria and they won't port the loan, you'll have to repay the mortgage in full when you sell. That means settling the outstanding balance, including all the rolled-up interest that has accumulated.

For a loan taken out five or more years ago, that figure can be considerably higher than the original amount borrowed. A £100,000 lifetime mortgage at 5% compound interest grows to around £128,000 after five years, and to roughly £165,000 after ten. These are not small numbers when you're also trying to fund a purchase.

On top of repaying the balance, you may face early repayment charges.

## How do early repayment charges work on lifetime mortgages?

Early repayment charges (ERCs) exist because lifetime mortgage lenders fund their loans over the long term, typically by issuing bonds linked to gilt rates. If you repay early, the lender may face a loss on their funding position. ERCs are their way of recovering that cost.

There are broadly two types:

**Fixed ERCs** taper over a set number of years. A typical structure might charge 5% of the outstanding balance if you repay in years one to five, 4% in years six to seven, 3% in years eight to nine, and nothing after that. Aviva, Legal & General and Pure Retirement all use fixed-ERC structures on various products, though the exact schedules differ between plans.

**Gilt-based (or market value adjustment) ERCs** are harder to predict because they move with interest rates. If gilt yields have fallen since you took out your plan, the ERC can be significant. If they've risen, the charge may be minimal or even zero. More2Life and some older plans from other providers have used this structure. The problem is that you cannot know in advance what you'll owe.

Equity Release Council standards do require that no ERC is charged in the event of death or entry into long-term care. Moving house, however, is a different matter - unless the new property simply cannot be accepted for reasons outside your control, in which case some lenders may waive the charge at their discretion. "May" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Don't bank on it.

Always request a redemption statement in writing before exchanging contracts on your current home. That gives you the precise figure you'd owe, and it's valid for a specific period (usually 28 days). Your solicitor needs this figure.

## What if I'm downsizing and the new property is worth less?

Downsizing is one of the most common reasons people with lifetime mortgages want to move, and it introduces an extra layer of complexity.

If the outstanding loan is higher than the value of the new property, portability isn't possible in full. Some lenders will allow a partial port, where you transfer what the new property can support and repay the remainder. Others require full repayment. Key Later Life Finance, which acts as a broker and adviser rather than a direct lender, has written a fair amount on this scenario, and it's worth speaking to an adviser who handles these cases regularly.

A few lenders have introduced downsizing protection features. Under these, if you want to move to a property that doesn't meet the lending criteria, the ERC is waived, provided the plan has been in force for a minimum number of years (often five or more). Aviva's product range includes a version of this. It's worth checking whether your plan has this feature - it may not be the headline selling point, but it can save a meaningful sum.

## What's the process when you move with a lifetime mortgage?

Once you've had a preliminary indication that the new property should be acceptable, the practical steps are relatively familiar.

You instruct a solicitor with experience in equity release transactions. Your current lender needs to be notified formally that you intend to port the loan. They'll instruct an independent valuation of the new property. Assuming that passes, the legal teams on both sides co-ordinate the transfer of security at completion, synchronised with your purchase and sale.

The process typically adds two to four weeks to what you'd expect from a standard transaction. It isn't enormously complicated, but it does require solicitors who know what they're doing. Not every high-street conveyancer handles equity release portability routinely, and a firm that's unfamiliar with the documentation can cause delays.

Your existing lender won't charge a new arrangement fee in most cases, though they may charge a valuation fee for the new property. Ask upfront.

:::callout
**Choosing a solicitor:** Look for a firm on the Equity Release Council's list of approved solicitors, or ask your equity release adviser for a referral. A solicitor who handles these transfers regularly will understand exactly what the lender requires and where delays typically occur.
:::

## A note on taking further advice

If you're considering a move and you have a lifetime mortgage, the single most useful thing you can do early on is speak to an FCA-authorised equity release adviser. Not your estate agent, not your conveyancer, and not a general financial adviser who handles equity release occasionally.

An adviser who specialises in this area can pull your plan documents, identify the ERC structure, check whether downsizing protection applies, and approach your lender for a preliminary property assessment. That's work that costs relatively little upfront and can prevent an expensive surprise later.

For a broader overview of how lifetime mortgages work and what to consider before taking one out, see our [equity release pillar guide](/equity-release/).

The Equity Release Council's website also publishes plain-English guidance on portability standards, and the Money Helper service (run by the Money and Pensions Service, a government-backed body) has an equity release section worth reading before any significant decision.

Moving house is stressful enough without a financial complication sitting underneath it. The good news is that, for most people with a relatively recent plan from a Council-member lender, portability is a genuine option. You just need to do the homework before you fall for the new kitchen.
