# Alternatives to equity release you might not have considered

Equity release is not the only route available to homeowners who need money in later life. Depending on what you actually need the money for, options such as downsizing, renting a room, an unclaimed benefits check or a Disabled Facilities Grant may give you what you're looking for without the long-term costs that come with a lifetime mortgage. This article runs through the main alternatives honestly, including where each one falls short.

If you do want to understand how equity release itself works first, the [equity release pillar guide](/equity-release/) covers the mechanics in full.

---

## Why it's worth pausing before you apply

A £150,000 lifetime mortgage at 6.5% interest, compounded annually, becomes roughly £522,000 owed after 20 years. That is not a reason to rule it out, but it is a reason to make sure you've looked at everything else first.

Equity release is a perfectly legitimate financial product. Providers regulated by the Equity Release Council must offer a no-negative-equity guarantee, the right to remain in your home for life, and the ability to move the mortgage to a suitable new property. Those protections matter. But the compound interest is real, and it erodes the inheritance you might want to leave, or the equity you might need later for care costs.

Start by asking a cleaner question: what do I actually need this money to do?

---

## Could downsizing do the same job?

For many people, it can. Selling a larger home and buying something smaller releases cash as a lump sum with no interest, no monthly payments and no debt attached to your estate.

The numbers can be substantial. The average gap between a three-bedroom and a two-bedroom property in England is over £60,000 according to ONS house price data, and in parts of the South East it runs considerably higher. If you're rattling around in a four-bedroom house, the gap could be six figures.

The honest downsides are real, though. Moving costs — estate agent fees, solicitor fees, stamp duty if you're buying above the threshold, removal costs — typically absorb £10,000 to £15,000. And that's before the emotional cost of leaving a home you may have lived in for decades.

Downsizing also takes time. A property chain can take six months or more to complete. If you need money quickly for care, for a repair or to help a child onto the housing ladder, downsizing isn't fast.

That said, for people in their late 60s or early 70s who are open to moving and want a clean break from debt, it is the option I'd suggest looking at first. Not because it's right for everyone, but because the arithmetic is genuinely simpler.

---

## Have you checked your benefits entitlement recently?

This is the alternative that gets overlooked most often, and it can be the most immediately useful.

Age UK estimates that around £2.5 billion in Pension Credit goes unclaimed every year in Great Britain. Attendance Allowance, which is specifically for people aged 65 and over with a physical or mental disability, is also commonly missed. Council Tax Reduction can reduce a bill by up to 100% for eligible households. These are not trivial sums.

Pension Credit alone, for a single person, currently tops up income to a minimum of £218.15 a week (2024/25 figure from GOV.UK). If you're not receiving it and your income is below that level, you're leaving money on the table that the government has set aside for you.

:::callout
**Free benefits check:** Age UK's online benefits calculator at ageuk.org.uk takes around 10 minutes. Alternatively, call their free helpline on 0800 678 1602 and they'll run through entitlements with you. If you'd rather speak to someone independent, Turn2us (turn2us.org.uk) offers the same service. Do this before you speak to any equity release provider.
:::

The reason this matters in the context of equity release is that taking out a lifetime mortgage can actually reduce your benefits entitlement. A large cash lump sum can affect means-tested benefits for a period, depending on how quickly you spend it and what you spend it on. Getting the benefits picture clear first changes the maths.

## What about the Disabled Facilities Grant?

If the reason you're thinking about equity release is to fund home adaptations, a Disabled Facilities Grant may cover some or all of the cost without you borrowing a penny.

The grant is administered by local councils in England and is means-tested. The maximum in England is £30,000, though some councils can top this up from their own funds. In Wales the maximum is £36,000; in Northern Ireland it differs again. Scotland has its own scheme called Care and Repair.

It covers adaptations including stairlifts, wet rooms, widened doorways, ramp access and ceiling hoists. You don't have to own your home outright to apply. Tenants can apply too, with landlord consent.

The process starts with a referral to an occupational therapist (OT) from your GP or directly through your council's social care team. The OT assesses what you need; the council then assesses what you're eligible for. It's not fast, and there are waiting lists in many areas, but the money is a grant, not a loan. Nothing has to be repaid.

For people whose equity release interest is specifically about funding adaptations, this route is worth exhausting before borrowing.

---

## Renting out a room — the numbers are better than you'd think

If you have a spare bedroom, renting it out under the government's Rent a Room scheme lets you earn up to £7,500 per year tax-free. That's not a lump sum, but as a way of generating regular income it's worth considering.

At £625 a month, you could cover a significant portion of day-to-day costs. Some people rent to a lodger long-term; others use platforms like SpareRoom to let short-term.

The practical complications are real. You're sharing your home, which is a significant lifestyle change. There are insurance implications (check with your home insurer before you advertise). Your mortgage lender, if you still have one, may need to be notified. And some people simply don't want a stranger in the house, which is entirely reasonable.

But for someone who has the space, is comfortable with company, and needs income rather than capital, it's a meaningful option that many people dismiss too quickly.

---

## Borrowing from family: it can work, but structure it properly

Informal loans from adult children are more common than people admit, and there's nothing wrong with them in principle. The problems tend to arise when the arrangement is vague.

If a child lends a parent £40,000 with a handshake understanding that it'll "come out of the estate", what happens if the parent lives another 25 years? What happens if the lending child's own circumstances change and they need the money back? What happens if there are other siblings who weren't involved?

A written loan agreement doesn't need to be drafted by a solicitor, though that's the cleanest approach. At minimum it should set out the amount, the date, whether interest applies, and the repayment terms. Some families formalise this as a deed of loan so it's properly recorded for probate.

This is also an area where the tax position can be relevant. A loan is not a gift for inheritance tax purposes, provided it's recorded as a loan and intends to be repaid. The difference matters if the estate is above the inheritance tax threshold.

I'd suggest anyone considering this route speaks to a solicitor for an hour, not because it's complicated, but because the paperwork protects the relationship as much as it protects the money.

## Retirement interest-only mortgages: not equity release, but worth knowing about

A retirement interest-only mortgage (RIO) is a regulated mortgage product, not an equity release product. You borrow against your property and pay the interest monthly, just as you would with a standard interest-only mortgage. The capital is repaid when you sell the property, move into care or die.

Because you're servicing the interest, the debt doesn't compound. That's the key difference from a lifetime mortgage.

The catch is affordability. Lenders assess whether you can sustain the monthly payments on your retirement income. If your income is limited, you may not qualify. Providers include Nationwide, Bath Building Society and Hodge Bank, among others.

RIOs suit people who have sufficient regular income to cover interest payments and want to preserve their equity for the long term. They don't suit people whose income is unpredictable or who struggle to meet regular outgoings.

## How to think about all of this

None of these alternatives is a universal solution. Downsizing is disruptive. Benefits take time to claim. The Disabled Facilities Grant has waiting lists. Family loans need careful handling. RIOs require reliable income.

Equity release, done properly with an FCA-authorised adviser, is a legitimate choice for people who have considered the alternatives and found them wanting. The Equity Release Council's standards provide real consumer protections. Providers such as Aviva, Legal & General and Pure Retirement offer products that have been through regulatory scrutiny.

The point isn't to avoid equity release at all costs. It's to make sure you've actually looked at what else might work before you commit to a product where compound interest will run for the rest of your life.

A good starting point is a free, no-obligation conversation with an independent later-life finance adviser who can look at the full picture: benefits entitlement, any grant eligibility, your income, your assets and your actual goals. Key Later Life Finance and Age Partnership both offer initial consultations, as do many independent firms. Any advice you receive must come from an FCA-authorised adviser.
