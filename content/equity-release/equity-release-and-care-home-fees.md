# Does equity release affect eligibility for care home funding?

If you've already taken out equity release, or are wondering whether to do so, the short answer is this: any cash sitting in your bank account from a lifetime mortgage or home reversion plan counts as your capital for local authority care funding purposes. Once your assessable capital exceeds £23,250 in England (the figures differ slightly in Wales and Scotland), the council will not contribute to your care costs until that capital is spent down. That's not a reason to panic, but it is a reason to plan carefully and take regulated advice before making any move.

## How does the local authority means test actually work?

When someone applies for residential care and asks the local authority to contribute, the council carries out a financial assessment. In England this follows the Care Act 2014 and associated regulations. The council looks at two things: your income (pensions, benefits, annuity payments) and your capital (savings, investments, and, after an initial period, property).

The capital thresholds in England as of 2025 are:

- Above £23,250: you pay all your own care costs ("self-funder")
- Between £14,250 and £23,250: you receive partial support on a sliding scale
- Below £14,250: capital is largely disregarded and the council funds your assessed needs

These thresholds have not risen in line with inflation for many years. Age UK publishes updated figures each April and is worth bookmarking if you're planning ahead.

Your main home is, in most situations, **disregarded for the first 12 weeks** of a permanent residential care placement. After that initial period it is brought into the assessment unless a "qualifying occupier" still lives there — a spouse or civil partner, a dependent child, or a relative aged 60 or over, among others. The full list is set out in the Care and Support (Charging and Assessment of Resources) Regulations 2014.

## Where does equity release money fit into this?

A lifetime mortgage doesn't change the ownership of your home. You retain the title deeds; the lender holds a charge against the property. That means the property itself continues to be valued in the same way it would be for any homeowner during a means test.

What changes is your liquid capital. Once the lender releases funds to you, those funds sit in your account. They are assessable capital from that moment.

Say you release £80,000 and hold £10,000 in existing savings. Your assessable capital is now £90,000. You are a self-funder and will remain so until that sum is spent down to £23,250. At current average care home costs of around £1,200 per week for a residential placement (LaingBuisson 2024 data), that drawdown could take several years. But the interest on your lifetime mortgage is compounding throughout. A £80,000 loan at 6% compounds to roughly £143,000 in ten years, £256,000 in twenty.

None of this makes equity release straightforwardly wrong. It does make the timing and sequencing of decisions genuinely important.

:::callout
**Worth knowing.** If you took equity release some time ago and spent the funds on home improvements, holidays or topping up income, those funds are no longer in your account and are not assessable. The means test looks at what capital you hold at the time of assessment, not what you received in the past. However, deliberate "deprivation of assets" rules apply if a council believes assets were disposed of specifically to reduce a care bill. GOV.UK guidance on deprivation of assets sets out how councils assess this.
:::

## What is a deferred payment agreement, and is it worth comparing?

A deferred payment agreement (DPA) is offered by local authorities in England under a duty created by the Care Act 2014. Where your capital (excluding the home) falls below £23,250 but you own property, the council can fund your care costs and place a legal charge on your home. The debt is repaid when the property eventually sells, either during your lifetime or from your estate.

It is not a loan product in the conventional sense. The interest rate is set nationally by the government (capped at a fixed rate reviewed annually by the Department of Health and Social Care) and has historically been significantly below commercial equity release rates. In 2024/25 the rate was 3.45% on the deferred amount.

The key distinction from equity release: interest accrues only on the care costs the council is covering, not on the full value of your home. That's a narrower debt base by some margin.

There are conditions. You need sufficient equity in the property to cover projected costs. The home generally needs to be unoccupied or owned outright. Your council must agree. And DPAs don't cover everything — there's usually a "personal expenses allowance" element you manage yourself, and the agreement won't fund care above your assessed needs level if you choose a more expensive home.

For many families looking at this question, a DPA is worth understanding before reaching for equity release. Whether it's appropriate depends on your specific circumstances, which is a conversation for a regulated care fees adviser rather than a general equity release broker.

## If I already hold a lifetime mortgage, does that complicate things?

It can. The most direct complication is the capital position described above. But there's a second issue worth understanding.

Equity Release Council member products (which include products from Aviva, Legal & General, More2Life, Pure Retirement and others) carry a **right to remain** guarantee. You can live in your home for life, or until you move into long-term care. When you do move into permanent care, the property is typically sold and the loan plus accumulated interest repaid from the proceeds.

That's fine in principle. The complication is the pace of interest accumulation between the point you move into care and the point the property sells. Probate, estate administration and property sales don't always move quickly. Any delay means more interest.

Some lifetime mortgage products from providers such as Key Later Life Finance and Pure Retirement allow drawdown facilities rather than a single lump sum. If you haven't yet drawn the full facility, you're only paying interest on what you've actually drawn. That's a relevant detail when thinking about care cost scenarios.

:::callout
**One thing people often miss.** If your partner or spouse remains in the family home after you move into care, the property is disregarded in the means test for as long as they live there. Your lifetime mortgage stays in place. Interest compounds. The eventual bill comes from the estate when the second person either moves into care or dies. Families sometimes discover this is larger than expected. It's worth discussing with an adviser well before that point.
:::

## Does it make sense to take equity release specifically to fund care?

I'd be cautious here, and I think most advisers would say the same. Equity release is generally designed for people who intend to remain in their home. If you're already at the point of moving into permanent residential care, or believe that's likely within a few years, the calculus changes.

You'd be taking a loan secured on an asset you'll be selling relatively soon, paying compound interest over a period you can't easily predict, and converting a disregarded asset (your home, during those first 12 weeks) into assessable liquid capital. That combination requires careful thought.

There are narrower scenarios where it might make sense. For example, funding a period of care at home (rather than residential) while you retain the property, or releasing a modest sum to cover a care home deposit while other arrangements are finalised. But these are specific situations, not a general pattern.

Any adviser covering this ground needs to be FCA-authorised. For the care funding side specifically, look for someone with the CF8 qualification (Certificate in Financial Planning: Long Term Care Insurance) or a member of the Society of Later Life Advisers (SOLLA). Our [main equity release guide](/equity-release/) covers how to find and verify regulated advisers.

## What should families do before a care funding assessment?

Timing matters more than most people realise. A few things worth addressing before or immediately after a care need becomes apparent:

1. Establish whether the person moving into care qualifies for **NHS Continuing Healthcare (CHC)**. If they do, the NHS funds all care costs regardless of assets. It's needs-based, not means-tested, and is frequently overlooked. The NHS website sets out the assessment process.
2. Check whether a qualifying person lives in the family home. If so, the property remains disregarded indefinitely and the picture is considerably simpler.
3. Ask the local authority about a deferred payment agreement before drawing down further equity release funds, particularly if care is already being considered.
4. If an equity release plan is already in place, request a current statement from the provider showing the outstanding loan plus accrued interest. Solicitors and financial advisers will need this for estate planning.

None of this is a substitute for regulated advice. Care funding sits at an awkward intersection of financial planning, benefits entitlements and property law. Getting one element wrong — drawing down funds at the wrong moment, missing a CHC assessment, or misunderstanding the deprivation of assets rules — can be genuinely costly.
