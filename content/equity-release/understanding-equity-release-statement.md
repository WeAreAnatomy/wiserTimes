# How to read your equity release annual statement

Once a year, usually around the anniversary of when your plan started, your provider will send you a statement. It is one of the most important documents you will receive — and, in my experience, one of the most misread. Most people glance at the balance, feel a flash of anxiety, and file it away. That is understandable, but it is worth ten minutes of your attention.

This guide walks through what the statement contains, how to check whether the figures are correct, and what to do if something does not look right.

## What does an equity release annual statement actually contain?

The exact layout differs between providers, but every Equity Release Council-compliant plan will show you at minimum:

- The outstanding balance at the start of the statement period
- Interest added during the year
- Any drawdowns you took (if you have a drawdown plan)
- Any voluntary repayments you made
- The outstanding balance at the end of the period

Some statements also include a running total of all interest charged since the plan began, which is useful context. Legal & General, for instance, shows a cumulative interest figure alongside the year-end balance. Aviva's statements tend to include a brief summary of your plan features on the reverse, including your fixed interest rate and whether your plan includes a drawdown facility.

If you took out your plan through a broker such as Key Later Life Finance, you may also receive a separate annual summary from them, though this is a courtesy, not a regulatory requirement.

## How to check your outstanding balance is correct

Start with the opening balance. This should match exactly the closing balance from your previous statement. If it does not, that discrepancy needs explaining before you look at anything else.

Next, look at the interest charged. Your statement will show either the annual interest rate or the monthly equivalent (or both). Do a rough sense check: multiply your opening balance by your annual interest rate as a decimal. On a £200,000 balance at 5.5%, you would expect to see roughly £11,000 in interest added. You won't get to the penny this way, because interest compounds monthly rather than annually, but you should be in the right ballpark. If the figure looks wildly out, call the provider.

If you have a drawdown lifetime mortgage and took additional funds during the year, those amounts should appear as separate line items with the date of each drawdown. Check these against any letters or confirmation emails you received at the time.

Voluntary repayments, if you made any, should also be itemised with dates. Verify these against your bank statements.

:::callout
**Keep a paper trail.** Every time you make a drawdown or a voluntary repayment, your provider should send a confirmation letter. Keep these in the same folder as your statements. It takes five minutes to set up and makes any future query much easier to resolve.
:::

## How compound interest builds up over time

This is the part of the statement that catches people off guard, especially if they have held their plan for several years.

Lifetime mortgages do not work like a standard repayment mortgage where the balance falls each month. No monthly payments are required (unless you opt into an interest-payment plan), so the interest rolls up. Each year, the interest is calculated on the previous year's total, not just the original loan. That is compound interest, and it accelerates.

Here is a concrete illustration. Say you borrowed £150,000 in 2015 at a fixed rate of 6.5%. By 2025, after ten years of rolling interest with no repayments, the outstanding balance would be in the region of £280,000. After twenty years, around £522,000. The loan has not grown because you did anything wrong. It has grown because that is precisely how the product is structured.

This is not a criticism of equity release. Many people find that the freedom the lump sum provided was well worth the accumulated interest. But it is worth understanding clearly when you look at your statement, because the balance will always be higher than you might intuitively expect.

If your plan was arranged recently, rates on many products sat between 5% and 7% for fixed lifetime mortgages through 2023 and 2024. If you are unsure of your exact rate, it will appear on the statement, usually near the top.

For a broader explanation of how lifetime mortgages work and what to expect over the life of a plan, see our [guide to equity release](/equity-release/).

## What the statement does not tell you

Your statement only shows the loan side of the equation.

It does not tell you what your property is worth today. It does not calculate your remaining equity. It will not tell you whether you are approaching the point at which the loan exceeds a significant proportion of your home's value.

To get a sense of where you stand on equity, you would need a current property valuation alongside the outstanding balance. Estate agents will usually provide an indicative figure at no cost, or you can commission a RICS-registered surveyor for a more formal assessment.

This matters particularly if you are considering whether to move home. Most modern plans are portable, meaning you can transfer the loan to a new property subject to it meeting the lender's criteria, but the maths depends on what the gap between loan and property value actually looks like. That is a conversation for an FCA-authorised adviser.

## Who to contact if something looks wrong

Start with your provider directly. The customer service number and address will be on the statement. Put your query in writing, whether by letter or email, and keep a copy. State clearly what you believe is wrong, quote the relevant figures from the statement, and ask for a written explanation.

Major providers such as Aviva, Pure Retirement and More2Life all have dedicated later-life lending customer service teams. They are, in my experience, generally responsive when contacted in writing. Phone calls are fine for initial queries, but anything substantive should go in writing.

If you are not satisfied with the provider's response, you have two further options. Age UK's telephone advice line (0800 678 1602) can offer general guidance. For a formal complaint, the Financial Ombudsman Service handles disputes about regulated financial products at no cost to you. You do not need a solicitor.

If your plan was arranged by a broker, they have an ongoing duty of care and should be willing to help you interpret your statement or liaise with the provider on your behalf.

:::callout
**Still have your original plan documents?** Your Key Facts Illustration (KFI) from when the plan was arranged will show projections of the outstanding balance at various points in time. It is worth comparing those original projections against your actual statement figures to sense-check whether the interest accumulation is running broadly as expected.
:::

## What to do if your circumstances have changed

Your annual statement is also a useful prompt to think about whether your situation has shifted since the plan was arranged.

If a partner has died and you are now the sole named borrower, contact your provider to update the records. The plan terms themselves should not change, but the paperwork needs to reflect the current position.

If you are considering moving into care, your plan's terms around early repayment become relevant. Early repayment charges on lifetime mortgages can be substantial, particularly in the first ten years. These charges should be documented in your original mortgage offer, and your statement may include a reminder of when any fixed early repayment charge period ends.

And if you find that your needs have changed and you want to consider releasing further equity or restructuring your arrangements, speak to an FCA-authorised equity release adviser before making any decisions. Products and rates available now may differ significantly from those available when your original plan was arranged, and it is not always straightforward to switch providers mid-plan.

## A note on paper versus online statements

Several providers now offer online account access as an alternative or supplement to paper statements. Aviva and Legal & General both allow plan holders to log in and view their balance at any time, which is genuinely useful if you want to check a figure without waiting for the annual letter.

That said, the annual statement is still the definitive record. If there is ever a discrepancy between what you see online and what appears on the formal statement, raise it immediately in writing. The paper statement is the document that counts.
