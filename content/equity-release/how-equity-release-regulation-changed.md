# How has equity release regulation changed over the years?

The equity release market of the 1980s and early 1990s was, by any honest assessment, a dangerous place for older homeowners. Some products left people owing more than their homes were worth, unable to move, and with no legal protection whatsoever. The market today, regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority and shaped by the Equity Release Council's voluntary standards, looks almost unrecognisably different. Understanding how that change happened matters, because it explains both why today's safeguards exist and what the industry once got badly wrong.

## What did equity release look like before regulation arrived?

The products that emerged in the 1980s were not inherently dishonest, but they were sold into a regulatory vacuum. "Home income plans" were the dominant form: a homeowner would take out a mortgage, invest the proceeds in an annuity, and use annuity income to cover the interest. The flaw was that interest rates and investment returns don't stay in alignment. When interest rates rose sharply in the late 1980s, many borrowers found their annuity income no longer covered their mortgage interest. Some ended up paying out of their own pockets. A few were at risk of repossession.

The other product causing serious harm was the fixed-repayment mortgage. Borrowers were told they owed a fixed amount at the end of the term, without anyone explaining clearly that compound interest could push what they actually owed far beyond that figure. By the time some people discovered the shortfall, they had little recourse.

There was no regulator. There was no ombudsman with clear jurisdiction. There was, in effect, no floor.

## How did the Equity Release Council come about?

In 1991, a group of providers and advisers formed SHIP: Safe Home Income Plans. It was a voluntary trade body, not a regulator, but the timing reflected genuine alarm within the industry about where things were heading. SHIP's founding standards required members to offer a no-negative-equity guarantee (meaning a borrower could never owe more than the property's sale value), a guarantee of tenure for life, and the right to move to a suitable alternative property and transfer the loan.

These were not trivial commitments. They cost providers real money, particularly the no-negative-equity guarantee, which meant absorbing any shortfall themselves. The fact that providers were willing to make these commitments voluntarily is worth noting: the reputational damage from early scandals was severe enough that responsible operators had a commercial incentive to differentiate themselves.

SHIP rebranded as the Equity Release Council in 2012, a name that better reflected its broader remit by then. Membership grew steadily, and today the Council's standards are so widely adopted that a non-member provider is, in practice, a red flag worth taking seriously.

## When did the FCA take over, and what did regulation actually change?

The Financial Services Authority (the FCA's predecessor) brought lifetime mortgages within its scope in October 2004, under the mortgage regulation regime introduced by the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. This was a significant moment. For the first time, selling a lifetime mortgage without FCA authorisation became a criminal offence. Advisers had to meet competency standards, firms had to hold adequate capital, and customers gained access to the Financial Ombudsman Service if things went wrong.

Home reversion plans, which involve selling a share of your home outright rather than borrowing against it, were treated as investments rather than mortgages, and so fell outside the 2004 regime. That anomaly was closed in April 2007, when the FSA brought home reversions within its regulatory perimeter. From that point, both main forms of equity release sat under a single framework.

The practical consequences went beyond having someone to complain to. Firms had to produce clear, fair and not misleading communications. Key Facts Illustrations (KFIs) became mandatory, giving borrowers a standardised document showing the total amount repayable, the impact of compound interest over time, and the effect on any means-tested benefits. Advisers had to make a clear record of their suitability assessment.

:::callout
**A note on "independent" advice**

FCA rules require that equity release advice be given on a "whole of market" basis, meaning the adviser must consider products from across all available providers rather than recommending from a restricted panel. This is a stronger standard than applies to ordinary mortgage advice. If you are speaking with an adviser who represents only one or two providers, they are not meeting this standard for equity release.
:::

## What protections does the Equity Release Council add on top of FCA rules?

FCA regulation sets a floor. The Equity Release Council's standards, which apply to its members, go further in some areas.

The no-negative-equity guarantee is the most significant. FCA rules do not in themselves require this: an FCA-authorised lender could, in theory, offer a lifetime mortgage without it. Equity Release Council membership does require it. This means that if your property sells for less than the outstanding loan when you die or move into care, the shortfall is absorbed by the lender, not passed to your estate.

The Council also requires that borrowers have the right to make voluntary penalty-free partial repayments of at least 10% of the original loan per year. This standard, introduced in 2022, was a response to the period of very low interest rates when many borrowers wanted to service some of the interest but were contractually prevented from doing so.

Additionally, the Council requires that solicitors independently confirm the borrower has understood the terms before completion. This is not simply a box-ticking exercise on the adviser's side: it is a separate legal check.

For a full picture of current consumer protections, the Equity Release Council publishes its product standards on its website, and Age UK's equity release guidance provides a useful plain-English summary.

## How has the market grown since regulation improved?

The numbers tell their own story. According to the Equity Release Council's market data, annual lending in 2006 was around £1.1 billion. By 2022, it had reached a record £6.2 billion, before falling back in 2023 as rising interest rates cooled demand. A market that had been avoided by mainstream financial services firms for decades gradually attracted household names: Aviva, Legal & General, and later Pure Retirement and More2Life all became significant players.

The involvement of large regulated firms changed things further. Aviva and Legal & General brought with them existing compliance cultures, actuarial disciplines, and reputational risk management. They also brought scale, which drove down some margins and supported product innovation, including the penalty-free partial repayment features that eventually became a Council standard.

This is not to paint everything since 2004 as unambiguously positive. Interest rates on lifetime mortgages remained stubbornly high relative to standard residential mortgages for many years, partly reflecting the no-negative-equity guarantee embedded in the product's pricing. And the sales process, even with its safeguards, is not immune to pressure: there have been FCA enforcement actions against individual firms and advisers since regulation began.

## What are the remaining gaps and criticisms?

Regulation has demonstrably improved. But it has not eliminated every concern.

Independent financial advisers working in retirement planning sometimes argue that the mandatory "whole of market" advice requirement, while sound in principle, can create a false sense of comprehensiveness. The equity release market has far fewer providers than the standard residential mortgage market, and in a period of rising rates, the range of competitive options can narrow significantly.

There is also a broader question about whether equity release is always considered alongside its alternatives. FCA suitability rules require an adviser to assess whether a product is suitable for the specific client, but critics note that the advice process focuses on equity release rather than on whether equity release is the right approach at all. Downsizing, benefit entitlement checks, or conventional borrowing may sometimes be more appropriate. A good adviser will raise these, but there is no regulatory requirement to conduct a formal comparison across all options before recommending equity release. If you are at the start of this process, our [equity release pillar guide](/equity-release/) covers the full range of alternatives worth considering.

Key Later Life Finance and similar equity release advisers are now part of larger financial services groups, which creates its own set of questions about independence. None of this makes the regulated market unsafe, but it is worth understanding the structure of whoever is advising you.

:::callout
**The compound interest point deserves its own warning**

A £100,000 lifetime mortgage at 6% interest, with no repayments, grows to around £321,000 in 20 years. At 7%, that figure is approximately £387,000. Regulation requires that your KFI shows you these projections, but the number can still come as a shock to people who have not engaged with compound interest before. Ask your adviser to walk through the figures with you in detail. This is their job, not an imposition.
:::

## What should a borrower look for today?

Given the history above, there are a few specific things worth checking before signing anything.

First: is the provider a member of the Equity Release Council? Membership is searchable on the Council's website. A non-member is not necessarily acting illegally, but they are not bound by the product standards described above.

Second: is your adviser FCA-authorised and operating on a whole-of-market basis? You can check any firm's authorisation status on the FCA Register at register.fca.org.uk.

Third: has a solicitor independently reviewed the terms and confirmed your understanding? This should happen as a matter of course with an Equity Release Council member, but ask explicitly.

Any regulated advice on whether equity release is appropriate for your specific circumstances must come from an FCA-authorised adviser. The history of this market is a clear illustration of what happens when that kind of oversight is absent.
