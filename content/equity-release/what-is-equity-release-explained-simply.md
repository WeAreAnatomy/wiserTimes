# What is equity release? A plain-English explanation

Equity release is a way for homeowners aged 55 or over to access money tied up in their property without having to sell it or move out. The most common form is a **lifetime mortgage**: you borrow against your home, the interest rolls up over time, and the loan is repaid when you die or move into long-term care. The second form, a **home reversion plan**, involves selling a share of your property to a provider at a discount, in exchange for a lump sum or regular income, while retaining the right to live there.

Neither product is a gift. Neither is "free money". Both carry significant long-term costs that deserve your full attention before you sign anything.

## How does a lifetime mortgage actually work?

You borrow a lump sum (or draw down smaller amounts over time) secured against your home. The lender charges interest, typically fixed for life. You don't make monthly repayments unless you choose to. Instead, the interest compounds, meaning it accrues on the original loan plus all the interest already added.

That compounding effect is the thing most people underestimate. A £100,000 loan at 6.5% fixed for life becomes roughly £360,000 after 20 years. Borrow £150,000 at the same rate and you're looking at around £522,000. The loan is repaid from the sale of your property, so there's no bill landing on your family's doorstep while you're alive. But the equity left in your estate will be substantially reduced.

Some lenders, including Aviva and Legal & General, offer plans with an **interest-serviced** option: you choose to pay some or all of the monthly interest, which keeps the balance stable. This suits people with enough regular income to service the interest but not enough liquid savings to cover a one-off expense.

:::callout
**The compound interest point matters more than almost anything else in this article.** Ask any adviser you speak to for an illustration showing the projected loan balance at 10, 15 and 20 years. It will clarify the long-term cost far better than any percentage figure on its own.
:::

## What is a home reversion plan?

A home reversion plan works quite differently. You sell a percentage of your property - say, 40% - to a provider in exchange for a cash lump sum or regular payments. You retain the right to live in the property rent-free until you die or enter care. When the property is eventually sold, the provider takes their agreed percentage of whatever the sale price is.

Because you are selling at a significant discount (providers typically pay 20-60% of the market value of the share they buy), home reversion is less popular than lifetime mortgages and less commonly offered. Providers such as Bridgewater Equity Release still offer them, but you will find the range is far narrower.

Home reversion tends to suit older applicants, roughly 70 or above, because the older you are, the higher the percentage of market value a provider will pay for that share. At 60, the discount is steep. At 80, it is much smaller.

## Who is equity release designed for?

There's no single "typical" borrower. In my experience, the people who find equity release genuinely useful tend to fall into one of two situations.

The first is someone who is asset-rich but income-poor: they own a home worth £300,000 or more but their pension income barely covers the bills. They're not looking to move, but a flooded kitchen, a car that's given up or a gap in social care funding has made liquid cash a priority.

The second is someone who wants to gift money to children or grandchildren now, while the tax and emotional value of doing so is highest, rather than leaving it all via probate.

Equity release is not designed for someone who has straightforward alternative options. If you have other savings, an unused pension allowance you haven't drawn, or a property you'd genuinely consider downsizing, those are almost certainly worth exploring first. An FCA-authorised financial adviser can help you compare those routes honestly.

## What protections do Equity Release Council standards provide?

The Equity Release Council is the industry body that sets product standards for its member providers. Its standards are not optional extras; they are the floor below which a responsible provider should not go. The four protections that matter most are:

- **No-negative-equity guarantee.** You will never owe more than your home is worth. If the loan balance exceeds the property sale price, the lender absorbs the difference.
- **Right to remain.** You cannot be asked to leave your home while you are alive, unless you move permanently into long-term care.
- **Right to move.** Your plan must be portable to a suitable alternative property if you choose to move.
- **Fixed or capped interest.** Interest rates must be fixed for life, or capped so they cannot exceed a stated ceiling.

Any plan carrying the Equity Release Council logo must meet all of these. Before you proceed with any provider, confirm their membership at [equityreleasecouncil.com](https://www.equityreleasecouncil.com). It takes thirty seconds and it matters.

## What does equity release cost, beyond the interest?

Interest is the big number, but it isn't the only one. Setting up equity release typically involves several layers of cost.

An arrangement fee to the lender, often £600 to £1,500, is common. You'll need a solicitor to act on your behalf (this is mandatory for Equity Release Council-approved plans); expect to pay £500 to £1,000 for conveyancing. A specialist equity release survey may be required on top of a standard valuation. And if you're using an equity release broker like Key Later Life Finance or a tied adviser, their advice fee can run to £1,500 or more, though many operate on commission paid by the lender.

Some providers, including Pure Retirement and More2Life, have streamlined fee structures, but "no arrangement fee" deals sometimes come with a slightly higher interest rate. Always ask for the full cost of credit illustration, not just the headline rate.

:::callout
You have the right, under FCA rules, to receive a Key Facts Illustration (KFI) before you commit to anything. The KFI sets out the total amount repayable, the projected loan balance over time, and all fees in a standard format. Read it carefully. Then read it again.
:::

## How is equity release different from a standard mortgage or a remortgage?

With a standard residential mortgage, you make monthly capital and interest repayments until the debt is cleared. With a remortgage, you replace one mortgage deal with another, again with monthly repayments, and lenders assess affordability based on your income.

Equity release lenders do not assess affordability in the same way. Because no monthly repayments are typically required, they assess the value of the property and your age, rather than your income. That is why it is accessible to people whose income alone wouldn't satisfy a high-street lender - but it also means the usual safeguards of income assessment don't apply.

A retirement interest-only mortgage (RIO) sits somewhere between the two. With a RIO, you pay monthly interest on the loan and the capital is repaid when the property is eventually sold. RIO products from providers like Nationwide or Hodge Bank require you to demonstrate you can afford the monthly interest payments. They are worth considering if you have a reliable income and want to prevent the balance from growing.

## What are the main reasons people decide against it?

Equity release is not the right answer for everyone, and I'd be doing you a disservice to suggest otherwise.

The most common reason people step back after getting advice is the impact on inheritance. If leaving as much as possible to children or grandchildren is a priority, a loan that compounds over 15 to 20 years works directly against that goal. It's worth having a frank conversation with your family before you proceed.

Means-tested benefits are another consideration. If you or your partner currently receive Pension Credit, Council Tax Reduction, or certain care-related benefits, releasing a lump sum could take your savings above the relevant threshold and reduce those entitlements. Our benefits guide covers this in more detail, and a specialist adviser should run through this with you before you commit.

Finally, some people explore equity release to cover costs that could be met in other ways. Before going further, it is worth checking whether you're claiming all the benefits you're entitled to (Age UK runs a free benefits check service), whether your home qualifies for a local authority disabled facilities grant, or whether downsizing to a smaller property might release similar equity without the long-term interest cost.

## Getting independent advice

Equity release is a regulated financial product, and you should only proceed on the basis of regulated advice from an FCA-authorised adviser. An adviser who is accredited by the Equity Release Council has also committed to specific professional standards around this product category.

The Money Helper service (part of the Money and Pensions Service, a government-backed body) offers impartial guidance at no cost and can help you understand whether equity release is even worth exploring further. You can reach them at [moneyhelper.org.uk](https://www.moneyhelper.org.uk).

Taking advice is not optional niceity. The FCA requires it for this product specifically, because the long-term financial implications are significant enough that a generic comparison site cannot substitute for a conversation about your individual circumstances.
