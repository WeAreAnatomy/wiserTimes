# How to talk to your family about equity release

The conversation itself is often harder than the product. Most people considering a lifetime mortgage spend weeks researching interest rates and loan-to-value ratios, then put off telling their children for months. That's understandable, but it tends to go badly. Family members who find out after the fact - or worse, after completion - frequently feel blindsided, and the resulting friction can outlast the financial transaction by years.

Transparency isn't just good manners here. It's practical. Your family may have information you don't: knowledge of your property's future potential, concerns about your care needs, or awareness of alternatives you haven't considered. Getting them involved early costs nothing and occasionally changes the outcome for the better.

## Why does this conversation feel so difficult?

Partly because money and inheritance are awkward subjects in most British families. Partly because raising equity release feels, to some people, like admitting a financial shortfall - something to be embarrassed about rather than a legitimate planning tool used by around 45,000 households a year (Equity Release Council, 2023 figures).

There's also the inheritance dimension. If you're releasing equity from a property worth £400,000, your children may have spent years quietly factoring that asset into their own financial futures. That's not greed; it's human. Acknowledging it openly tends to defuse it.

I'd suggest framing the conversation not as an announcement but as a discussion. You haven't necessarily decided anything yet. You're thinking through options and you want their input. That posture changes the dynamic considerably.

## What do family members typically worry about?

The concerns adult children raise tend to cluster around a handful of themes, and most of them are entirely legitimate.

**Compound interest.** This is the one that surprises people most. A £100,000 lifetime mortgage at 6% interest, with no monthly repayments, rolls up to roughly £320,000 over 20 years. Your children may not have seen that calculation before. Showing them the numbers - using the free calculator on the Money Helper website, or figures your adviser has modelled - turns an abstract worry into a specific one that can be discussed and planned around.

**What happens to the house if you need care.** If you move into a care home, the lifetime mortgage becomes repayable, usually within 12 months of you leaving the property permanently. The house would typically be sold. That concerns children who may have assumed you'd eventually live with them, or who are hoping to keep the family home in some form. It's worth explaining the no-negative-equity guarantee (a standard Equity Release Council requirement), which at least means the debt can never exceed the sale proceeds.

**Alternatives that haven't been explored.** Adult children sometimes raise options their parents haven't considered: downsizing, a family loan, letting a room under the Rent a Room scheme. Some of these will be impractical for your situation; some might not be. Your adviser is required to discuss alternatives before providing a recommendation, but hearing that reassurance from you directly can help.

**Whether you've been targeted or pressured.** Older readers are disproportionately targeted by financial scams, and a child who hears about equity release for the first time after the fact may jump to that conclusion. Showing them that you're working with a firm authorised by the Financial Conduct Authority and that the product meets Equity Release Council standards goes some way towards addressing this.

## How to approach the conversation itself

Pick a calm moment, not Christmas dinner or a rushed phone call. Face-to-face is better than a message or email, because the emotional register of a text conversation is too flat for this kind of topic.

Be specific about what you're considering and why. "I'm thinking about releasing some equity to cover the bathroom conversion and top up my income" is a more productive starting point than a vague mention of "looking at the house". Specificity invites a real response rather than a generalised panic.

Bring something concrete. A one-page summary of the product you're considering, or a printout from Key Later Life Finance or Age Partnership showing indicative rates and how compound interest works, gives the conversation a factual anchor. It also signals that you've done your homework.

Expect pushback. It doesn't mean they're wrong. If a child raises a concern you can't immediately answer, write it down and take it to your adviser. A good adviser will welcome the question.

:::callout
**A note on timing:** Most solicitors acting on equity release transactions will ask whether you've discussed the decision with family. Some lenders, including Aviva and Legal & General, actively encourage family involvement as part of their process. If you complete without telling anyone, that's your legal right - but the post-completion conversation tends to be harder.
:::

## Should family members come to the advice appointment?

This is worth asking your adviser about directly. The formal advice appointment is yours; it's based on your income, your property, your needs and your attitude to risk. A third party sitting in can occasionally complicate the dynamic, and some advisers prefer to see clients alone first.

That said, many advisers at firms like Key Later Life Finance or Age Partnership will offer a follow-up call or meeting that includes a family member, specifically to answer questions and explain the product. If your children have specific concerns, a short call between them and your adviser can resolve weeks of circular family debate in 20 minutes.

What family members almost never need to be present for is the initial eligibility check or the exploratory conversation. That's yours.

## What if a family member has lasting power of attorney?

If a family member holds a property and financial affairs LPA and you currently have full mental capacity, the LPA does not apply. You make this decision yourself. The LPA only becomes active if you lose the mental capacity to manage financial decisions.

Where it becomes relevant is if capacity is already a concern, in which case equity release lenders and solicitors will need to satisfy themselves that you have the capacity to enter the contract. This is an area where a specialist solicitor is essential. If you need guidance on LPA more broadly, Margaret's guides on wills and lasting power of attorney cover this in detail.

## How to handle a family that remains opposed

You may have a thorough conversation, address every concern, share the numbers, involve the adviser, and still have a child who thinks you're making a mistake. That happens.

Their opposition doesn't give them a veto. You are the property owner; the decision is yours under law. What I'd suggest, though, is taking one final step before you proceed: ask your adviser to document, in writing, that the alternatives were discussed and considered. Ask your solicitor to confirm that you received independent legal advice. Keep those documents somewhere your family can access them after the fact. It won't resolve the disagreement, but it does demonstrate that this was a considered decision, not an impulsive one.

If the relationship with a child is close enough that their sustained opposition genuinely troubles you, that's information worth sitting with before signing anything. Equity release can be undone, but only at a cost (early repayment charges on most lifetime mortgages are significant). Getting the decision right the first time matters.

## A word on inheritance protection

Some lifetime mortgage products include an inheritance protection feature, which ring-fences a percentage of your property's value to guarantee a minimum inheritance. More2Life and Pure Retirement both offer variants of this. It reduces the maximum amount you can borrow, but it can be a useful way of signalling to your family that their interests have been factored in. This won't suit everyone, and the trade-off between borrowing capacity and inheritance protection is something to model carefully with your adviser.

:::faq
- q: Do I have to tell my family I'm taking out equity release?
  a: Legally, no. But your solicitor will typically encourage it, and the Equity Release Council's standards require you to take independent legal advice before completion. Most advisers will strongly suggest involving family, because the decision affects their inheritance and can cause significant conflict if it comes as a surprise later.
- q: Can my children stop me from taking out equity release?
  a: No. Provided you are over 55 and own a qualifying property, equity release is your decision. Adult children have no legal power to veto it. What they can do is raise concerns, ask questions, and provide information you may not have considered.
- q: Should my children be at the equity release advice appointment?
  a: It's worth asking your adviser whether a family member can sit in, or whether a separate session can be arranged. Not all advisers offer this as standard, but many will accommodate it. The formal advice appointment is yours alone - a family session is usually informal and explanatory.
- q: What if my family disagrees with my decision?
  a: Their concerns deserve a fair hearing, but the final decision is yours. If they raise specific financial worries - about the interest compounding, the impact on means-tested benefits, or the suitability of alternatives - those are worth putting to your adviser directly before you proceed.
- q: Does equity release affect my children's inheritance tax position?
  a: Reducing your estate through equity release may reduce the inheritance tax liability on your estate, because the loan and rolled-up interest are deducted before IHT is calculated. Whether that matters in practice depends on your total estate value. A tax specialist or estate planner can model this for your specific situation.
:::
