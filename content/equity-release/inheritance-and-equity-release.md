# Equity release and your estate: what family members should know

Equity release reduces the inheritance your family will receive. That is the blunt reality, and it is worth stating at the start rather than the end. A lifetime mortgage compounds interest over years, sometimes decades, and the loan repaid on death or care entry can be considerably larger than the amount originally borrowed. What your estate receives, if anything, depends on how much equity remains in the property after that repayment.

That said, the picture is not simply "equity release wipes out your inheritance." Modern plans from Equity Release Council members come with legal protections that matter: a no-negative-equity guarantee, the right to stay in the property for life, and optional features that ringfence a share of the final sale value for your beneficiaries. Understanding how these work is useful for anyone whose parent or partner is considering a lifetime mortgage.

## How does the loan get repaid when someone dies or moves into care?

The full loan balance, plus all interest that has accumulated, becomes due when the last borrower on the plan either dies or moves permanently into long-term residential care. "Permanently" is the operative word; a temporary stay in hospital or a care setting does not trigger repayment.

Once the repayment event occurs, most lenders allow the estate 12 months to settle the debt. In practice, this almost always means selling the property. The solicitor handling the estate notifies the lender, a sale is arranged, and the lender is repaid from the proceeds. Whatever is left goes to the beneficiaries under the will.

Some lenders, including Aviva and Legal & General, allow beneficiaries to repay the loan using other assets rather than forcing a sale, which can matter if the family wants to keep the property. This is worth asking about before a plan is taken out, not after.

One detail families often miss: if there are two borrowers (a couple, for instance), the plan does not crystallise when the first person dies. It continues until the second borrower dies or moves into care. The surviving borrower keeps the right to live in the property, and the interest continues to accrue.

## What does compound interest actually do to the loan over time?

This is where many families get a shock. Lifetime mortgages charge compound interest, meaning interest accrues on the interest already added to the loan. Over a short period, the effect is modest. Over 15 or 20 years, it is dramatic.

A worked example: someone borrows £80,000 at a fixed rate of 6.2% with no monthly repayments. After 10 years, the outstanding balance is roughly £145,000. After 20 years, it is around £265,000. The original £80,000 has more than tripled, and all of that comes off the property sale proceeds before a single penny reaches the beneficiaries.

Rates have come down from the highs of 2022 and 2023. As of mid-2025, fixed rates on lifetime mortgages from providers like Pure Retirement and More2Life are broadly in the 5.5% to 7% range depending on loan-to-value and product features, though you should verify current rates with an FCA-authorised adviser.

The compound effect is why I would always suggest that families look at an illustration showing the projected loan balance at 10, 15 and 20 years, not just the headline amount borrowed. Lenders are required to provide these. They are sobering, and rightly so.

:::callout
**Ask to see the long-term projection.** Any Equity Release Council-compliant plan comes with a personalised illustration showing how the loan balance grows over time. If your parent is considering equity release, ask to see this document before any decision is made.
:::

## What is protected equity, and how does it work?

Protected equity (sometimes called an inheritance guarantee) lets the borrower ringfence a percentage of their property's eventual sale value for their estate. If you choose 25% protected equity, for example, at least 25% of whatever the property sells for will pass to your beneficiaries, regardless of the size of the outstanding loan.

The trade-off is that protecting equity reduces the maximum amount you can borrow. Providers calculate your loan ceiling based on your age and property value, and a protected equity feature effectively reduces that ceiling. The more you protect, the less you can release.

It is an optional feature, not a default. Families should not assume it is in place without checking. If an existing plan was taken out several years ago, it may or may not include this protection. The plan documents will confirm it.

Some providers, including Key Later Life Finance and Aviva, offer a range of protected equity percentages. Others offer a fixed option or none at all. This is a meaningful point of comparison when a plan is being chosen, not a footnote.

## Does the no-negative-equity guarantee fully protect the estate?

Yes, within its scope. The no-negative-equity guarantee is a core Equity Release Council product standard. It means that if the property sells for less than the outstanding loan, the lender writes off the difference. Your estate owes nothing beyond the net proceeds of the sale.

This matters because property values can fall. It also matters because a long-lived borrower, or one who borrowed relatively late in life at a higher interest rate, might accumulate a loan that eventually exceeds their property's value. Without this guarantee, the shortfall would fall to the estate. With it, it cannot.

The guarantee applies to plans from Equity Release Council members. Not every equity release product on the market carries Equity Release Council membership; some home reversion plans and older products may not. If you are looking at an existing plan or a new one, checking council membership is basic due diligence.

It is also worth noting that the guarantee does not apply if the borrower breaches the plan terms, for instance by subletting the property without permission or failing to maintain it adequately. These are not hypothetical concerns; they come up in practice.

## How does equity release interact with inheritance tax?

Equity release reduces the net value of your estate, because the outstanding loan is a debt secured against the property. That can reduce an inheritance tax (IHT) liability, since the taxable estate is valued after deducting liabilities.

In some cases, this is one of the reasons people release equity: to spend the money on gifts to children or grandchildren during their lifetime, bringing the estate below the IHT threshold. The current nil-rate band is £325,000 per person, with a residence nil-rate band of up to £175,000 available on top when passing a home to direct descendants (GOV.UK confirms current rates and thresholds).

The complication arises if you gift the released funds and then die within seven years. The gift could become a "potentially exempt transfer" for IHT purposes, meaning it may still be counted as part of your estate depending on how long you survive the gift. Families should not assume that releasing equity and gifting the proceeds is a clean IHT solution. It can be, but only with proper planning.

For this, I'd strongly suggest seeing a specialist. This genuinely sits at the intersection of equity release and estate planning, and getting it wrong is costly. The Society of Later Life Advisers (SOLLA) publishes a directory of accredited advisers if you are looking for somewhere to start.

:::callout
**IHT and equity release together are complex.** If inheritance tax is part of your thinking, please speak to an FCA-authorised financial adviser and a solicitor together, not separately. The interaction between the two areas is where people most often trip up.
:::

## What should families ask before a plan is signed?

If your parent or partner is considering equity release, or if you are helping them review a plan they have already taken out, these are the questions worth putting to the adviser or provider directly.

Is the plan from an Equity Release Council member? This confirms the no-negative-equity guarantee and the right to remain in the property for life.

Does the plan include protected equity? If so, what percentage, and does that match what the family actually needs?

What does the projected loan balance look like in 10 and 20 years? Ask to see this in writing.

Can the loan be repaid from other assets rather than a property sale? Some families have the means to do this and want the option.

Are voluntary repayments permitted? Many modern plans allow the borrower to make partial repayments without early repayment charges, up to a defined limit (often 10% of the outstanding balance per year). This can slow the compound effect significantly.

What happens if the borrower needs to move? The Equity Release Council's right-to-move standard means the borrower can transfer the plan to a new property if it meets the lender's criteria, but this is not unlimited. Understanding the restrictions before the plan is taken out avoids problems later.

For a broader overview of how lifetime mortgages work, and how they compare to home reversion plans, see our [guide to equity release](/equity-release/).

## What families often wish they had known earlier

In my experience, the conversations that go badly are not the ones where families disagreed about equity release on principle. They are the ones where the family was simply not involved until after the plan was in place, by which point the decisions were already made.

The practical effect of compound interest over a long period is not intuitive. It needs to be explained, and it needs to be shown in numbers, not described in abstract terms. A borrower who releases £60,000 at 70 and lives to 90 will have an outstanding loan that may well exceed the value of their home. That is not a failing of the product; it is an arithmetic consequence of a long-lived loan. But families need to understand it going in.

Protected equity features and voluntary repayment options exist precisely to manage this. They are not automatically included, and they come with trade-offs. The time to think about them is before signing, with the family involved in the conversation if the borrower is willing.

If you are reading this as an adult child trying to understand a plan your parent has already taken out, start by getting a copy of the original illustration document. That will show you the projected balance at different future dates and confirm whether any inheritance protection is in place. Most providers will send a current loan balance statement on request.
