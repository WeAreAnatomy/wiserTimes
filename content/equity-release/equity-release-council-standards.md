# What is the Equity Release Council and why does it matter?

The Equity Release Council is a UK trade body that sets mandatory product standards for lifetime mortgages and home reversion plans. If a lender, adviser or solicitor is a Council member, they have contractually agreed to follow those standards - which means you, as a customer, get specific legal protections built into your product from day one. The most well-known of these is the no-negative-equity guarantee: you can never owe more than your home is worth, no matter how long you live or how much the loan compounds.

That single guarantee changed the equity release market when it was introduced. Before it existed, people could - and did - end up with loans that outgrew their property value, leaving families to settle the shortfall. That scenario is now prohibited for any Council-member product.

## How did the Equity Release Council come about?

The Council traces its origins to SHIP (Safe Home Income Plans), a voluntary standards body founded in 1991 at a time when the equity release market had a genuinely poor reputation. A number of shared-appreciation mortgages and home-income plans from the 1980s had left older homeowners in serious difficulty. SHIP was a direct response: an attempt by the better-regarded firms to distinguish themselves and restore trust.

SHIP became the Equity Release Council in 2012, broadening its membership to include solicitors, financial advisers and surveyors alongside lenders. Today its membership covers most of the active UK market, though membership is voluntary rather than legally required.

That distinction is worth holding in mind. The FCA regulates equity release as a financial product - any firm advising on or selling a lifetime mortgage needs FCA authorisation. The Council sits alongside that, adding product-level protections that go beyond what the FCA mandates. Think of it as a second layer rather than a replacement.

## What standards do Council members have to meet?

The Council publishes a Statement of Principles that all members sign up to. The specific product standards for lifetime mortgages currently include four main commitments.

First, the no-negative-equity guarantee. The loan plus rolled-up interest can never exceed the eventual sale proceeds of the property, provided it is sold for a fair market value. Any shortfall is written off by the lender, not passed to your estate.

Second, the right to remain in your property for life (or until you move into long-term care). The lender cannot repossess simply because the loan has grown large relative to the property value. You have a legal right to stay.

Third, the right to move. If you want to downsize or relocate, you can transfer your lifetime mortgage to a new qualifying property. The lender can refuse only if the new property is unsuitable as security, not simply as a way of forcing early repayment.

Fourth, fixed or capped interest rates for life. Variable-rate lifetime mortgages are not permitted under Council standards. Your interest rate must be fixed at outset, or have a cap that it cannot exceed. This matters enormously for long-term planning: compound interest on a variable rate would be extraordinarily difficult to model or predict.

:::callout
**The interest compounding point deserves real attention.** A £100,000 lifetime mortgage at 6% fixed compounds to around £180,000 after ten years and roughly £321,000 after twenty, assuming no repayments. The no-negative-equity guarantee protects your estate from a shortfall, but the loan can still consume most or all of your property's value if you live a long time. Get a personalised illustration from an FCA-authorised adviser before making any decision.
:::

## Why is independent legal advice a requirement?

Council standards require that you receive independent legal advice from a solicitor before completing any equity release product. Specifically, the solicitor must not be the same firm acting for the lender.

This is not a formality. The solicitor's job is to confirm you understand what you are signing, that you have not been pressured, and that the terms of the contract are as you were told they would be. They will go through the key terms with you individually - the interest rate, the compounding mechanics, the conditions under which early repayment charges apply, and what happens when the plan ends.

In my experience, clients sometimes feel impatient at this stage. They have already met an adviser, received an illustration and decided they want to proceed. The solicitor can feel like a delay. But this is the moment where a good solicitor occasionally identifies something worth pausing on: a fixed early repayment charge window that runs longer than expected, or a condition the borrower had not fully registered.

The cost is usually absorbed into the completion fees you pay, though it varies by solicitor. Ask upfront.

## Does using a Council member actually protect me?

It does, within limits. The protections are real and contractually binding, not just aspirational. The no-negative-equity guarantee, in particular, has been tested: estates have been protected from shortfalls when property values fell or borrowers lived longer than anticipated.

But Council membership is not a substitute for careful consideration of whether equity release suits your circumstances at all. A lender meeting every Council standard can still sell you a product that turns out to be the wrong solution for your situation. The Council's standards govern how the product behaves; they do not assess whether you should take it.

That assessment is what an FCA-authorised adviser is for. The Council actually requires its adviser members to hold appropriate FCA permissions and to provide advice, not just information. If an equity release firm is Council-registered but its adviser is not individually FCA-authorised, that is worth questioning.

You can cross-reference adviser status on the FCA Register at register.fca.org.uk.

## How does this apply to home reversion plans?

Home reversion plans work differently from lifetime mortgages. Instead of taking a loan against your property, you sell a percentage of it to the provider in exchange for a lump sum or income, while retaining the right to live there rent-free for life. Because there is no debt involved, the no-negative-equity guarantee does not apply (there is nothing to go negative).

The Council has separate standards for reversion plans, covering the right to remain, clear written terms, and independent legal advice. The key risks with reversion plans differ: you are selling at a discount to current market value (typically 20-60% below, depending on your age and whether you take a lump sum or income), so the provider shares in any future property price growth on the proportion you sold.

Reversion plans represent a much smaller slice of the market than lifetime mortgages. Providers currently active in this space include Bridgewater Equity Release, though the market has contracted over the past decade.

## What should you ask before taking out any equity release product?

Before you proceed with any provider, Council member or not, I would suggest working through a short list of practical questions with your adviser.

What is the total amount I could owe after ten, fifteen and twenty years at the quoted interest rate? Ask for actual figures, not just a percentage.

Are there early repayment charges, and if so, how are they calculated? Some are fixed percentages; others are gilt-linked and can be substantial if gilt yields have moved significantly since you took out the product.

What happens to the plan if I need to move into care? This affects both your right-to-remain and how quickly the property must be sold.

Does this product have the Equity Release Council's standards built into the contract? Ask to see the relevant clause if you are unsure.

Your adviser should be able to answer all of these without hesitation. If they cannot, or if they steer you away from the questions, that tells you something useful.

:::callout
**Checking Council membership takes about thirty seconds.** Go to equityreleasecouncil.com and use the member search. It covers lenders, advisers and solicitors. Major lenders including Aviva, Legal & General, More2Life and Pure Retirement are currently listed. Key Later Life Finance, one of the larger advisory firms, is also a member. If a firm you are considering is not listed, ask them directly why not.
:::

## Where does this fit in the wider equity release picture?

The Equity Release Council's standards are one piece of a broader framework. FCA regulation, independent legal advice, proper financial advice and your own due diligence all play a part.

If you are still getting to grips with how equity release works at a basic level, the [equity release pillar page](/equity-release/) covers the different product types, how interest compounds, and the circumstances in which it tends to suit (or not suit) older homeowners.

Age UK also publishes a free factsheet on equity release (factsheet 65) that is useful background reading before you speak to any commercial firm. It does not sell anything and has no product relationships to manage.

One last thought. The existence of the Equity Release Council, and its standards, is a genuine improvement on the market of thirty years ago. The protections are meaningful. But the fact that protections exist does not mean the product is right for your situation. Take proper advice from an FCA-authorised adviser before making any decision.
