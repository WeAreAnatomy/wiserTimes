# What happens to an equity release plan when the homeowner dies?

When someone with a lifetime mortgage dies, the clock starts. Their estate typically has 12 months to repay the outstanding loan, and in most cases that means selling the property. The process is more straightforward than many families fear, but there are real decisions to make under time pressure, often while grieving. Knowing what to expect before that moment arrives makes everything easier.

This article covers how repayment works, what lenders expect from the family, how joint plans differ, and a few things that catch estates off guard.

---

## What triggers repayment of an equity release plan?

A lifetime mortgage becomes repayable on two events: the death of the borrower, or the permanent move into long-term residential care. On a single-person plan, death triggers repayment immediately. On a joint plan, repayment is deferred until the second named borrower has either died or moved permanently into care.

Most plans are lifetime mortgages regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority. If the plan was taken out through a lender who is a member of the Equity Release Council (and the vast majority of plans since the mid-1990s are), it will carry the Council's product standards, including the no-negative-equity guarantee and the right to remain in the property for life. Those guarantees matter at the repayment stage.

Home reversion plans work differently: the homeowner sold a share of the property to the provider when they took the plan out, so on death that share belongs to the provider already. The remaining share passes to the estate. Repayment in the traditional sense doesn't arise. Home reversions are now rare, but it's worth checking which type of plan you're dealing with before assuming anything.

---

## How does the family find out what's owed?

The lender should be notified of the death as soon as is practically possible. You don't need to do this before registering the death or applying for probate, but it's sensible to contact the lender early so that interest doesn't continue accruing unnecessarily, and so you can get a current redemption figure.

To notify the lender, you'll typically need a copy of the death certificate and, once it's been granted, a copy of the grant of probate (or grant of letters of administration if the person died without a will). The lender will then write to the executor or administrator with a formal redemption statement setting out the outstanding balance.

If you can't immediately locate the plan documents, the Land Registry title register will show whether a mortgage or legal charge is registered against the property. That gives you the lender's name. From there, a call to the lender's bereavement team is the starting point. Aviva, Legal & General, More2Life and other providers all have dedicated bereavement teams and are used to dealing with families in this situation.

:::callout
**Finding the plan documents:** Equity release lenders are required to send annual statements. These should be somewhere in the deceased's paperwork, and they'll show the current balance and the lender's contact details. If you're handling the estate and can't find them, the solicitor dealing with probate can request a search of the Land Registry.
:::

---

## How long does the family have to repay?

The standard repayment window is 12 months from the date of death. Some lenders start the clock from the date probate is granted rather than the date of death itself, which can make a difference if probate takes several months to come through.

During the repayment period, most Equity Release Council member lenders agree to a period where they will not charge additional interest, or will pause interest accrual, while the estate is being wound up and the property sold. The exact terms vary by lender and plan, so it's important to confirm this in writing with the lender early in the process.

If the 12-month deadline is at risk of being missed, contact the lender. In genuine cases of delay (a contested estate, a slow conveyancing chain, a property that has proved difficult to sell), most lenders will negotiate a short extension rather than take possession action. They are not in the business of forcing rushed sales; they want full repayment, and a slightly longer timeline usually achieves that more reliably.

---

## What actually happens to the property?

In practice, the property is usually sold, the loan is repaid from the sale proceeds, and whatever remains passes to the beneficiaries of the estate.

The executor manages the sale, typically instructing an estate agent and a conveyancing solicitor in the usual way. The lender holds a first legal charge over the property, which means they are repaid first from the sale proceeds, ahead of any other creditors and ahead of the beneficiaries.

If the property sells for more than the outstanding loan (which includes all the compounded interest), the surplus goes to the estate. If it sells for less, the no-negative-equity guarantee means the lender absorbs the shortfall. The estate cannot be pursued for the difference. This protection applies to all plans issued by Equity Release Council members.

One thing families sometimes underestimate is how much the loan may have grown. A £150,000 lifetime mortgage taken out at 6.5% and never partially repaid compounds to around £522,000 over 20 years. If the property has also risen in value over those 20 years, there may still be meaningful equity remaining. But if the property has been static in value while the loan has grown, the residual equity could be very small. The annual statements the lender sends should give a clear picture of where things stand.

The estate does not have to sell. If a beneficiary wants to keep the property, they can raise a mortgage (or use other funds) to buy it from the estate at market value, with the lender being repaid from the proceeds. This is less common, but it happens.

---

## What changes with a joint equity release plan?

On a joint lifetime mortgage, both borrowers are named on the plan. When the first borrower dies, the surviving borrower simply carries on living in the property. Nothing becomes repayable at that point. The plan continues exactly as before, with interest still accruing on the outstanding balance.

Repayment only becomes due when the surviving borrower either dies or moves permanently into long-term residential care. At that point the same 12-month process described above begins.

This is one of the practical reasons couples are generally advised to take out joint plans rather than single plans, even if only one partner's income or property ownership is driving the application. A single plan on one partner's life means the surviving partner could face losing their home if the property has to be sold to repay the loan after the first death. Joint plans carry this protection as a matter of course. If you're reviewing an older plan and aren't certain whether it's joint or single, the plan documents or lender will confirm.

:::callout
**Already a single-person plan?** If an older plan was taken out in one name only and both partners live in the property, check the plan terms urgently. The Equity Release Council's current standards require that all residents are named on the plan, but older plans may not have included a non-borrowing spouse. A solicitor or FCA-authorised adviser can help you understand the options.
:::

---

## Can the estate repay early to reduce costs?

Yes, and in some circumstances it makes sense to do so. If probate comes through quickly and the estate has other liquid assets, repaying the loan before the property sells avoids further interest accruing and may simplify the conveyancing.

Most lifetime mortgages include early repayment charges if repaid within a fixed term. On death, however, the majority of Equity Release Council member lenders waive early repayment charges entirely. Check the specific terms of the plan, but this is a standard feature of most modern plans.

More2Life, Pure Retirement and Key Later Life Finance all publish their bereavement procedures, and their plan documents will specify the early repayment charge position on death. If you're unsure, the lender's bereavement team will confirm it in writing.

---

## What should families do right now, before a death occurs?

If you have a parent or partner who holds a lifetime mortgage, there are a few things worth doing while there's no urgency.

Locate the plan documents and annual statements. Know which lender holds the plan and what the approximate balance is. Make sure the executor named in the will knows the plan exists and roughly what it involves. If there isn't a will, get one made: administering an estate with equity release under the rules of intestacy adds unnecessary complication.

It's also worth having a candid look at the latest annual statement together. The balance will be higher than the original loan, and it will keep growing. Understanding that now avoids a shock for beneficiaries later.

For anyone in the process of taking out a plan now, or reviewing an existing one, our [guide to how lifetime mortgages work](/equity-release/how-does-equity-release-work/) covers the compounding mechanics in more detail.

Any substantive decisions about whether to repay early, whether to keep the property in the estate, or how equity release interacts with inheritance tax planning, need regulated advice from an FCA-authorised adviser. An estate planning solicitor is also worth consulting if the estate is complex.

:::faq
### Frequently asked questions

**How long does the family have to repay an equity release plan after death?**
Most lenders allow 12 months from the date of death, though some set the clock from the date probate is granted. Check the specific terms in the plan documents. Lenders will usually pause or waive interest accrual during an agreed repayment period, but this varies.

**Does the family have to sell the house to repay equity release?**
Not necessarily. The estate can repay the loan using other assets, or a family member can remortgage to buy the property. In practice, most families sell, because the sums involved are large. The no-negative-equity guarantee means the estate will never owe more than the sale proceeds.

**What happens if there is money left over after repaying the equity release loan?**
Any remaining equity belongs to the estate and passes to the beneficiaries under the will, or under the rules of intestacy if there is no will. This is why understanding how compound interest erodes equity over time matters when the plan is first taken out.

**Does equity release affect inheritance tax?**
The outstanding loan reduces the value of the estate, which can reduce the inheritance tax liability. However, the interaction with IHT planning is complex, and you should speak to a specialist estate planning solicitor or an FCA-authorised adviser before drawing any conclusions.

**What if the surviving partner wants to stay in the property?**
On a joint lifetime mortgage, the surviving borrower simply continues living in the property under the existing plan. The loan only becomes repayable when the second borrower dies or moves permanently into long-term care. This is one of the key reasons couples take out joint rather than single plans.
:::
