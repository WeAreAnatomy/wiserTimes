# How to get the most out of a smart TV

This guide is for anyone who has a smart TV sitting in the corner and isn't quite sure what it can do, or for family members helping a parent get started. By the end, you'll be able to connect to your home broadband, watch BBC iPlayer, Netflix and YouTube, and adjust the picture, subtitles and audio settings to suit you. No technical background needed.

A quick note on what "smart" means here: a smart TV is simply a television with built-in internet access and a set of apps, much like a smartphone but on a bigger screen. You don't need a separate box for most streaming services. The TV handles it all.

---

## Step 1: Connect your TV to Wi-Fi

Before any of the apps will work, the TV needs to be connected to your home broadband. This is a one-time setup that takes about five minutes.

Take your remote and look for a button labelled **Settings**, **Menu** or a small cog icon. Press it. On screen you'll see a list of options. Look for **Network** or **Wi-Fi** (Samsung TVs often call this "Network"; LG TVs use "Wi-Fi Connection"; Sony TVs label it "Network & Internet"). Select it and the TV will search for nearby Wi-Fi networks.

Find your home network's name in the list. If you're not sure what it's called, check the label on the back of your broadband router (the box your internet provider gave you). Select your network, then type in the password, also printed on the router label. TV remotes can be slow for typing, so take your time. Press OK or Connect when done.

The TV will confirm the connection, usually with a green tick or the words "Connected".

:::callout{type="warning"}
**Wrong password?** Wi-Fi passwords are case-sensitive. "Orange5" and "orange5" are different passwords. If the connection fails, try again carefully, checking for capital letters and numbers that look like letters (0 and O, 1 and l).
:::

---

## Step 2: Set up BBC iPlayer

BBC iPlayer is free, requires no subscription, and gives you live BBC channels plus years' worth of programmes on demand.

From your TV's home screen, look for an icon labelled **BBC iPlayer**. On most modern smart TVs (Samsung, LG, Sony, Hisense), it's pre-installed. If you don't see it, look for an **Apps** section on the home screen and search for "BBC iPlayer" there.

Open the app. It will ask you to sign in with a BBC account. If you haven't got one, you can create one free at bbc.co.uk/register on a phone or computer. You'll need an email address and a password you choose yourself. Once you have those details, select **Sign In** in the app and enter them using the remote.

You'll also be asked to confirm you have a valid TV licence. Simply select **Yes** if you do.

After signing in, you can browse programmes by category, search for something specific, or go straight to a live channel. The top of the screen usually has a row labelled **Channels** for live viewing.

---

## Step 3: Set up Netflix

Netflix requires a paid subscription, starting from around £4.99 a month as of early 2025 (the standard tier costs more and removes adverts). If someone in your household already has an account, you can use their login details.

Open the **Netflix** app from your TV's home screen. Select **Sign In**. Enter the email address and password linked to the Netflix account. The TV remote keyboard can be fiddly, so go slowly.

Once signed in, you'll see a row of profile icons. Each person in the household can have their own. Select yours (or set one up if you're new).

Netflix will remember you after this. The next time you open the app, it goes straight to your profile.

:::callout{type="warning"}
**Forgotten your Netflix password?** Don't try to guess it through the TV. Go to netflix.com on a phone or computer, click "Forgot password?" and reset it by email first. Then sign in through the TV with the new password.
:::

---

## Step 4: Set up YouTube

YouTube is free and carries an enormous range of content: cookery, gardening, documentaries, music, old TV clips, exercise classes for older adults and much more. You don't need to create an account to watch, which makes it the easiest of the three to start with.

Find the **YouTube** app on your home screen and open it. You'll be asked whether you want to sign in or continue as a guest. For a first go, selecting **Use YouTube without signing in** is perfectly fine. You can browse and search for anything you like.

If you do want to sign in (it means YouTube remembers what you've watched and suggests similar things), you'll be given a short code on screen. Go to youtube.com/activate on your phone or computer, type the code in, and the TV will log in automatically. No typing your password through the remote.

---

## Step 5: Turn on subtitles

Subtitles make an enormous difference, especially in rooms with background noise or for anyone with any degree of hearing loss.

**On BBC iPlayer:** While a programme is playing, press OK or the centre button on your remote to bring up the playback controls. Look for a speech bubble icon or a button labelled **AD/Subtitles**. Select **Subtitles: On**.

**On Netflix:** Press the down arrow on your remote while something is playing. A menu appears at the bottom of the screen. Select the speech bubble icon (Audio & Subtitles), then choose **English** under the Subtitles column.

**On YouTube:** Press down while a video is playing to see the controls. Select the three-dot menu or the **CC** button (Closed Captions) and switch subtitles on.

On some TVs, you can also set subtitles on by default for all content. Look under **Settings > Accessibility > Subtitles** and set them to always on. This saves doing it manually each time.

---

## Step 6: Turn on audio description

Audio description (AD) adds a narrator who describes on-screen action, so you can follow what's happening without relying entirely on visuals. It's particularly useful for anyone with sight loss.

**On BBC iPlayer:** The same AD/Subtitles button mentioned in Step 5 also controls audio description. Select **Audio Description: On**.

**On Netflix:** Through the Audio & Subtitles menu (the speech bubble icon), look for an audio track labelled **English - Audio Description** and select it.

**On your TV generally:** Many TVs also have a system-wide audio description setting for broadcast TV. On Samsung, go to **Settings > General > Accessibility > Audio Description**. On LG, it's **Settings > Accessibility > Audio Description**. Turn the toggle on.

Not all programmes carry an audio description track; those that do will usually show a small "AD" badge in the programme listing.

---

## Step 7: Adjust text size and picture settings

The default text on many smart TVs is surprisingly small. A few minutes adjusting the accessibility settings can make a real difference.

For **text size**, go to **Settings > Accessibility** on your TV. Samsung, LG and Sony all include a text scaling or "Zoom" option here that enlarges menus and on-screen text. It won't affect the picture itself, just the TV's interface.

For **picture brightness and contrast**, particularly helpful if the room is bright or if the picture looks harsh, go to **Settings > Picture**. Look for a mode called **Movie** or **Cinema**; in my experience, it tends to be gentler on the eyes than the default "Standard" or "Vivid" mode, which is often set very bright in showrooms.

If you use a hearing loop or other assistive audio device, also check **Settings > Accessibility > Hearing Accessibility** (Samsung) or **Settings > Accessibility > Menu Audio** (LG) for options to boost menu audio or mono sound.

:::callout{type="warning"}
**Remote feeling complicated?** Several companies make simplified remotes designed for older users. The Flipper Big Button Remote (around £25) works with most UK smart TVs and cuts the number of buttons right down. It's worth considering if the original remote is causing frustration.
:::

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need a smart TV to watch iPlayer or Netflix?**
Not necessarily. If you have an older TV with an HDMI socket, you can plug in a small streaming stick instead. Amazon Fire TV Stick (from around £29.99) and Google Chromecast (from around £35) both add iPlayer, Netflix and YouTube to any TV. A smart TV is simply more convenient because those apps are already built in.

**My smart TV says "No network found" even though my Wi-Fi is working. What do I do?**
First, try switching the TV off at the wall for 30 seconds, then on again. Also restart your broadband router the same way. If the problem persists, the TV may be too far from the router; a Wi-Fi extender (sold by TP-Link, BT and others) placed between the two usually sorts it.

**Is it safe to enter my card details into Netflix on the TV?**
Yes, provided you're signing into Netflix directly through the app. Your payment details are held by Netflix, not stored on the TV itself. Avoid clicking links in any emails that claim to be from Netflix, though; always go through the app on the TV, not a link.

**Can I control the TV with my voice instead of the remote?**
Most modern Samsung, LG and Sony smart TVs have a built-in microphone in the remote. Hold the microphone button (it looks like a small mic) and say what you want: "Open BBC iPlayer" or "Turn on subtitles". It works reasonably well for basic commands and can be much easier than hunting through menus.

**The picture keeps freezing or buffering. How do I fix it?**
Buffering usually means the internet connection is slow or intermittent. If possible, connect the TV to the router using an Ethernet cable (a physical internet cable plugged into the back of both devices). This is more reliable than Wi-Fi, especially in larger homes. If cabling isn't practical, a powerline adapter kit (Devolo and TP-Link both make good ones, from about £40 for a pair) carries the internet signal through your home's existing electrical wiring.
:::
