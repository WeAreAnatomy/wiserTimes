# How to spot and deal with phishing and scam emails

Phishing emails are fake messages designed to trick you into clicking a link, entering a password, or transferring money. They nearly always pretend to be from someone you trust: HMRC, your bank, Royal Mail, even the NHS. The good news is that once you know what to look for, most of them are fairly easy to spot. This guide walks you through the warning signs, what to do if you've already clicked something, and how to report it.

## What does a phishing email actually look like?

There is no single template. Fraudsters constantly update their tactics, and some fakes are genuinely convincing.

That said, the most common phishing emails in the UK follow a small number of scripts. You owe a customs fee on a parcel. HMRC is threatening legal action over unpaid tax. Your bank account has been locked. A streaming service needs you to update your payment details or your subscription will be cancelled.

The emotional hook is usually urgency, fear, or a threat of financial loss. The goal is to make you act quickly, without stopping to think.

## Six warning signs worth knowing

**The sender's address looks slightly wrong.** The name displayed might say "HMRC" or "Lloyds Bank", but if you click or hover on the sender's name, the actual email address is something like hmrc-refund@gov-uk-returns.com. Legitimate government emails in the UK come from addresses ending in .gov.uk. Your bank will write from its official domain, not a random string of words.

**The greeting is vague.** "Dear Customer" or "Dear Account Holder" suggests the sender doesn't actually know who you are. Your bank knows your name and will use it.

**There's a link that doesn't match the supposed sender.** Before you click any link, hover your mouse over it (or on a touchscreen, press and hold). The real destination address will appear. If the email claims to be from Royal Mail but the link points to something like royalmail-delivery-fee.info, don't click it.

**The email creates a tight deadline.** "You have 24 hours to respond or your account will be closed" is a pressure tactic. Legitimate organisations do sometimes impose deadlines, but they don't typically threaten you with immediate account closure over email.

**Attachments you weren't expecting.** A genuine parcel delivery notification won't ask you to open a Word document or a ZIP file. Unexpected attachments should be treated with suspicion, even when the email looks official.

**Something feels slightly off about the writing.** Odd punctuation, inconsistent fonts, or sentences that sound a little stilted can be a giveaway. Scammers often operate from overseas and their copy isn't always polished. More sophisticated versions are better written, so don't rely on spelling mistakes alone.

:::callout
**A quick check that takes ten seconds:** If an email asks you to log in or pay something, don't use the link in the email. Open a new browser tab, type the organisation's real website address yourself, and log in that way. If there's a genuine problem with your account, you'll see it there.
:::

## The tricks that catch people out

One of the most effective phishing tactics I've seen reported is what's sometimes called "spear phishing": a message that uses your name, mentions a recent transaction, or references something specific to make it feel genuine. This information is often gathered from data breaches or social media.

Another common one is the "friend in trouble" scam. You get an email that appears to be from someone you know, saying they're stranded abroad and need money urgently. If the email account of someone you know has been hacked, the message will come from their actual address. Ring them directly before doing anything else.

Then there are fake invoice emails sent to businesses, or to people who run small side businesses. These look like legitimate invoices from suppliers. The account details have simply been swapped for the fraudster's own.

## What to do if you clicked a link

Don't panic. Clicking a link doesn't automatically mean your device is compromised or that money has left your account.

The first thing to do: close the page immediately if it asked you for any information and you didn't enter anything. You're likely fine.

If you did enter information, the steps depend on what you gave away.

If you typed a password, change it right now on the real website. Change it on any other site where you use the same password. (Yes, I know you know you shouldn't reuse passwords. Most of us do anyway.) If the account has two-factor authentication, turn it on.

If you entered any bank details or card numbers, call your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card. Not the number in the email. Banks have specialist fraud teams available around the clock, and they can put a block on your card before any money moves. UK Finance, the banking industry body, reports that over half of all fraud in the UK involves some form of impersonation, so fraud teams are well-practised at handling this.

If you downloaded a file, run a security scan. Windows computers include Windows Defender, which is free and does a reasonable job. On a Mac, Malwarebytes offers a free version that's worth running. If you're unsure, a local computer repair shop can check for you.

:::callout
**Worried about your device?** Age UK's telephone helpline (0800 678 1602) can point you towards local digital support. Some libraries also offer free one-to-one tech help sessions.
:::

## How to report a phishing email in the UK

Reporting matters. Every report helps the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) identify and take down scam websites, sometimes within hours.

**Forward the email to report@phishing.gov.uk.** This is the NCSC's Suspicious Email Reporting Service. You don't need to write anything in the email; just forward it. The service has taken down hundreds of thousands of scam sites since it launched.

**Report to Action Fraud** if you've lost money or given away personal details. You can do this online at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. Action Fraud is the UK's national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime, run by the City of London Police. Reporting doesn't guarantee a police investigation, but the data is used to track fraud trends and support prosecutions.

If the email pretended to be from HMRC specifically, you can also forward it to phishing@hmrc.gov.uk. HMRC takes impersonation seriously and actively pursues cases.

Don't feel embarrassed about reporting. The Office for National Statistics' Crime Survey for England and Wales consistently shows that fraud is the most commonly experienced crime in the country. You are far from alone.

## How to make yourself a harder target going forward

A few habits make a real difference.

Use a different password for each important account. A password manager such as Bitwarden (free) or 1Password makes this practical rather than a memory exercise. You only need to remember one master password, and the manager handles the rest.

Turn on two-factor authentication wherever it's offered, especially for email and banking. This means that even if someone steals your password, they still can't log in without a second code sent to your phone.

Keep your devices updated. Software updates often include security patches for vulnerabilities that fraudsters actively exploit. It's worth letting them install rather than clicking "remind me later" indefinitely.

Be careful what you share publicly on Facebook and other social media. Scammers do look at profiles. Dates of birth, home towns, family members' names: all of this can be used to make a fake email feel more personal and plausible.

You might also find it worth reading our [guide to staying safe online](/tech-guides/staying-safe-online/), which covers broader topics including safe browsing and password basics.

## What if the email is about something genuinely worrying?

Sometimes a phishing email lands in your inbox at the same time as a real problem: you were expecting a parcel, you do have an HMRC query, your bank account did have unusual activity. The coincidence makes it harder to judge.

The rule stays the same regardless: don't use the links or phone numbers in the email. Look up the organisation's contact details independently, via a search engine or by checking the back of your card or a previous statement, and make contact that way. If the issue is real, they'll be able to tell you. If the email was a scam, you've lost nothing.

In my experience, taking 60 seconds to do this one check prevents the vast majority of phishing attacks from succeeding. Fraudsters rely on speed and panic. Slowing down removes their main advantage.

  - q: Can a phishing email infect my device just by opening it?
