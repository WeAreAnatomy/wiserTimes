# Staying safe online: a practical guide for beginners

The internet is genuinely useful - for staying in touch with family, managing money, booking appointments, even watching the grandchildren open birthday presents over video call. But it does carry risks, and those risks tend to target people who are newer to it. The good news is that a handful of straightforward habits will protect you against the vast majority of online threats. You don't need to be technical. You just need to know what to look for.

---

## Why online scams target people over 55

Age UK estimates that fraud costs UK adults over 65 more than £1 billion a year, and much of it happens online. That figure isn't a reflection of naivety. It's partly because older people often hold more savings, and partly because scammers have become extremely sophisticated. The emails and text messages they send can look identical to genuine ones from HMRC, your bank, or Royal Mail.

Knowing the tactics they use is half the battle.

---

## How to spot a suspicious email or text

The single most useful question you can ask when an email or text message arrives is: did I expect this? If HMRC contacts you unexpectedly about a tax rebate, that's almost certainly a scam. If Royal Mail says there's a parcel on hold and asks for a £2.99 fee, same thing.

Beyond that, look for these warning signs:

- A sender's email address that doesn't match the organisation. A genuine email from Lloyds Bank will come from a @lloydsbank.com address, not something like lloyd5-bank@fastmail.net.
- Urgent language. "Your account will be suspended within 24 hours" is a classic pressure tactic designed to stop you thinking clearly.
- A link that doesn't go where it claims. On a computer, hover your mouse over any link before clicking - the actual destination appears at the bottom of the screen. On a phone, press and hold the link briefly to see the URL.
- Poor spelling and grammar, though I'd caution against relying on this alone. AI tools have made scam emails far more polished in recent years.

If you receive a suspicious email, don't click anything. Forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk, which is the National Cyber Security Centre's reporting service. For suspicious texts, forward to 7726 (that spells SPAM on most keypads) - it's free on all major UK networks.

:::callout
**Received something that looked official but felt wrong?** You can report it to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. They're the UK's national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime. Reporting takes about ten minutes and genuinely helps police identify patterns.
:::

## How to create passwords that actually work

Most people use the same password for multiple sites, and it's usually something memorable: a pet's name, a birth year, an address. Criminals know this. When one website gets hacked and passwords leak, the first thing fraudsters do is try those same passwords on banking apps and email accounts.

The fix is straightforward. Use a different password for every site, and make each one hard to guess.

A method that works well for many people is to pick three unconnected words and string them together: "tulip+garage+Tuesday8" is far harder to crack than "Margaret1952", even though the second one feels more complicated. The length is what makes the difference.

If keeping track of dozens of different passwords sounds impossible, a password manager does it for you. **Bitwarden** is free and well-regarded. **1Password** costs a few pounds a month but has a particularly clear interface that many older users find easier. Both work on phones, tablets and computers. You create one strong master password to access the manager, and it fills in all the others automatically.

For your most important accounts - email, banking, HMRC's Government Gateway - turn on two-factor authentication as well. Your bank almost certainly already requires it under Open Banking regulations, but email accounts often don't have it switched on by default. Go into your account's security settings and look for "two-step verification" or "2FA".

## Is your Wi-Fi connection safe?

Your home broadband is generally safe, provided you've changed the default password on your router. That default password is usually printed on a sticker on the router itself, which means anyone who visits your home could see it. Change it to something unique via the router's settings page (your broadband provider's helpline can walk you through this if needed).

Public Wi-Fi is a different matter. The network at a coffee shop or library is shared with everyone else in the building, which makes it easier for someone technically minded to intercept data passing across it. For general browsing, reading the news, or looking something up, the risk is low. For anything involving a password, a bank account, or personal details, I'd suggest waiting until you're home.

One exception: if you use your mobile phone as a hotspot rather than connecting to the café's Wi-Fi, you're using your phone network instead, which is considerably more private.

## Why software updates matter more than most people realise

"Update available" notifications are easy to dismiss, and most of us do. That's understandable - they tend to arrive at inconvenient moments, take time to install, and seem to change things you'd got used to.

But security updates in particular patch specific holes that criminals are actively trying to exploit. The WannaCry ransomware attack in 2017, which knocked out parts of the NHS for several days, took advantage of a Windows vulnerability that Microsoft had already issued a patch for. The affected computers simply hadn't been updated.

Turning on automatic updates means the device handles it without you needing to remember:

- **iPhone or iPad:** Settings > General > Software Update > Automatic Updates, then toggle on "Download iOS Updates" and "Install iOS Updates".
- **Android phone or tablet:** Settings > System > Advanced > Automatic system updates (exact wording varies by manufacturer).
- **Windows PC:** Start menu > Settings > Windows Update > Advanced Options > turn on "Receive updates for other Microsoft products" while you're there.

If a device is genuinely too old to receive updates anymore, that's a sign it may be time to replace it. An iPhone 6, for instance, stopped receiving security updates in 2022. Running an unpatched device is a bit like leaving a window open in an otherwise locked house.

## Simple habits that make a real difference

None of the above requires technical knowledge. What it requires is a small shift in habit. A few things worth building into your routine:

Lock your phone or tablet when you're not using it. A six-digit PIN or fingerprint lock means that if you leave your phone in a taxi, no one can access your accounts.

Be cautious about what you share on social media. Publicly listing your birthday, address, the dates you're going on holiday, or details about your finances gives scammers material to make their approaches seem more convincing. Facebook's privacy settings are worth revisiting if you haven't looked at them in a while. Age UK has a free guide to social media privacy that walks through the main platforms step by step.

Check your bank and credit card statements regularly. Most banks offer app notifications for every transaction; turning these on means you'll spot anything unusual quickly. UK Finance data consistently shows that the sooner fraud is reported, the more likely money is to be recovered.

And if something online ever makes you feel rushed or pressured, stop. Legitimate organisations don't demand instant decisions. The pressure itself is the warning sign.

## Where to get help if you're unsure

You don't have to figure any of this out alone. Several organisations offer free, patient support specifically for people getting to grips with the internet:

**Age UK** (ageuk.org.uk) runs a free digital inclusion programme in many areas, with one-to-one help available at local centres.

**AbilityNet** (abilitynet.org.uk) offers free home visits from trained volunteers for people over 65 or with disabilities. Their advisers can help with device setup, settings changes, and internet safety questions in plain English.

**Silver Surfers** (silversurfers.com) has a lively community forum where you can ask questions and get answers from other people in the same position.

For anything that might be a scam or fraud, the reference points are Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk) and the National Cyber Security Centre (ncsc.gov.uk). The NCSC publishes particularly good plain-language guidance on everything from phishing to password managers.
