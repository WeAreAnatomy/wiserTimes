# Streaming services explained: BBC iPlayer, Netflix, and beyond

If you've heard the phrase "streaming" and wondered what it actually means in practice, here's the short version: instead of recording a programme or buying a DVD, you watch it over the internet, on demand, whenever suits you. No downloads, no discs, no waiting for a specific broadcast time. You press play and it starts.

There are now well over a dozen streaming services available in the UK. Some are completely free. Some cost a monthly subscription. A few offer both. This guide covers the ones most likely to be useful, what they actually contain, and how to get started without needing to understand anything technical.

## What do I actually need to start streaming?

You need four things: a screen (a TV, tablet, laptop or smartphone), an internet connection, an account with the service you want to use, and — for paid services — a debit or credit card.

A broadband speed of at least 10 Mbps is enough for most streaming in standard quality. If you're watching in HD or have several people using the internet simultaneously, 25 Mbps or faster is more comfortable. You can check your current speed for free at speedtest.net.

A tablet such as an Amazon Fire HD (from around £74.99) is often the easiest starting point if you're new to this. The screen is large enough to watch comfortably, it's light to hold, and the main streaming apps come pre-installed or are simple to add.

## BBC iPlayer: the free one you almost certainly already know

BBC iPlayer is the BBC's own streaming service and it costs nothing beyond your TV licence. You can watch almost everything the BBC has broadcast over the past year, including EastEnders, Countryfile, Antiques Roadshow and all the major BBC dramas. There's also a growing collection of boxsets — full series of older programmes such as Peaky Blinders and Fleabag — available to watch at any time.

To use it, you create a free BBC account at bbc.co.uk/iplayer using your email address. The whole process takes about three minutes. You confirm that you hold a valid TV licence and that's essentially it.

Worth knowing: the BBC has gradually made iPlayer more than just catch-up. It now offers some programmes that appear on iPlayer before they air on television, which is a genuine reason to look in occasionally even if you watch the BBC regularly already.

:::callout
**TV licence reminder:** The TV licence fee is currently £174.50 per year (2025/26). If you're 75 or over and receive Pension Credit, you qualify for a free licence. Contact TV Licensing on 0300 790 6117 or visit tvlicensing.co.uk to check your eligibility.
:::

## ITVX and Channel 4: free catch-up with a lot more besides

ITVX (the rebrand of ITV Hub, in case the name sounds unfamiliar) and Channel 4's streaming service are both free to use, funded by adverts. Both carry recent episodes of their broadcast programmes — so Coronation Street, Emmerdale, Gogglebox, The Great British Bake Off — plus substantial back-catalogues.

ITVX has a paid tier called ITVX Premium at £3.99 per month, which removes adverts and adds a selection of extra content. Channel 4 dropped its paid tier in 2024 and is now fully free. Neither service requires a TV licence to use, which matters to anyone who has decided not to renew theirs.

Signing up to either follows the same basic pattern: go to the website or open the app, enter your email address, choose a password, confirm your age, and you're in.

## Netflix: the big paid service, and what it actually contains

Netflix is the service people tend to mean when they say "streaming" in a general sense. It's a subscription service with no free tier — prices start at £4.99 per month for its Standard with Ads plan, which includes adverts during programmes. The ad-free Standard plan is £7.99 per month; the Premium plan, which allows four streams at once and includes downloads for offline viewing, is £17.99 per month.

For that, you get access to Netflix's entire library: a mix of American, British and international drama and comedy, documentaries, films and some excellent nature programming. Original productions such as The Crown (filmed largely in the UK), Squid Game and various David Attenborough-narrated nature series have attracted large audiences. Netflix also holds licences for some classic BBC and ITV series.

You sign up at netflix.com. You'll need an email address and a payment method. Netflix offers no free trial at present in the UK.

One practical note: Netflix has tightened its rules on password sharing. Since 2023, an account is formally linked to one household. If your adult children want their own access, they'll need their own account.

## Amazon Prime Video: worth checking if you already have Prime

If you pay for Amazon Prime (currently £8.99 per month or £95 per year), Prime Video is included at no extra cost. It carries a solid selection of original series — The Grand Tour, Reacher, Mr and Mrs Smith — alongside a wide range of films and some classic British television.

What catches some people out is that not everything on Prime Video is included in the subscription. Newer cinema releases and some specialist channels are available as add-ons at an extra monthly cost. These are clearly labelled, but it's worth checking before you expect something to be free.

If you don't have Amazon Prime, you can subscribe to Prime Video alone for £5.99 per month.

## Disney+: films, documentaries and a surprising amount of history

Disney+ costs £4.99 per month (with adverts) or £7.99 per month without. Its library is broader than the name implies. Yes, it carries the Disney animated films and Pixar catalogue, plus the Marvel and Star Wars series. But it also holds the full National Geographic archive, a substantial range of historical documentaries, and a large collection of classic films through its Star channel.

For grandchildren who visit, it's probably unbeatable. For adults without children in the picture, the National Geographic and Star content is often underestimated.

## Apple TV+: small library, high quality

Apple TV+ is the outlier. At £8.99 per month, it carries fewer titles than any other service here. What it does have tends to be very well made: Slow Horses (a spy thriller based on the Mick Herron novels, filmed in London), Silo, Severance, and Ted Lasso. If any of those appeal to you, it's worth a month's subscription at minimum.

Apple offers a seven-day free trial. If you've recently bought an Apple device (an iPhone, iPad or Apple TV box), you may be entitled to three months free.

## Pluto TV and Freely: worth knowing about

Pluto TV is genuinely free and requires no account. You open it, pick a channel, and watch. It works like traditional television in that sense, but the content is streamed over the internet rather than broadcast. The library skews towards American crime dramas and older films, but there's a reasonable amount to explore.

Freely is a newer platform, launched by the BBC, ITV, Channel 4 and Channel 5 jointly, designed for people who want to receive UK broadcast television over the internet rather than via an aerial. It's available on a growing range of smart TVs. If your aerial signal is poor or you're in a flat where an aerial isn't practical, it's worth looking into at freely.tv.

## How do I actually sign up to a streaming service?

The process is essentially the same across all paid and free services:

1. Go to the service's website on a computer, tablet or phone — or find and open its app on a smart TV or tablet.
2. Select "Sign up" or "Create account."
3. Enter your email address and choose a password. Write them down somewhere safe.
4. For paid services, enter your debit or credit card details and choose a subscription tier.
5. You'll usually receive a confirmation email. Click the link in it to activate your account.

Once your account is set up, you can use it on multiple devices. Most services let you stay logged in permanently so you don't need to enter your password every time.

If you find small on-screen text difficult to read, both Android and Apple tablets allow you to increase the text size in the settings. Smart TVs have similar accessibility options, usually found under Settings, then Accessibility.

## What are the total costs if I subscribe to several services?

It adds up faster than people expect. Here's a realistic picture of what different combinations cost per month in mid-2025:

| Service | Cheapest monthly option |
|---|---|
| BBC iPlayer | Free (TV licence required) |
| ITVX | Free |
| Channel 4 | Free |
| Netflix Standard with Ads | £4.99 |
| Amazon Prime Video | £5.99 (standalone) |
| Disney+ (with ads) | £4.99 |
| Apple TV+ | £8.99 |

Most people find that two or at most three paid services, rotated as interest dictates, works out far cheaper than subscribing to everything at once. There's no contract on any of the above; you can cancel and restart month by month without penalty.
