# Smart doorbells and home security cameras: what you need to know

A smart doorbell is a small camera fitted where your old doorbell button used to be. When someone presses it, or simply walks up to your door, your phone rings. You can see the visitor, speak to them, and decide whether to open the door, all without leaving your armchair. For anyone who finds rushing to the front door difficult, or who wants to check on deliveries while they're out, it's a genuinely useful piece of kit.

This guide covers how the technology works, what to look out for when buying, and the privacy questions that trip people up.

## How does a smart doorbell actually work?

The doorbell contains a small camera, a microphone, a speaker, and a Wi-Fi radio. When motion is detected or the button is pressed, the doorbell sends a signal over your home broadband to an app on your smartphone or tablet. You see a live picture, and the two-way audio lets you hold a proper conversation.

Most models record a short clip when triggered and store it in the cloud (a remote server run by the manufacturer). You can play back that clip later to see who called while you were asleep or in the garden.

The camera quality is measured in resolution: 1080p is now the standard minimum, which gives a clear enough picture to read a face or a delivery label. Some newer models shoot at 1080p but with a square or tall frame, which is better at showing a person from head to toe rather than just their face.

Night vision is handled by infrared LEDs. You won't see colour in the dark, but you'll see a clear black-and-white image of anyone on your doorstep.

## What should you look for when buying?

There are four practical questions worth asking before you spend any money.

**Wired or battery?** If your existing doorbell runs off mains wiring (most traditional UK doorbells do), you can buy a wired smart doorbell that connects to those same wires. No batteries to charge, no gaps in coverage. If your doorbell is wireless, or you don't have a doorbell at all, a battery-powered model is much simpler to fit. Battery life varies: Ring's Video Doorbell (battery version) typically lasts six to twelve months between charges depending on how much activity you get.

**App and phone compatibility.** All the main brands, Ring (owned by Amazon), Google Nest, Eufy and Arlo, support both iOS and Android. If you use an Amazon Echo Show or a Google Nest Hub as a home display, check whether the doorbell integrates with it. Ring works natively with Alexa; Nest doorbells work with Google Home.

**Storage costs.** This catches people out. The doorbell hardware has a one-off price, but recorded clips usually live behind a monthly subscription. Ring's Basic plan is £3.49 a month per device; Google's Nest Aware starts at £5 a month for up to two devices. Eufy sells models with a local storage hub so you avoid ongoing fees entirely, which suits people who'd rather pay once and be done.

**Field of view.** A wider angle (140 degrees or more) covers more of your path and garden. But a very wide lens also captures more of the street or your neighbour's property, which matters for privacy (more on that below). A tight, forward-facing view is usually better for a doorbell specifically.

:::callout
**Worth knowing before you buy:** some older UK homes use a transformer that runs at a lower voltage than a smart doorbell needs. If you're fitting a wired model, check the voltage requirements in the manual, or ask an electrician to have a quick look first. It's a small job.
:::

## How difficult is installation?

Fitting a battery doorbell is about as hard as hanging a picture frame. You screw a small backplate to the wall, click the doorbell into it, download the app, and follow the setup steps. Most people manage it in under twenty minutes.

Wired installation is slightly more involved because you're connecting to your existing doorbell wiring. The wires are low-voltage and safe to handle, and the app walks you through every step. Ring's wired models come with good printed instructions. That said, if you're not confident with any kind of DIY, there's no shame in asking someone to help.

If you'd prefer a professional to do it, many local electricians will fit a smart doorbell for a modest call-out fee. Some larger branches of Currys also offer an installation service.

## What about data and privacy?

This is where a lot of people have reasonable concerns, and it's worth taking them seriously.

Under UK GDPR, if your doorbell camera captures images of people in a public space (the pavement, the road, your neighbour's front garden), you become what the law calls a "data controller." That sounds alarming, but in practice it means you should point the camera at your own property as much as possible, and you shouldn't share footage of identifiable people without a good reason.

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) has specific guidance on domestic CCTV and smart cameras. Their position is that cameras intended for personal home security are largely exempt from formal registration requirements, but you do have a responsibility to avoid capturing more than you need.

A few specific points worth knowing:

- Amazon (Ring) and Google (Nest) are US companies. Your footage may be stored on servers outside the UK. Both companies publish privacy policies explaining this, but it's a fact of life with cloud-based systems.
- Ring faced scrutiny in 2023 over US law enforcement data-sharing practices. In the UK, police requests for Ring footage must go through formal legal channels, not a voluntary neighbourhood portal.
- Eufy attracted attention in late 2022 when a security researcher found that some clips were being uploaded to the cloud despite the company's claim that footage stayed local. Eufy updated its practices following the publicity. It's a reminder to read the privacy settings properly on any device you buy.

None of this means smart doorbells are a trap. Millions of UK households use them without incident. But go in with your eyes open.

## Are there alternatives worth considering?

A standalone smart doorbell isn't the only option. Some people fit a small security camera above their door rather than replacing the doorbell itself. Arlo and Eufy both make compact outdoor cameras that do everything a smart doorbell does, except answer the door.

If you want to cover the back garden or a side gate as well, a two-camera system is often better value than buying individual devices. Arlo's Pro 4 cameras are well-regarded and work on battery. Alternatively, a system with a central recorder (called an NVR or DVR) means you're not dependent on a subscription at all, though the setup is more involved.

Age UK's website has a short guide to home security technology that's worth reading alongside this one, particularly if you're thinking about security more broadly rather than just the front door.

## What do they cost, and which brands are worth looking at?

Prices span a wide range. At the budget end, a Blink Video Doorbell (also Amazon-owned) costs around £50 and does the basics competently. Ring's mid-range wired doorbell sits around £90 to £110. Google's Nest Doorbell (battery) is typically £180. Arlo's doorbell costs around £150.

I'd suggest starting with Ring or Google Nest if you're new to this. Both have large user communities, clear apps, and decent customer support. The Ring Video Doorbell Wired, at around £50 to £60 on offer, is probably the most straightforward entry point for anyone who already has mains wiring at their door.

That said, this won't suit everyone. If you dislike the idea of a US company holding your footage, a locally-stored Eufy system is a reasonable alternative even if the setup is slightly more involved.

:::callout
**A note on Wi-Fi signal:** smart doorbells sit near your front door, which is sometimes the furthest point from your router. If your phone shows weak signal near the front of the house, consider a Wi-Fi extender (sometimes called a mesh node) before buying the doorbell. A poor signal causes lag and missed alerts, which rather defeats the point.
:::

## A quick word on scam calls

One unexpected benefit worth mentioning: several readers have told us that being able to see and speak to doorstep callers from a phone has made them much more confident about not opening the door to unexpected visitors. You can tell someone you're "just coming" while you work out whether they're legitimate, without revealing you're alone or unwell.

It doesn't replace other precautions, and it's not a substitute for the door chain or advice from Age UK's Scam Awareness resources. But as one small layer of confidence, it does the job.
