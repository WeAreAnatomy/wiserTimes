# How do you shop online safely in the UK?

Online shopping is overwhelmingly safe for the vast majority of people, most of the time. That said, fraud does happen: UK Finance reported that nearly £1.2 billion was lost to fraud in the UK in 2023, and a portion of that starts with a dodgy website or a phishing link. The good news is that protecting yourself doesn't require any technical knowledge. A handful of habits, applied consistently, will see off almost everything you're likely to encounter.

## How do you spot a fake or fraudulent website?

The most common trap is a website that looks like a real shop but isn't. These sites take your money, send nothing, and vanish.

The first thing to check is the web address. Look at the full URL in your browser's address bar, not just the name that appears on the page. A site called "amazonn.co.uk" or "argos-deals.net" is not Amazon or Argos. Fraudsters rely on people not reading carefully.

Next, look for the padlock icon to the left of the address. This tells you the connection between your device and the site is encrypted, which means your payment details can't be intercepted in transit. It does not, on its own, mean the site is honest. A fake shop can have a padlock too. But the absence of one is an immediate reason to leave.

Then look for these things before you hand over any money:

- A UK address and phone number (not just a contact form)
- A genuine returns and refund policy
- Real customer reviews on an independent platform like Trustpilot
- The company registered on Companies House (free to search at find-and-update.company-information.service.gov.uk)

If a price looks too good to be true, it almost certainly is. Counterfeit goods, non-delivery, and outright fraud cluster around suspiciously cheap listings. I'd be especially cautious with anything sold via social media adverts for branded goods at a fraction of the usual price.

## Which retailers are safest to buy from?

The simplest answer: buy from names you know. John Lewis, M&S, Boots, Argos, Amazon and the major supermarkets all have established customer service teams and clear returns processes. They also carry financial risk if things go wrong, which keeps them honest.

For smaller or unfamiliar retailers, a bit of homework pays off. Search the company name alongside the word "reviews" or "scam" before you buy. Check when the website was created using a free WHOIS lookup (try whois.domaintools.com); a site that appeared three weeks ago selling designer handbags at half price deserves scepticism.

The government-backed Cyber Aware scheme (cyberaware.campaign.gov.uk) has a simple checklist worth bookmarking if you shop online regularly.

## How does a secure checkout work?

When you reach the payment page of a legitimate shop, a few things should happen automatically. The address bar should still show the padlock. You may be redirected briefly to your bank's own verification page: this is 3D Secure, now widely branded as "Mastercard Identity Check" or "Verified by Visa". Your bank sends a one-time code to your phone, you enter it, and the payment is authorised. This extra step has reduced card fraud significantly.

If a website asks for your card details but skips all of this, that's not necessarily fraudulent (some smaller merchants opt out), but it does mean you have less automatic protection. Using a credit card rather than a debit card adds an important safety net here, because Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 makes your credit card provider jointly liable for purchases between £100 and £30,000. Get defrauded or receive faulty goods, and you can claim directly from the card company.

PayPal is another option worth considering. You pay via PayPal's own systems rather than entering your card number on the retailer's site, and PayPal's Buyer Protection covers you if an item doesn't arrive or isn't as described.

:::callout
**Worth knowing:** If you're paying by debit card for something under £100, you can still claim a refund through your bank under the "chargeback" scheme. It's not a legal right in the same way as Section 75, but the major banks honour it routinely. Contact your bank as soon as a problem arises.
:::

## How do you track a delivery once you've ordered?

Most UK retailers send an automated email or text with a tracking link once your order has been dispatched. Click the link and you'll see where the parcel is in the courier's system.

A word of warning: parcel delivery scam texts are extremely common. You may receive a message appearing to be from Royal Mail, Evri, DPD or Hermes saying your delivery needs a small fee paid before it can be released. Do not click the link. Royal Mail and the major couriers do not ask for payment via text message links. If you're unsure whether a message is genuine, go directly to the courier's official website by typing the address yourself, then enter your tracking number there.

If a parcel is shown as delivered but hasn't arrived, check with a neighbour and look for a card through your door first. If it genuinely hasn't turned up, the Consumer Rights Act 2015 is on your side: the retailer bears responsibility for delivery, not you. Contact the shop, not the courier. The retailer must arrange a replacement or refund.

## How do you return something bought online?

This is one area where online shopping is genuinely more flexible than buying in a physical shop. Under the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2014, you have the right to cancel most online purchases within 14 days of receiving them, for any reason at all. You then have a further 14 days to return the item. No need to give a reason.

The main exceptions are personalised items, perishable goods, and digital content you've already downloaded. Sealed audio recordings, video games or software where you've broken the seal are also excluded.

In practice, you start a return through the retailer's website or customer service team. Many large retailers, including Next, ASOS and Amazon, offer free returns. Some smaller shops charge a return postage fee, which should be stated in their returns policy before you buy. I'd suggest reading that policy before you order, particularly if you're buying clothing where fit is uncertain.

:::callout
**Practical tip:** Always keep proof of postage when returning an item. If you're sending something valuable, use a tracked service. Once you've handed it to the courier without tracking, proving it was returned becomes your word against theirs.
:::

## What are the safest ways to pay?

Credit card is the strongest option for consumer protection, as explained above. If you don't have one, a debit card with chargeback rights is the next best thing.

Prepaid cards, like those from Caxton or Revolut, are useful if you're nervous about using your main bank card on a new site. You load only what you need, so even if the details were somehow stolen, the damage is limited.

Cash, bank transfer, and cryptocurrency offer little or no consumer protection for online purchases. If a website insists on payment only by bank transfer, that's a serious warning sign.

Gift cards as a payment method are widely accepted by major retailers and are fine for everyday shopping. Be extremely cautious, though, if anyone contacts you to say a debt, fine, or problem can only be resolved by purchasing gift cards and sharing the codes. That is always a scam.

## What should you do if something goes wrong?

Start with the retailer. Most problems, whether a parcel is missing, an item is faulty or a return hasn't been refunded, are resolved at this stage. Keep records of every communication: screenshot email exchanges, note down the date and content of phone calls.

If the retailer won't help, your next steps depend on how you paid. Credit card holders can invoke Section 75 with their card provider. Debit card holders can pursue chargeback through their bank. PayPal users can open a dispute through the PayPal Resolution Centre.

For persistent disputes, the Citizens Advice consumer helpline (0808 223 1133) can advise you, and for online purchases from EU-based retailers, the Online Dispute Resolution platform is an option, though Brexit has made this less straightforward than it once was.

Age UK's digital support pages also cover online shopping safety and are written with older readers in mind. You can find them at ageuk.org.uk.

:::faq
- q: How do I know if an online shop is genuine?
  a: Check for a padlock in the browser address bar, look up the company on Companies House, read reviews on Trustpilot, and be suspicious of prices that seem far too low. A genuine UK retailer will have a physical address and a proper returns policy.
- q: Is it safe to save my card details on a shopping website?
  a: Generally yes, if the site is a well-known retailer. For smaller or unfamiliar shops, it's safer not to. Using PayPal or a credit card adds a layer of protection even if you do save your details.
- q: What should I do if a parcel goes missing?
  a: Contact the retailer first, not the courier. Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, the retailer is responsible for getting goods to you. If they won't help, you can raise a dispute with your bank or credit card provider.
- q: Can I return something I bought online even if there's nothing wrong with it?
  a: Yes. Under the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2014, you have at least 14 days to cancel most online orders and a further 14 days to return the item. Some retailers offer longer windows voluntarily.
:::
