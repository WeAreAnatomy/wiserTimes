# Accessibility features on smartphones that make them easier to use

Most people don't realise that their phone already contains everything they need to make it significantly easier to use. No extra apps to buy, no engineer to call. Both iPhones and Android phones come loaded with accessibility settings covering vision, hearing and physical control. The problem is that these features are buried several menus deep, and nobody tells you they're there.

This guide covers the features that genuinely make a difference: larger text, display zoom, voice control, the built-in magnifier and hearing aid compatibility. Everything here is free and already on your phone.

---

## How do I make text larger on my phone?

This is the single most popular adjustment people make, and for good reason. Default text sizes are set for younger eyes.

**On an iPhone:** Go to Settings, then Display & Brightness, then Text Size. You'll see a slider. Drag it right and text throughout the phone gets bigger immediately. If you need text larger still, go to Settings, Accessibility, Display & Text Size, and switch on "Larger Accessibility Sizes". That extends the slider further.

**On an Android phone:** The exact wording varies slightly by manufacturer, but on most phones (Samsung, Google Pixel, Motorola) you'll find it under Settings, Accessibility, Vision, then Font Size. Some Samsung phones put it under Settings, Display, Font Size and Style. Again, a slider controls it.

Worth knowing: increasing text size affects most of your phone's apps. A small number of older apps won't respond to it, but apps from major providers like BBC News, WhatsApp and the NHS App all honour your choice.

## What is Display Zoom and should I use it?

Text size and display zoom are different things. Text size only makes words bigger. Display zoom makes everything bigger: icons, buttons, spacing, the whole layout.

On an iPhone, you'll find it under Settings, Display & Brightness, Display Zoom. You'll see a "Standard" and a "Larger Text" option. Choosing Larger Text effectively shrinks everything down slightly so there's more room on screen, but the content itself is rendered at a bigger size. It suits people who find icons as fiddly to tap as text is to read.

On Android, the equivalent is called Display Size (not Font Size). Samsung calls it Screen Zoom. It sits in the same Accessibility or Display menus mentioned above, usually just beneath the font size slider.

I'd suggest trying text size first, then display zoom if that isn't enough. Using both at once can occasionally push buttons off the edge of the screen in some apps, so it's worth checking a few of your most-used apps after you change the settings.

## How does voice control work on a smartphone?

Voice control lets you operate your phone by speaking to it, without touching the screen. This is useful if you have arthritis, a tremor, or simply find the touchscreen fiddly.

Both platforms have this built in.

**iPhone:** Apple calls it Voice Control (not to be confused with Siri, which answers questions). Go to Settings, Accessibility, Voice Control, and turn it on. Once active, you can say things like "Tap Messages", "Scroll down", or "Go home". Numbers appear on screen next to every tappable element, so you can say "Tap 3" if you're not sure of the button's name. It works without an internet connection, which Siri does not.

**Android:** Google's equivalent is called Voice Access. You may need to download it from the Play Store first (it's free and from Google). After installing it, enable it under Settings, Accessibility, Voice Access. It works in a similar way, displaying numbered labels on screen.

Siri and Google Assistant are worth mentioning separately. They're not the same as Voice Control or Voice Access, but they're genuinely useful for day-to-day tasks. "Hey Siri, call David" or "OK Google, set a timer for ten minutes" requires no screen interaction at all. For many people, these voice assistants handle 80% of what they actually need to do.

## How do I use the magnifier on my iPhone?

The iPhone magnifier is one of those features that seems small until you actually use it. It turns your phone's back camera into a proper magnifying glass.

Go to Settings, Accessibility, Magnifier, and turn it on. After that, you can open it quickly by pressing the side button (on Face ID iPhones) or the home button (on older models) three times. You can also add it to your Control Centre so it's one swipe away.

Once it's open, a slider lets you increase magnification up to around 15x. There are also brightness and contrast controls, and a freeze frame button so you can take a still image and study it without holding the phone steady. The colour filter options (inverted colours, yellow on black, and others) can make menus, medicine labels or small print considerably easier to read.

Android doesn't have a built-in equivalent that's quite as polished, though Samsung devices include a "Magnification" feature under Accessibility that does the same job. Google Lens (pre-installed on most Android phones) can read text aloud from your camera view, which is a different but often more practical solution for reading product labels.

## Are smartphones compatible with hearing aids?

Many are, but the compatibility depends on both the phone and the hearing aid model.

Apple introduced Made for iPhone (MFi) hearing aid support several years ago. If your hearing aids are MFi certified, you can pair them directly to your iPhone via Bluetooth, and they'll behave like wireless earphones. You control volume and settings through the Health app or directly from Control Centre. There's no separate app to install. Apple maintains a list of compatible hearing aids at apple.com/accessibility.

Android introduced a similar standard called ASHA (Audio Streaming for Hearing Aids). It's supported on Android 10 and above, which covers most phones sold in the last four or five years. Not every hearing aid brand supports ASHA yet, so check with your audiologist.

If your hearing aids aren't directly compatible with either standard, you can often use a small intermediary device (sometimes called a streamer) that clips to clothing and connects to both the phone and the hearing aid. Brands like Phonak, Oticon and Widex all offer these. Your audiologist will know what's available for your model.

:::callout
**A practical note on phone calls:** If you're struggling to hear clearly on calls even with hearing aids, check your phone's call audio settings. On iPhone, go to Settings, Accessibility, Audio/Visual, and turn on "Phone Noise Cancellation". On Android, call quality settings vary by manufacturer but are usually found in the Phone app's settings.
:::

Even without hearing aids, both platforms offer real-time captions. On iPhone, go to Settings, Accessibility, Live Captions (this feature rolled out in iOS 16 and may not be available on older iPhone models). On Android, open Settings, Accessibility, Live Caption. Both transcribe speech from phone calls, videos and conversations around you, with a text window that floats over whatever else is on screen.

## What other accessibility features are worth knowing about?

A few others come up repeatedly when I speak with readers.

**Bold text:** Making text bold throughout the phone helps some people, independent of size. On iPhone it's under Settings, Accessibility, Display & Text Size, Bold Text. On Android, Font Style settings usually include a bold option.

**Reduce Motion:** Animations between apps can cause dizziness or nausea for some people, particularly those with vestibular conditions. On iPhone, Settings, Accessibility, Motion, Reduce Motion turns most of them off. Android has a similar "Remove Animations" switch under Developer Options or, on newer phones, directly in the Accessibility menu.

**AssistiveTouch (iPhone only):** This creates a floating on-screen button that gives you access to common phone functions without pressing the physical buttons. It's helpful if you find the side button or volume buttons difficult to press. Go to Settings, Accessibility, Touch, AssistiveTouch.

These features don't require any particular technical knowledge to use. A few minutes in the Accessibility menu will show you more options than you'd expect.

## Where can I get hands-on help with these settings?

Reading about this is one thing. Actually finding the right menu on your specific phone is another.

Age UK runs a free telephone advice service and can often direct you to local digital support sessions. The Digital Unite website lists local courses and one-to-one support sessions across the UK. Some libraries offer drop-in sessions with volunteers who'll sit with you and walk through your phone's settings.

If you have an iPhone, Apple Stores offer free "Today at Apple" sessions specifically covering accessibility features. You can book through the Apple website. Samsung's larger stores offer similar support.
