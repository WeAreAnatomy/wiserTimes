# Ways to stay in touch with family using technology

Keeping in close contact with family doesn't require technical expertise or expensive kit. A smartphone or tablet, a reliable Wi-Fi connection, and the right app are genuinely enough to hold a weekly video call with grandchildren in Edinburgh, share holiday photos with a daughter in New Zealand, or trade silly messages with a sibling all day long. This guide runs through the main options - video calls, group messaging, photo sharing and plain old voice calls - and offers some honest thoughts on which tools work well and which add more frustration than they're worth.

---

## What's the simplest way to make a video call?

The answer depends almost entirely on which devices your family already uses.

If everyone has an iPhone or iPad, **FaceTime** is the easiest option by a considerable margin. It's built into every Apple device, costs nothing beyond your internet connection, and starting a call takes two taps. There's no account to create, no separate app to install, nothing to remember. For a lot of people, that simplicity alone makes it the default choice.

If your family uses a mix of iPhones and Android phones (Samsung, Google Pixel, and so on), **WhatsApp** is the practical answer. It's free, it's installed on roughly 90% of UK smartphones, and its video call quality is excellent over a decent Wi-Fi connection. Group video calls on WhatsApp support up to eight people at once, which covers most family gatherings without any fuss.

**Zoom** earns its place when numbers get bigger. Free accounts allow up to 100 participants for 40 minutes per session, which is more than enough for a Sunday family catch-up. The slightly more involved setup (you need an account, and the host usually has to "start" the meeting) puts some people off, but the reward is reliable quality and the ability to see a grid of faces all at once. Many local libraries and Age UK branches run free Zoom introduction sessions if you'd prefer a guided start.

:::callout
**Before your first video call:** prop your phone or tablet against a stack of books or a purpose-made stand so it's roughly at eye level. Looking slightly downward into the camera is more flattering and less tiring than holding the device up throughout a call.
:::

## How does group messaging actually work in practice?

Think of a group message thread as a shared noticeboard where everyone in the family can post a quick note, a funny photo or a short voice message at any point in the day. Nobody has to reply immediately. It's less formal than a phone call and, for many families, becomes a genuinely warm part of daily life.

**WhatsApp groups** are the UK standard for family messaging. You can share text, photos, short videos, voice notes (handy if typing feels slow) and documents. One person creates the group, names it (something cheerful, usually), and adds everyone by their phone number. After that, it mostly runs itself.

A couple of things catch people out at first. Notifications. By default, WhatsApp will alert you every time anyone posts to the group, which can quickly become overwhelming if you have an enthusiastic family. Go into the group settings and select "Mute notifications" for a year - you can still check the thread whenever you like, but your phone won't buzz constantly. Worth doing on day one.

**iMessage** (the blue-bubble text service on iPhones) works similarly, but only between Apple users. If even one person in your family has an Android phone, messages switch to a less reliable format. WhatsApp sidesteps this entirely.

For families who prefer something visually cleaner, **Telegram** is a good alternative. It's free, fast, and allows file sharing without the size limits WhatsApp imposes. In my experience it's slightly less intuitive than WhatsApp for first-time users, though most people find their feet within a day.

## Which photo-sharing apps let me keep things private?

Posting to Facebook or Instagram means putting your photos where acquaintances, advertisers and occasionally strangers can see them. That's not what most people want when they're sharing pictures of grandchildren or a family Christmas.

**Google Photos Shared Albums** let you create a private album, invite specific family members by email, and share freely within that circle. Anyone you invite can add their own photos too, so the album becomes a collaborative family record. It's free up to 15 GB of storage, which is a very large number of photographs.

**Apple Shared Albums** work the same way within the Photos app on iPhones and iPads. If your family is all on Apple devices, it's marginally easier to set up than Google Photos.

**Lifecake** deserves a mention, particularly if you have grandchildren. It's designed specifically for family photo and video sharing, presents everything as a timeline from birth onwards, and keeps the whole thing completely private. Free for basic use, with an optional paid tier for extra storage. Fairly popular among UK families with young children.

What all three have in common: nobody outside your invited circle sees anything. No algorithm, no targeted adverts based on the faces in your photos. For a lot of people that matters more than any feature comparison.

## Are plain voice calls still worth using?

Completely. Not everything needs a screen.

A regular phone call - over the mobile network or via an app like WhatsApp - is often quicker, warmer and less fatiguing than a video call. You don't have to think about how you look. You can walk around. For people who find screens tiring on the eyes, a good old-fashioned phone call is frequently the better choice.

WhatsApp voice calls use your internet connection rather than your phone minutes, so they're free over Wi-Fi regardless of where the other person is in the world. Call quality is generally excellent, often better than a standard mobile call.

If you have a landline and prefer it, BT, Sky and the major mobile networks all still support traditional calls. The one thing worth knowing: the UK's old copper telephone network is being switched off between now and 2027 and replaced with digital lines (sometimes called VoIP). This won't disrupt your calls, but if you use a basic plug-in phone it may need replacing with one that works over your broadband router. Your provider should contact you before any change affects your line.

:::callout
**If you use a telecare alarm or pendant:** check with your provider before your landline is migrated to a digital line. Some older alarm systems don't work over digital connections and may need upgrading. The Telecare Services Association has guidance on this at **tsa-voice.org.uk**.
:::

## How do I choose what's right for me?

There isn't a single right answer, and I'd suggest resisting any advice that tells you there is.

The most useful starting point is a conversation with the family member you're most likely to call. What app do they already use? Start there, because the best tool is the one everyone will actually pick up. Installing something new that half the family ignores is more isolating than helpful.

A few other considerations worth thinking through:

**Screen comfort.** If you have macular degeneration or find small text difficult, look for tablets over phones. The iPad (standard model, not the Pro) is widely used among older adults and has good accessibility options built in. The text size, display brightness and button size can all be adjusted in the Settings menu under "Accessibility."

**Hearing.** WhatsApp voice notes are worth trying if phone calls are difficult. The other person records a short audio message; you play it back at whatever volume suits you, as many times as you like. Subtitles are available on Zoom video calls too, which some people find useful.

**Cost.** All the tools mentioned in this article are free for basic use over Wi-Fi. The only ongoing cost is your internet connection. If you're on a limited data mobile plan and don't have home broadband, that's worth factoring in.

## What if I get stuck or lose confidence?

This is where asking for help is entirely sensible. Most areas of the UK have free or low-cost digital support through local libraries, Age UK branches and charities like AbilityNet, which offers free one-to-one help by phone (0300 0120 406) and home visits in some areas.

The **Good Things Foundation** runs a network of community "online centres" across the UK where you can sit with a volunteer and work through exactly this kind of thing at your own pace. Their website at **goodthingsfoundation.org** has a postcode search to find your nearest centre.

One more thing: don't be discouraged if something takes a few attempts. Every person who now video-calls their grandchildren with ease had a first call where the camera was facing the ceiling or nobody could hear anything. That's just part of it.

:::faq
- q: What is the easiest video calling app for older people?
  a: FaceTime is the simplest if everyone in your family uses an iPhone or iPad. If not, WhatsApp video calling works on any smartphone and is free over Wi-Fi. Zoom is worth considering if you want to include larger groups, since it allows up to 100 participants on a free account.

- q: Do I need Wi-Fi to make a video call?
  a: Not strictly, but without Wi-Fi you'll use your mobile data allowance, which can run down quickly during a video call. Most providers suggest you'll use roughly 200–270 MB per hour on a standard call. If your data plan is limited, connect to home Wi-Fi first.

- q: Is WhatsApp safe for older people to use?
  a: WhatsApp uses end-to-end encryption, which means only you and the person you're messaging can read what you send. It's free, widely used and generally considered safe. As with any app, be cautious about messages from unknown contacts, and never share your bank details or passwords via any messaging platform.

- q: How do I share photos with family without social media?
  a: Google Photos and Apple's Shared Albums let you create a private album that only invited family members can see. Neither requires you to post publicly. Alternatively, the Lifecake app is designed specifically for family photo sharing and is used widely in the UK.

- q: My grandchildren use different phones to me. Does that matter?
  a: Less than it used to. WhatsApp, Zoom and Google Meet all work across iPhones, Android phones and tablets regardless of make or model. FaceTime is the exception - it only works between Apple devices.
:::
