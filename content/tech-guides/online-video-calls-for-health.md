# Using video calls for GP and hospital appointments

NHS video appointments have moved from a pandemic workaround to a standard part of how GP surgeries and hospital outpatient departments operate. Most practices now offer them for follow-ups, medication reviews, and initial symptom assessments. You don't need a smart television or a laptop. A smartphone that's a few years old will usually do the job, and in most cases you won't need to install a single thing.

This guide walks through what to expect, how to get your device ready, and the situations where it's entirely reasonable to ask for a face-to-face instead.

## What actually happens in an NHS video consultation?

The appointment itself is very similar to a phone call, except you and the clinician can see each other. You'll receive a text or email from your surgery or hospital with a link. At the agreed time, you tap that link, your camera and microphone switch on, and the call begins.

Most NHS practices in England use one of two platforms: Accurx (used by the majority of GP surgeries) or DrDoctor (common for hospital outpatients). Both run inside a web browser. You don't need an account, a password, or anything downloaded in advance. Scotland and Wales have rolled out Near Me, a similar browser-based service. Whichever platform your practice uses, the principle is the same: click the link, wait in a virtual waiting room for a minute or two, and the clinician joins.

The consultation usually lasts between five and fifteen minutes, roughly what you'd expect for a routine appointment. The GP can share their screen to show you a result or a diagram if they need to. They can issue a prescription electronically to your nominated pharmacy, just as they would in person. What they can't do, of course, is examine you physically, which matters for some problems more than others.

## How do I prepare my device before the appointment?

You don't need to do much, but doing it the day before saves a lot of stress on the day.

First, check your battery. A video call draws more power than a phone call. Plug in if you can, or start with at least 50% charge.

Second, find a quiet spot with decent light. The clinician needs to see your face clearly, so sit with a window or a lamp in front of you rather than behind you. A bright window behind you turns you into a silhouette. Background noise makes it harder to hear and to be heard. The kitchen with the radio on is not the best choice.

Third, test your camera and microphone. On an iPhone or Android phone, open the default camera app and look at yourself. If you can see yourself clearly, the camera is working. For the microphone, the simplest test is to make a brief voice memo or record a short video and play it back.

:::callout
**Before the appointment day:** Make sure your browser is up to date. On an iPhone, go to Settings > General > Software Update. On Android, go to Settings > System > System Update. An out-of-date browser is one of the most common reasons video calls fail to connect.
:::

Fourth, check your internet connection. Stand or sit where you plan to take the call and open a website you don't usually visit (so it isn't cached). If it loads quickly, your connection is fine. If pages take several seconds to appear, move closer to your router or switch to mobile data and try again.

Finally, when the link arrives, tap it once just to confirm it opens correctly. You won't join the waiting room by doing this; you'll just see a page asking you to enter your name. That's enough to know it works.

## What if I'm not confident with technology?

This is where it's worth knowing about a few practical options.

The NHS App, available free on both iPhone and Android, is worth having regardless of video calls. It lets you view your medical records, order repeat prescriptions and book appointments. If your surgery links its video appointments through the NHS App, the interface is straightforward enough that most people manage without help.

Age UK runs a free telephone befriending and support service, and many local Age UK branches run one-to-one digital help sessions in libraries and community centres. It's genuinely worth going along even once; having someone show you how to tap a link and check your camera in person makes it much less daunting the next time.

If you'd rather have help at home, AbilityNet offers a free remote digital support service for people over 65. You can request help at abilitynet.org.uk, and a volunteer will walk you through it at a time that suits you.

For families reading this on behalf of a parent: a ten-minute practice run before the first real appointment does a lot. Sit with them, use the link from a test message if the surgery provides one, and let them do the tapping themselves. The confidence that comes from doing it once is the whole point.

## When is a video appointment not appropriate?

Video is genuinely useful for a lot of things. It isn't right for everything, and you should feel comfortable saying so.

Ask for a face-to-face if:

- You need a physical examination. Chest pain, an unusual lump, a skin condition the GP needs to look at closely, a wound that might be infected: these need hands-on assessment.
- You have significant hearing loss and lip-reading is an important part of how you follow a conversation. Video quality isn't always consistent enough to support this reliably.
- Your broadband or mobile signal at home is genuinely poor and the connection keeps dropping.
- The subject matter is something you find very difficult to discuss without feeling that you're properly in the room with someone. Some people find it easier to talk about mental health, memory concerns or end-of-life questions face to face. That's a completely valid preference.

NHS England's own guidance acknowledges that video consultations are not suitable for all clinical situations, and GPs are expected to offer face-to-face appointments when there is a clinical need (see NHS England, *Delivering safe and high-quality consultations*, updated 2023). You don't need to justify the request. "I'd prefer to come in" is sufficient.

:::callout
**Your right to a face-to-face appointment:** Under NHS England guidance, patients must be offered a face-to-face appointment where that is clinically appropriate or where the patient requests one. If a receptionist says this isn't possible, ask to speak to the practice manager.
:::

## What about hospital outpatient video appointments?

These follow the same general pattern but tend to be a little more formal. DrDoctor is common across NHS hospital trusts for sending appointment links, though you may also receive a link directly from the trust's patient portal.

One practical difference: hospital consultants often run behind. In a physical outpatient clinic you'd sit in a waiting room; in a virtual one, you sit in a virtual waiting room, which is to say you wait on the page after clicking the link. Keep the tab open and don't lock your phone. The clinician will join when they're ready, and the system usually shows you a message to confirm you're in the queue.

For follow-up appointments after surgery or a procedure, video works well. The consultant can ask how you're recovering, review your latest blood results and adjust your medication without you making the journey. For older patients who find hospital travel tiring or difficult, this is one of the more genuinely useful changes to come out of the shift toward remote care.

## Does any of this cost money?

The appointment itself is free on the NHS. The data used during a video call is modest; a ten-minute video call uses roughly 200-300MB, depending on the platform and video quality. If you're on a limited mobile data plan, it may be worth connecting to home Wi-Fi first. Most home broadband packages have no data limit, so there's no extra cost there.

The NHS App is free. Accurx and DrDoctor do not charge patients. There is no subscription required for any of the NHS video platforms.

If your practice or consultant has offered you an appointment through a private service rather than the NHS, that's a different situation. Private online GP services such as LIVI, Push Doctor and Babylon charge per consultation or via a monthly subscription. These are outside the scope of this guide, but they're worth knowing about if NHS appointment times are very long and you'd rather not wait.

## Questions readers often ask

**Do I need to download an app for an NHS video appointment?**
It depends on which system your surgery uses. Many NHS practices use Accurx or DrDoctor, which run directly in your phone or tablet browser with no download needed. Others use the NHS App. Your surgery will tell you which to use when they send the link.

**What if my internet connection drops during the call?**
The GP or hospital team will usually try to call your mobile number if the video connection fails. Make sure your surgery has your current mobile number before the appointment, and keep your phone nearby during the call.

**Can I have a family member on the call with me?**
Yes. Tell the receptionist when booking, or mention it at the start of the call. Clinicians are generally happy for a family member or carer to be present, exactly as they would be in the room with you.

**Are video appointments suitable for urgent problems?**
If your problem is urgent, call 111 or 999 rather than waiting for any kind of appointment. Video consultations are best suited to follow-ups, medication reviews and initial assessments for non-urgent symptoms.
:::
