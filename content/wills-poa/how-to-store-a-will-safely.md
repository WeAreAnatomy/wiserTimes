# Where to keep your will so it can be found when needed

A will only does what you intend if someone can find it, read it, and prove it is the real, signed original. A surprising number of carefully written wills never reach probate because they were tucked in a drawer, sealed in an envelope a relative was afraid to open, or stored with a solicitor's firm that closed years later. The question of where to keep your will is, in practical terms, just as important as what it says.

Here is a straightforward look at your options.

---

## What are the main places people store a will in the UK?

There are four realistic choices: at home, with a solicitor or will-writing firm, deposited at the Principal Probate Registry, or registered (and often stored) through the National Will Register. Each has genuine advantages and at least one real drawback. None is universally ideal.

Most people in England and Wales end up with their will held by the solicitor or will-writing service that drafted it. That is convenient, but it is not automatically the safest or most sensible approach.

---

## Is keeping a will at home a good idea?

It can be, if you are organised and your executor is reliable. The advantage is obvious: no fees, immediate access, and no third party involved.

The problems are also obvious. Fires, floods and burglaries aside, home storage creates a subtler risk: the family finds a document but cannot be sure it is the latest version, or they hesitate to open it before probate and then cannot locate the original under pressure. I have heard of wills found inside old books, behind radiators, and in the freezer (apparently a real approach some people favour, on the basis that a freezer survives house fires better than a filing cabinet).

If you do keep your will at home, use a fireproof, waterproof document safe, not a cardboard folder or a kitchen drawer. Tell your executor exactly where it is and how to access it. Write this down somewhere separate and keep that note up to date.

One thing to avoid: sealing the will inside an envelope and asking someone to promise not to open it until you die. Executors need to be able to confirm the document is intact and that it is the most recent version. An envelope marked "DO NOT OPEN" achieves exactly the opposite of what you want.

---

## What does a solicitor charge to store a will, and is it worth it?

Many solicitors store wills free of charge, particularly if they drafted the document. Others charge an annual fee, typically between £20 and £40 a year, though this varies. Some will-writing companies, including Farewill and Beyond, offer storage as part of their service, sometimes bundled with a one-off fee rather than an annual subscription.

The practical benefit of professional storage is that solicitors hold wills in fireproof strongrooms, keep records of who the testator is, and are experienced in releasing documents to executors quickly when the time comes. Most will also send you a certified copy for your own records.

The catch is that firms close, merge, or are acquired. If your will is held by a solicitor's practice that no longer exists, tracking it down falls to your executor. The National Will Register (more on that below) can help in those situations, but only if the storage location was registered in the first place.

I would suggest that if you use a solicitor for storage, you ask for a receipt confirming the will's location, keep that receipt somewhere accessible, and tell at least two people where it is.

---

## How does the National Will Register work?

The National Will Register, run by a company called Certainty, is a searchable database that records the location of wills. It is not a government service and does not hold wills itself, though it can store them in partnership with solicitors. Registration costs around £30 as a one-off fee.

When someone dies, executors and probate solicitors can search the register to check whether a will exists and where it is being held. Certainty claims its database is the most widely searched in the UK for will searches at death.

Registering does not give your will any special legal status. It simply means that when your executor runs a search (which most probate solicitors do as a matter of routine now), a result will come back. If your will is held at home or with a small local firm, registration is a sensible belt-and-braces step. It costs very little and can save your executor weeks of uncertainty.

:::callout
**Worth doing regardless of where you store your will.** Registering the location with the National Will Register (certainty.co.uk) takes about ten minutes and costs around £30. It does not replace proper storage, but it ensures a routine probate search will find your will's location.
:::

---

## Can I deposit a will at the Probate Registry?

Yes. In England and Wales, you can deposit your original will at the Principal Registry of the Family Division in London for a fee of £20 (correct as of 2025; check GOV.UK for the current fee). Scotland and Northern Ireland have separate arrangements.

This is one of the most secure options available. The Probate Registry holds the document indefinitely, it is a government institution, and executors can retrieve it with relative ease. You can withdraw the will during your lifetime if you want to change it.

The downsides are minor but real. Depositing requires posting or delivering the original document, which makes some people nervous. And because it involves a government office, some people assume it is more complicated than it is. It is not.

If you want total certainty that the document will be found and cannot be accidentally destroyed, the Probate Registry deposit is worth serious consideration.

---

## What are the biggest mistakes people make with will storage?

A few patterns come up repeatedly.

**Storing the will in a safe deposit box at a bank.** Banks will sometimes seal a deceased customer's boxes before probate is granted, on the sensible grounds that they cannot verify who should have access. This creates a circular problem: you need the will to get probate, but you need probate to get the will. It is not insurmountable, but it adds delay and stress at an already difficult time.

**Giving the original to a beneficiary.** This creates an obvious conflict of interest and can lead to questions about whether the document has been tampered with or whether it is genuinely the final version.

**Not telling anyone.** The most meticulously stored will in the world is useless if the executor does not know it exists. Your executor needs to know, before you die, where your will is kept, how to access it, and who to contact.

**Making informal copies and not being clear about which is the original.** Only the signed, witnessed original will can be admitted to probate in ordinary circumstances. Photocopies are useful for reference but are not substitutes. If there are multiple signed copies floating around, it creates complications for the executor and potentially for the courts.

---

## Should I register my will with a digital vault or online service?

Several services now offer digital will storage, document vaults, and "death admin" platforms. These include companies like Settld and Octopus Legacy, among others. Storing a digital copy as a reference document is sensible. Knowing where the original signed paper will is located remains essential.

Courts require the original, signed, witnessed document. A scan, a PDF, or a digital copy is not a substitute for probate purposes. Digital storage works well as a signposting tool ("the original is at Smith & Jones Solicitors in Leeds, reference WF2024-0047") rather than as the primary storage method.

---

## A practical checklist before you put your will away

Before you file or deposit your will, run through these:

- Does your executor know where the original is stored?
- Do you have a certified copy for your own reference?
- Have you registered the storage location with the National Will Register?
- Is the storage location actually accessible (not a sealed bank box or a locked safe with a forgotten combination)?
- If you are using professional storage, have you kept the receipt or reference number somewhere separate?

For further reading on what a will should contain and when to update it, see our [guide to writing a will in the UK](/wills-poa/how-to-write-a-will/).

:::faq
### Frequently asked questions about storing a will in the UK

**Is the National Will Register the same as registering a will with the government?**
No. The National Will Register (run by Certainty) is a private service. There is no official government register of wills in England and Wales. Registering with Certainty does not give a will any legal force; it simply records where the document is stored.

**Can I store my will in a bank safe deposit box?**
You can, but it creates a practical problem. Banks sometimes seal a deceased person's accounts and boxes before probate is granted, which can make accessing the very document needed to start probate difficult. A solicitor's strongroom or the Principal Probate Registry is generally a better choice.

**Does a will need to be stored with the solicitor who wrote it?**
No. You can store a will anywhere you like, including with a different solicitor, a will-writing company, or at home. What matters is that the executor knows where it is and can access it promptly.

**What happens if a will is lost or destroyed?**
If the original signed will cannot be found, the estate may be treated as if the person died intestate, regardless of whether copies exist. Courts can sometimes accept a copy, but it requires an application and is not guaranteed. Safe, known storage matters enormously.

**Should I store my lasting power of attorney in the same place as my will?**
The LPA and the will serve different purposes at different times. The LPA is needed while you are alive; the will after your death. Keeping them together is convenient, but make sure your attorney knows where the LPA is right now, not just your executor.
:::
