# Advance decisions and living wills: making your wishes known

An advance decision to refuse treatment (ADRT) lets you set out, right now, which medical treatments you would refuse if you later lose the ability to communicate or make decisions for yourself. Made correctly under the Mental Capacity Act 2005, it is legally binding in England and Wales: doctors must follow it, even if they disagree with your choice. This guide explains what it covers, what it cannot do, and the specific steps that make one valid.

Scotland and Northern Ireland operate under different legislation. The principles are similar but the legal frameworks are not identical, so if you live there you will need separate guidance.

## What exactly can an advance decision cover?

An advance decision can only refuse treatment. It cannot request a particular treatment, demand that doctors do something specific, or ask anyone to hasten your death.

In practice, people use advance decisions to refuse things like cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), artificial ventilation, clinically assisted nutrition and hydration (a feeding tube), or dialysis. You can be as specific or as general as you like, but specificity is almost always better. A vague refusal is easier for a clinician to deem "not applicable" to the situation they are facing.

You can also set conditions. For example: "I refuse CPR if I have a terminal illness with no realistic prospect of recovery, but I do not refuse it in the event of an unexpected cardiac arrest with a reasonable prospect of full recovery." That kind of framing is entirely valid, and it reflects the sort of nuance that a blanket statement rarely captures.

What an advance decision cannot do: it cannot refuse basic care such as keeping you comfortable, clean and warm. It cannot be used to refuse treatment for a mental disorder if you are detained under the Mental Health Act. And it has no legal force in Scotland, where a different framework applies.

## How is an advance decision different from a Lasting Power of Attorney?

The two documents do different jobs, and in my experience people often assume they are alternatives when they are better thought of as complementary.

A health and welfare Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) appoints a specific person, your attorney, to make health and care decisions on your behalf once you lack capacity. That person can interpret new situations as they arise, weigh up your values and preferences, and exercise judgement. An LPA is flexible in a way that a written document never can be.

An advance decision, on the other hand, is a direct instruction. No intermediary. No interpretation. If it is valid and applicable, it must be followed.

The practical difference matters most in complex medical situations. An attorney can be briefed on your values over years of conversation. An advance decision is fixed at the moment you write it. For some people, having both in place makes sense: the LPA for decisions the advance decision does not cover, and the advance decision for the specific refusals you feel most strongly about.

If you have both, make sure they do not contradict each other. If they do, the more recently dated document generally takes precedence, but clinicians faced with conflicting documents may pause treatment pending legal clarity, which is the last thing anyone wants in a crisis.

Our guide to [Lasting Power of Attorney](/wills-poa/lasting-power-of-attorney-uk/) explains how to set one up and what it costs.

## What makes an advance decision legally valid in England and Wales?

For most treatment refusals, the requirements are relatively straightforward. You must:

- have mental capacity at the time you make it
- be aged 18 or over
- specify the treatment you are refusing
- specify the circumstances in which the refusal applies

There is no requirement to use a particular form or to involve a solicitor, though having a solicitor or your GP review it is sensible.

Life-sustaining treatment is different. If you want to refuse treatment that is keeping you alive, or that could keep you alive, stricter rules apply under section 25 of the Mental Capacity Act. The advance decision must be in writing, signed by you in the presence of a witness, and that witness must then also sign. Most critically, it must include a clear statement that the decision stands "even if my life is at risk." Without that exact kind of wording, a refusal of life-sustaining treatment is not legally binding, even if it is otherwise carefully drafted.

:::callout
**If you are refusing life-sustaining treatment**, the document must state explicitly that the decision applies even if your life is at risk. This is not optional phrasing. Omitting it means the refusal has no legal force for that treatment.
:::

The Mental Capacity Act also requires that the advance decision must not have been withdrawn (verbally or in writing) while you had capacity, and you must not have done anything clearly inconsistent with it since making it, such as requesting the very treatment you previously refused.

## How should you make an advance decision in practice?

There is no official government form, which surprises many people. You can write your advance decision yourself, use a template from a charity or legal service, or instruct a solicitor. The NHS provides guidance on what to include, and Age UK has a free template and factsheet worth reading first.

Dying Matters, the Compassion in Dying charity, and Compassionate Futures all offer template documents. Compassion in Dying runs a free helpline (0800 999 2434) and will review a draft advance decision if you want a second opinion on the wording.

A few practical points that often get overlooked:

Keep it somewhere accessible. A document in a filing cabinet nobody knows about is almost useless. Tell your GP, and ask them to add it to your medical records. Tell the people most likely to be with you in a medical emergency. If you have an attorney under an LPA, they should have a copy.

Review it. Circumstances change. A treatment you felt strongly about refusing at 65 may feel different at 75. There is no fixed review schedule required by law, but dating it clearly and revisiting it every few years is good practice. A simple written statement that you have reviewed and reconfirmed your decision, with a new date and signature, is sufficient.

Carry something on your person. Some people carry a card in their wallet or purse stating that they have an advance decision and where it can be found. The Compassion in Dying organisation provides wallet cards for this purpose.

## Will healthcare staff always follow it?

A valid and applicable advance decision is legally binding. But two conditions must be met: it has to be valid, and it has to be applicable to the specific situation.

Clinicians are entitled, and indeed obliged, to assess both. A doctor who cannot locate the document, or who has reason to believe it may have been revoked, or who judges that the clinical situation does not match the circumstances described, may proceed with treatment while the doubt is resolved. This is why precise, unambiguous drafting matters so much.

In genuine emergencies, staff may begin treatment before locating an advance decision, then stop once it is found. The law recognises this. But it reinforces the point about keeping the document accessible and making sure the people around you know it exists.

Healthcare professionals themselves are not required to act against their conscience, but they are required to arrange for another clinician to take over care rather than simply disregard your decision.

## What an advance decision cannot settle

There are limits worth being honest about.

An advance decision cannot address every scenario medicine might produce. It is a snapshot of your thinking at a particular moment, written without the full clinical context of whatever situation eventually arises. A thoughtful document drafted with your GP's input will cover more ground than one written in isolation, but it will never be exhaustive.

It also cannot substitute for a conversation with the people who matter to you. Families who know nothing about an advance decision, and who then discover one exists at the worst possible moment, sometimes struggle. That is not a legal problem; it is a human one. The conversation beforehand is, in my experience, just as important as the document itself.

And it cannot, of course, guarantee you a good death in the broader sense. For that, an advance care plan (which is different again from an advance decision) can help. An advance care plan is not legally binding but sets out your wishes for care, your values, your preferred place of death. Many GP practices and palliative care teams now offer support to draw one up.

## Getting help with the wording

If you want to write an advance decision without legal costs, Compassion in Dying and Age UK are the two most practical starting points. Both are free and have been working in this area for years.

If you want a solicitor to draft or review it, look for one who specialises in later-life planning or healthcare law. The Society of Trust and Estate Practitioners (STEP) directory lists relevant practitioners. Costs vary considerably; expect to pay somewhere between £150 and £400 for a straightforward document, more if it is part of a broader estate planning exercise.

Whatever route you take, do it while you have capacity and time to think. That is the one thing no template and no solicitor can substitute for.
