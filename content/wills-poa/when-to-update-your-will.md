# When should you update your will?

The short answer: any time your life changes in a way that affects who you want to leave things to, or what you want to leave. Marriage, divorce, a house move, a new grandchild, a significant inheritance — all of these can make a perfectly good will suddenly inadequate. And in some cases, they don't just make it inadequate; they can make it legally void.

A surprising number of people write a will once and assume the job is done. According to Canada Life research from 2023, around 5.4 million people in the UK have a will they haven't reviewed in more than a decade. A lot changes in a decade.

## What actually happens if you don't update your will?

Depending on the circumstances, one of two things: either your estate passes partly or wholly according to the intestacy rules (which follow a fixed legal formula, not your wishes), or it passes according to the will you wrote years ago, to people or in proportions that no longer reflect what you actually want.

Neither outcome is necessarily a disaster. But both can create real problems for the people you leave behind.

In England and Wales, the intestacy rules prioritise spouses and civil partners, then children, then more distant relatives in a strict order. If you have a long-term partner you're not married to, they get nothing under intestacy. If you've separated from a spouse but haven't divorced, they may inherit the bulk of your estate even if you haven't spoken in years. These aren't edge cases. They happen regularly, and they are entirely avoidable.

## Marriage and civil partnership

This is the most legally dramatic trigger, and the one people are most likely to overlook.

In England and Wales, getting married automatically revokes any will you made before the marriage. Your old will becomes legally void the moment you say "I do." If you don't make a new will, you die intestate. Your new spouse will inherit under the intestacy rules, but not necessarily everything — and anyone you'd named in your previous will gets nothing.

Scotland operates differently. Marriage does not automatically revoke a will in Scots law, but your spouse gains prior rights to the estate regardless, which can still produce outcomes you didn't intend.

The fix is straightforward: make a new will as soon as possible after marriage. Or, if you're planning ahead, make a will *in contemplation of marriage* before the ceremony, which explicitly states that it is made in anticipation of a specific forthcoming marriage and will survive it.

## Divorce and separation

Divorce doesn't revoke your will entirely, but in England and Wales it does cancel any gifts to your former spouse and removes them as executor. (Scotland, again, has its own rules.) On the face of it, that sounds like the legal system tidying things up for you. The problem is that it only kicks in once the divorce is finalised — decree absolute in old terminology, now called the final order.

If you die during the separation period, before the divorce is complete, your will stands exactly as written. Your estranged spouse could inherit everything you'd planned to leave them.

I'd suggest reviewing your will as soon as you separate, not once everything is legally settled. It's worth talking to a solicitor about the options during that period, because this is genuinely complex territory.

Also worth remembering: if your ex-spouse was named as a beneficiary in a trust set up under the will, or if they hold a lasting power of attorney that you haven't revoked, the situation becomes more involved still. Don't assume the legal system handles all of this automatically.

:::callout
**If you've recently separated:** Check your lasting power of attorney documents as well as your will. An LPA doesn't automatically lapse after separation or divorce. If your ex-spouse holds attorney powers, they remain valid until you formally revoke them. See our guide to [lasting power of attorney](/wills-poa/lasting-power-of-attorney/) for more on how to do this.
:::

## New children or grandchildren

A new grandchild doesn't invalidate your will, but it can mean a child you very much want to benefit is simply not mentioned.

Wills that list beneficiaries by name — "to my grandchildren James and Sophie" — won't automatically include a grandchild born after the will was signed. Wills that use broader language, such as "to all my grandchildren living at the date of my death," tend to be more future-proof. Worth checking which approach your will takes.

The same logic applies to stepchildren. Unless your will explicitly includes them, or they have been legally adopted, stepchildren have no automatic claim under a will or under intestacy. If you've taken on a partner's children and regard them as your own, the will needs to reflect that in plain terms.

## Moving house

Moving doesn't invalidate your will. But it can cause a specific problem if your will refers to a particular property.

Say your will states: "I leave my home at 14 Larkspur Close to my daughter." You then sell that house and buy somewhere else. The gift in the will fails, because 14 Larkspur Close is no longer yours to give. The new property isn't caught by the bequest. Depending on the rest of your estate and whether there's a residuary clause, the money from the sale might still reach your daughter — but it might not, and you'd have to read the will carefully alongside your actual estate to know.

If you move house, check your will. It's a ten-minute job with a potentially significant outcome.

## Significant changes to your assets or financial position

A large inheritance, the sale of a business, a pension drawdown decision, or a significant gift you've already made to a family member — any of these can affect how your estate should be structured.

If you've already given one child a substantial sum and you want your estate to end up fairly distributed, your will needs to reflect that. If you've inherited property that takes your estate over the inheritance tax threshold for the first time, you may want to talk to a solicitor about whether your existing will is still tax-efficient.

This is particularly relevant for equity release. If you've taken out a lifetime mortgage with a provider like Aviva or Legal & General, the outstanding loan reduces the value of your estate over time. A will written before you took out equity release may distribute an estate that is significantly smaller than the one you assumed. Worth reviewing the numbers and the document together.

## A beneficiary or executor dies before you

This sounds obvious but gets overlooked. If the person you've named as executor dies, your will doesn't automatically appoint a replacement. If a beneficiary dies before you do, the gift usually falls into the residue of the estate (or lapses, depending on the relationship and whether they had children of their own).

Review your will whenever someone close to you dies, particularly if they were named in it. It is not a pleasant task, but it's far simpler than the alternative — which is leaving your family to unpick an outdated document under grief.

## How to actually update a will

You have two options. You can add a codicil (a formal written amendment), or you can write a new will entirely.

Codicils are fine for small, specific changes: adding a single bequest, updating an executor's name. They must be signed and witnessed with exactly the same formality as the original will — two independent witnesses who don't stand to benefit. An improperly executed codicil can be challenged or ignored.

For anything more than a minor change, a new will is usually cleaner. Services like Farewill, Kwill, and Solicitors for the Elderly can help depending on how complex your situation is. A straightforward will typically costs between £100 and £300 through a solicitor; Farewill's online service starts at £90 for a single will at the time of writing. More complex estates, particularly those with trusts, business interests, or overseas property, need a specialist solicitor.

One practical note: when you make a new will, include a clause expressly revoking all previous wills. Then store it somewhere accessible, tell your executor where it is, and if you used a solicitor, they can hold a copy. The National Will Register (Certainty) allows you to register your will so it can be found.

:::callout
**What to do with the old will:** Once a new will has been properly signed and witnessed, physically destroy the old one. Tear it up, shred it. Leaving an outdated will alongside a new one creates unnecessary confusion, and there have been cases where an older will has been mistakenly used because it was found first.
:::

## How often should you review your will generally?

Even without a specific life event, a review every five years is sensible. Inheritance tax thresholds change (the current nil-rate band of £325,000 has been frozen until at least 2030 under current government plans, per HMRC). Property values shift. Family dynamics change. Beneficiaries move abroad, have children of their own, or develop needs you hadn't anticipated.

A review doesn't always lead to changes. Sometimes you read it, confirm it still reflects your wishes, and file it away again. That's fine. The point is to know.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does getting married automatically cancel my existing will?**
In England and Wales, yes. Marriage revokes any will you made before it, unless the will was drawn up specifically in anticipation of that marriage. Scotland has different rules. If you marry without making a new will, you die intestate — which may not reflect your wishes at all.

**How often should I review my will even if nothing has changed?**
Most solicitors suggest a review every five years as a minimum, even when life feels settled. Tax thresholds change, property values shift, and beneficiaries' circumstances evolve. A review doesn't mean rewriting everything — it just means checking that what you wrote still makes sense.

**Can I update a will myself, or do I need a solicitor?**
You can add a codicil — a formal written amendment — without a solicitor, but it must be signed and witnessed in exactly the same way as the original will. For anything more than a minor change, I'd strongly suggest using a solicitor or a regulated will-writing service like Farewill or Kwill.

**What happens if I move house without updating my will?**
The will itself remains valid, but if it refers to your home by its specific address — or if you have moved and the property you've left to someone is no longer yours — the gift may fail. It's worth a quick check every time you move.

**Does having a new grandchild mean I need to rewrite my will?**
Not necessarily rewrite, but you should review it. If your will leaves assets to your children and grandchildren by name, a new grandchild may be excluded. If you want them included, you'll need to update the document. Some wills use broader language that captures all grandchildren automatically — worth checking.
:::
