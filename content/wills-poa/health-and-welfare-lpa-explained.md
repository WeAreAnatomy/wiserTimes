# Health and Welfare LPA: what it covers and why it matters

A health and welfare lasting power of attorney (LPA) gives a person you trust the legal authority to make decisions about your medical care and personal wellbeing if you lose the mental capacity to make those decisions yourself. It is a separate document from the property and financial affairs LPA, and it can only be used once you've lost capacity, not before. Without one in place, your family has no automatic right to speak for you, even on something as personal as where you live or whether you have a particular operation.

That last point surprises a lot of people. I'll explain how the document works, what it does and doesn't cover, and what you need to think about before naming someone as your attorney.

---

## What decisions does a health and welfare LPA cover?

The document can cover a wide range of day-to-day and medical decisions. These include where you live (at home, in a care home, or elsewhere), what kind of care and support you receive, your daily routine such as diet and social activities, and medical treatment including whether to consent to or refuse specific procedures.

You can also use the LPA to give your attorney the authority to make decisions about life-sustaining treatment. This is optional and has to be explicitly granted. If you leave it out, doctors will consult your attorney on most things but will make decisions about life-sustaining treatment themselves, following the principles in the Mental Capacity Act 2005.

One thing the document cannot do is override an advance decision to refuse treatment (sometimes called a living will). If you've made a valid advance decision that refuses a specific treatment, and the LPA was made before that advance decision, the advance decision takes precedence. If the LPA came after, your attorney's authority prevails, assuming life-sustaining treatment authority was included. The interaction between these two documents is genuinely subtle, and if you're considering both, I'd strongly suggest speaking to a solicitor who specialises in this area.

---

## Who can you name as your attorney?

Your attorney must be at least 18 years old and have mental capacity themselves. They cannot be a paid care worker acting in a professional capacity (unless they're a close family member who happens to work in care). Beyond those rules, the choice is yours.

Most people name a spouse, adult child, or close friend. You can name more than one attorney and specify whether they must act jointly (all agreeing on every decision), jointly and severally (each able to act independently), or jointly for some decisions and severally for others. Jointly and severally is often more practical, particularly if your attorneys live far apart.

Think carefully about the person's temperament, not just their love for you. A health and welfare attorney will be dealing with consultants, care managers and sometimes the council in circumstances that are stressful and emotionally charged. The best person for this role is someone who listens, asks questions and stays calm under pressure. In my experience, that's not always the most obvious candidate.

You can also name a replacement attorney who steps in if your first choice is unable or unwilling to act. It's worth doing this, particularly if your primary attorney is around the same age as you.

---

## Does an attorney have to follow your wishes?

Your attorney is legally required to act in your best interests and to follow the principles of the Mental Capacity Act 2005. They must consider your past wishes, beliefs and values when making decisions, and wherever possible consult you even if you can no longer make the decision independently.

You can write specific instructions into the LPA. These are binding: your attorney must follow them. You can also include preferences and guidance, which are not binding but which your attorney should take into account. For example, you might instruct that you never want to be moved to a care home outside a particular area, and separately note that you'd prefer to continue attending your church if at all possible.

The distinction between instructions and preferences matters. Instructions limit your attorney's discretion. Preferences inform it. Both are worth thinking through carefully before you sign.

:::callout
**Put it in writing, even informally.** Many families find it helpful if the donor writes a separate letter of wishes alongside the LPA, explaining their values and priorities in their own words. This isn't a legal document, but it can be a meaningful guide for an attorney trying to make a difficult call years down the line. Solicitors who handle LPAs regularly can advise on how to draft one.
:::

---

## When can an attorney actually use the LPA?

This is the activation rule, and it catches people out. A health and welfare LPA can only be used when you lack the mental capacity to make the specific decision in question, and that has to be assessed at the time. Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific under the Mental Capacity Act: you might have capacity to decide what to eat for lunch but not to consent to a major surgical procedure.

In practice, a clinician or social worker will carry out a capacity assessment. Your attorney doesn't simply take over because you've been diagnosed with dementia or had a stroke. They step in for decisions where the assessment shows you can't currently make that particular choice yourself.

This is different from the property and financial affairs LPA, which you can set up to be used either from registration or only on loss of capacity, at your choice. The health and welfare version always requires a capacity assessment first.

---

## What are the limits of the document?

Your attorney cannot consent to certain things regardless of the powers granted. They cannot agree to deprivation of liberty arrangements (those require a separate legal process under the Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards, or the Liberty Protection Safeguards when those come into force). They cannot consent to you being given medical treatment purely for someone else's benefit. They cannot make a decision that conflicts with an existing valid advance decision.

Family conflict is also worth mentioning plainly. If two attorneys disagree and they're required to act jointly, the LPA is effectively paralysed until the dispute is resolved. If a family member who isn't named as attorney disagrees with a decision, they can raise concerns with the Office of the Public Guardian or apply to the Court of Protection. The court can remove an attorney if there's evidence of abuse or poor decision-making.

The document is registered with the Office of the Public Guardian, which has powers to investigate and intervene. That's not a criticism of the system; it's actually a safeguard worth knowing about.

---

## How do you set one up?

You can make a health and welfare LPA using the government's online service at GOV.UK, or through a solicitor. The fee for registering each LPA with the Office of the Public Guardian is currently £82 in England and Wales (as of 2025). If your income is below a certain threshold you may qualify for a fee reduction or exemption.

Services such as Farewill and Solicitors for the Elderly offer guided LPA preparation, at various price points. Some solicitors will do a home visit if mobility is an issue. Age UK also provides information and can signpost you to local services.

The form requires a certificate provider: someone who confirms you understand the document and aren't being pressured into signing it. This can be a solicitor, GP, or another professional. It cannot be a family member or someone named in the document.

Both the LPA and the property and financial affairs LPA need to be signed and then sent to the Office of the Public Guardian for registration before they can be used. Currently that process takes around 20 weeks, so there's no such thing as setting one up in a hurry when a crisis has already arrived.

:::callout
**Don't wait for a diagnosis.** The most common reason people can't make an LPA is that they've already lost the capacity to do so. Once that happens, a family member would need to apply to the Court of Protection for a deputyship order, which takes much longer, costs considerably more, and gives less flexibility than an LPA made in advance.
:::

---

## How does a health and welfare LPA fit alongside other documents?

It works alongside rather than instead of the property and financial affairs LPA. You can read more about how both types sit together in our guide to [lasting power of attorney](/wills-poa/lasting-power-of-attorney-guide/).

Some people also have an advance decision to refuse treatment, a Do Not Attempt Resuscitation (DNAR) order, or both. These are separate legal instruments. Your attorney, your GP and your care team all need to know what documents exist and where they're kept. Telling one person and assuming word will travel is not a plan.

If you're caring for someone else and wondering whether they still have capacity to make an LPA, speak to their GP first. A social worker or Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) can also help where family relationships are complicated.
