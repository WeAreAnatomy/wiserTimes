# Getting your affairs in order: a practical checklist

Most people know they should do this. Few actually sit down and do it. What follows is a straightforward checklist for UK residents who want to make things easier for the people they'll one day leave behind — or for those who simply want to feel more organised right now.

You don't need a solicitor to work through most of this. You do need a few hours, a notepad, and a willingness to look at the paperwork you've been avoiding.

## What does "getting your affairs in order" actually mean?

At its simplest, it means making sure that if something happened to you tomorrow, the right people would know where to find the right documents, who to call, and what you wanted.

That covers your will, your Lasting Power of Attorney, your insurance policies, your pension details, your bank accounts, your utility contracts, your subscriptions, your login credentials, and your wishes around things like funeral arrangements. It also means telling someone trusted where all of this lives.

None of this is morbid. It's practical. Families dealing with a bereavement or a sudden loss of capacity spend an enormous amount of time hunting for documents that were sitting in a drawer all along.

## Which documents should you gather first?

Start with the documents that have the biggest legal and financial consequences if they can't be found.

**Your will.** If you have one, locate the original now. If you don't have one, making one should sit at the top of your list. Farewill and Solicitors for the Elderly are both worth looking at; for more guidance on the process, see our full guide to making a will in the UK.

**Your Lasting Power of Attorney.** There are two types: one for property and financial affairs, one for health and welfare. If you've made them, the registered originals should be stored safely and your attorneys should know where they are. If you haven't made them, this is genuinely time-sensitive. You can only set up an LPA while you have mental capacity; waiting until a health crisis is too late.

**Pension documents.** This includes any workplace pensions, personal pensions, and your National Insurance record (available via your Personal Tax Account at gov.uk). If you have nominated beneficiaries on any pension, check those nominations are up to date. Pension providers often pay death benefits based on a nomination form you filled in years ago, not your current will.

**Life insurance and other policies.** Gather the policy numbers, provider names, and the contact numbers for any claims. If a policy has a named beneficiary, confirm that name is still correct.

**Property documents.** Your title deeds, if held in paper form. Your mortgage details if you have one. The Land Registry holds electronic records for most registered properties in England and Wales, but your solicitor may hold original deeds for older unregistered properties.

## How should you store everything?

A fireproof document safe is a reasonable first step. Sentry and Phoenix both make models suited to home use, starting at around £60 to £80. They won't survive a catastrophic house fire at extreme temperatures, but they'll protect documents from most domestic incidents.

For anything irreplaceable, consider a second copy held elsewhere. Many solicitors will store your original will for a modest annual fee, or free of charge if they drew it up. HM Courts and Tribunals Service also offers safe custody of wills through the probate registry, currently at a one-off fee of £20.

Keep digital scans too. A password-protected folder in cloud storage (iCloud, Google Drive, or a dedicated service like LegalVault) means the information survives even if the physical copies don't. That said, a digital copy of a will is not legally valid on its own; original signatures still matter for probate purposes.

One thing people often forget: tell someone where the safe is, and where the key or combination code is kept. A perfectly organised document system that only you know about helps nobody.

:::callout
**Make a single "if something happens to me" document.** A one or two-page summary listing where your will is held, who your solicitor is, your key account numbers, your insurance provider names, and the location of your safe. Give a copy to your executor and to one other trusted person. Review it once a year.
:::

## Who needs to know what?

Your executor needs to know: where your will is held, the name of your solicitor (if you have one), and roughly what assets and liabilities you have. They don't need access to your accounts while you're alive, but they do need enough information to act quickly after your death.

Your attorneys under any LPA need to know: where the registered original documents are held, the details of your main bank accounts, and your key medical contacts if the health and welfare LPA is relevant.

A trusted family member or friend should know: where your important documents are stored, the name and number of your GP, your NHS number, and whether you have any advance decisions (sometimes called living wills) about medical treatment.

Your GP surgery should hold a copy of any advance decision to refuse treatment, and ideally a record of any DNACPR (Do Not Attempt Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) decision if you have made one. Age UK has useful guidance on this.

## What about utilities, subscriptions, and digital accounts?

This section is the one people most often skip, and it's the one that causes the most friction for families afterwards.

Write down, on paper or in a secure digital note, every regular payment that leaves your account. That means:

- Gas and electricity suppliers (your MPAN and MPRN numbers are on your bill)
- Water company
- Broadband and phone contracts
- TV licence
- Any streaming services: Netflix, Amazon Prime, Disney+, and so on
- Magazine or newspaper subscriptions
- Any membership fees (RAC, National Trust, private health insurance)

When someone dies, executors have to track all of these down and cancel them. A prepared list can cut hours off that process.

Digital accounts are a separate consideration. Your email, your online banking login, your Apple ID or Google account all have value and in some cases hold financial information or sentimental content (photographs, for instance). You can't legally pass passwords to another person under most platform terms of service, but Google and Apple both offer legacy contact or inactive account manager settings, which let you designate someone to access your account after your death. It's worth setting these up.

For a deeper look at this topic, the Digital Legacy Association (digitallegacyassociation.org) is a UK charity with practical, non-commercial guidance.

:::callout
**Don't store passwords in your will.** A will becomes a public document once probate is granted. Write passwords and login details in a separate document, tell your executor where it is, and update it when things change.
:::

## What about funeral wishes?

You don't have to write a detailed plan, though some people find it helpful and some funeral directors, including Co-op Funeralcare and Dignity, offer pre-paid funeral plans that lock in costs and record your preferences formally.

At minimum, let someone know: burial or cremation, any religious or non-religious preferences, and whether you have a pre-paid plan in place. If you do have a plan, keep the paperwork with your other important documents and note the policy number.

One practical point: your will is often not read until after the funeral. If your funeral wishes are recorded only in your will, they may never be seen in time. Keep a separate note of your preferences and share it.

## How often should you review everything?

Once a year is a reasonable habit. Many people tie it to a birthday, the new year, or the anniversary of a significant event. The specific trigger doesn't matter; consistency does.

Review after any major life change without waiting for the annual check. Divorce, remarriage, the birth of a grandchild, a significant change in assets, the death of an executor or attorney, a house move: all of these can make existing documents outdated or in some cases legally ineffective. Marriage, for example, revokes a will made before it under English and Welsh law.

Your nominated beneficiaries on pensions and life insurance policies deserve a review too. These nominations sit outside your will and are sometimes overlooked entirely.

## A practical checklist to work through

This isn't exhaustive, but it covers the things that matter most.

**Documents to locate or create:**
- [ ] Will (original, stored safely; copy given to executor)
- [ ] Lasting Power of Attorney: property and financial affairs
- [ ] Lasting Power of Attorney: health and welfare
- [ ] Pension statements and nomination forms
- [ ] Life insurance policies
- [ ] Home insurance policy
- [ ] Property title deeds or Land Registry details
- [ ] Birth certificate and marriage certificate if applicable
- [ ] Passport and driving licence (note expiry dates)

**People to tell:**
- [ ] Executor knows where the will is
- [ ] Attorneys under LPA know where the registered documents are
- [ ] Trusted person has the "if something happens to me" summary document
- [ ] GP has a copy of any advance decision

**Practical records to write down:**
- [ ] Bank and building society account details
- [ ] Every regular direct debit and subscription
- [ ] Utility suppliers and account numbers
- [ ] Digital account legacy contacts set up (Google, Apple)
- [ ] Passwords document stored separately from the will

Getting this done takes a few hours at most. The alternative is leaving it to your family at the worst possible time.

If you haven't yet made a will or Lasting Power of Attorney, those are the two items that genuinely do need professional input. A solicitor registered with the Solicitors Regulation Authority, or a regulated will-writing service, can guide you through both. Citizens Advice and Age UK both offer free initial signposting if you're not sure where to start.

:::faq
- q: Where is the safest place to keep important documents in the UK?
  a: A fireproof home safe works for most people. Alternatively, a solicitor can hold originals, and some banks offer document storage. Tell at least one trusted person where to find everything — a document that nobody can locate is effectively useless.
- q: Should I store my will at home or with a solicitor?
  a: Both is sensible. Keep a copy at home in a known location, and store the original with a solicitor or Will Aid-registered service such as Farewill's solicitor partner network. HM Courts and Tribunals Service also offers probate registry storage for a small fee.
- q: What happens to direct debits and subscriptions when someone dies?
  a: They continue until cancelled. Executors and family members often discover ongoing payments months after a death. Keeping a written list of every subscription and standing order saves time and money.
- q: Do I need to tell my bank I've made a will?
  a: No. But telling your bank who your executor is, and ensuring your account details are recorded somewhere accessible, speeds up probate considerably.
- q: What is a Lasting Power of Attorney and where should I keep it?
  a: A Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) is a legal document appointing someone to make decisions on your behalf if you lose capacity. Register it with the Office of the Public Guardian, then keep the registered original somewhere safe and tell your attorneys where it is.
:::
