# How long does probate take in England and Wales?

For a straightforward estate with a valid will, no disputes and a modest asset list, probate in England and Wales typically takes between nine and twelve months from the date of death to final distribution. That figure often surprises people. Most of that time is not spent waiting at the probate registry; it is spent gathering valuations, settling tax, notifying institutions and waiting for third parties to respond.

Complex estates - those involving property abroad, business assets, disputed wills or Inheritance Tax complications - can run to two years or considerably longer.

---

## What does "probate" actually cover?

The word is used loosely to mean two different things, and it helps to separate them. In the strict sense, probate refers to the Grant of Probate: the legal document issued by the HMCTS Probate Registry that confirms an executor's authority to deal with the estate. If the deceased left no will, the equivalent document is called Letters of Administration.

In everyday speech, people use "probate" to mean the whole process of administering a deceased person's estate from death to final distribution. That is the broader sense I'll use here, because the grant itself is just one milestone in a longer journey.

---

## What are the main stages, and how long does each take?

The timeline breaks into several phases, each with its own variables.

**Registering the death and gathering paperwork (one to four weeks).** This cannot begin until the death is registered, which must happen within five days in England and Wales. Executors need the death certificate (you'll want several certified copies), the original will, and contact details for all beneficiaries. If the will is held by a solicitor, you may need to wait for it to be released.

**Valuing the estate (four to twelve weeks, sometimes longer).** Every asset and liability must be valued as at the date of death. That means bank balances, investments, pension death benefits, property, personal possessions and any business interests, offset against outstanding mortgages, debts and funeral costs. Property valuations require a formal RICS valuation for Inheritance Tax purposes. If assets include shares in a private company, agricultural land or overseas property, this stage can stretch considerably. HMRC's IHT400 form and its associated schedules are extensive documents; many executors underestimate the time required to complete them accurately.

**Applying for the Grant of Probate (two to sixteen weeks, depending on current registry waiting times).** Once the estate is valued and any Inheritance Tax is paid or arranged, the executor submits the application to the probate registry, either online via the HMCTS portal or by post. As of early 2025, the registry is processing straightforward applications in roughly eight to twelve weeks, though this has fluctuated significantly in recent years. The registry published average waiting times on GOV.UK, which I'd recommend checking before you plan the timeline.

One important point: Inheritance Tax (where applicable) must generally be paid before the grant is issued. HMRC operates a Direct Payment Scheme that lets executors pay IHT directly from the deceased's bank accounts before the grant is obtained, which avoids the awkward circularity of needing the grant to access funds but needing funds to get the grant.

**Collecting assets and settling debts (four to twelve weeks after the grant).** With the grant in hand, executors can close bank accounts, sell investments, and begin the conveyancing process on any property. Each institution has its own internal process. Some banks turn things around in a fortnight; others take two months. Conveyancing on a property adds its own variable timeline.

**Distributing the estate (one to three months after assets are collected).** Before distributing, prudent executors place a statutory notice in The Gazette and a local newspaper. This protects them against unknown creditors and typically runs for two months. After that period, and once any final tax returns and any Capital Gains Tax on estate assets are settled, the executor can distribute.

---

## What causes probate to take longer than expected?

In my experience, delays almost always fall into a handful of categories rather than being truly unpredictable.

**Inheritance Tax disputes with HMRC.** If HMRC questions a property valuation or challenges the eligibility of an asset for Business Relief or Agricultural Relief, correspondence with the Inheritance Tax office can add months. HMRC has wide powers to investigate estate valuations up to four years after the account was submitted, and disagreements over the value of a family business or farm can become protracted.

**Property that won't sell.** An executor cannot finalise the estate until property is either sold or transferred, and a slow conveyancing market or a difficult buyer can hold everything up. Some executors choose to distribute the rest of the estate and leave the property element open; that requires careful drafting of the assent.

**Missing beneficiaries.** If the will names someone who cannot be located, or if there is an intestacy involving relatives who need to be traced, a genealogist may need to be instructed. Missing beneficiary insurance is often taken out in these cases. Neither process is fast.

**Multiple executors who disagree.** Technically, most decisions in estate administration require all executors to act jointly. If two of three executors are willing to proceed but the third is uncontactable or obstructive, things stall. This is more common than people expect, particularly in families where relationships are strained by grief.

**A contested will.** If a beneficiary or excluded family member raises a challenge to the will's validity, or makes a claim under the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975, the estate cannot be distributed until the matter is resolved. Contested probate litigation can run for years.

:::callout
**The two-month waiting rule most executors forget**

Before distributing an estate, executors should place a statutory notice under s.27 Trustee Act 1925 in The Gazette and a local newspaper. This gives creditors and claimants two months to come forward. It is not legally compulsory, but without it an executor can be personally liable for debts that surface after distribution. A solicitor can place this notice on your behalf.
:::

## What can executors do while waiting for the grant?

Quite a lot, as it happens. The period before the grant arrives does not have to be dead time.

You can notify banks, pension providers and government departments of the death without the grant. You can obtain valuations of property and investments. You can correspond with HMRC and begin completing the IHT400 if one is needed. You can arrange for utilities and insurance on any property to be maintained. You can also take initial legal advice on any complications you have spotted.

What you cannot do without the grant is sell or transfer property held in the deceased's sole name, close bank accounts above the relevant institution's threshold (often around £15,000 to £50,000, though this varies widely), or formally transfer investments.

If an executor needs access to funds to pay the funeral or immediate bills, many banks will release funds directly to the funeral director and, in some cases, to the executor for essential expenses, before the grant arrives. It is worth asking.

---

## Does using a solicitor make probate faster?

Sometimes, but not always. A solicitor with a well-run probate team can avoid the common procedural errors that cause applications to be returned or queried, which genuinely saves time. The HMCTS digital portal is primarily designed for professional users, and solicitor applications have, in some periods, been processed faster than personal applications.

That said, a solicitor still depends on you to provide documents, and still waits for the same third parties. What a solicitor adds is process experience and the ability to handle correspondence with HMRC, missing beneficiaries and difficult institutions. For a straightforward estate, a confident executor who reads the GOV.UK guidance carefully can manage perfectly well without one. For anything involving Inheritance Tax, overseas assets or family tension, professional help is worth the cost.

Farewill and Co-op Legal Services both offer fixed-fee probate services that sit between full solicitor instruction and full DIY. They are worth considering for mid-complexity estates where the executor wants some support without paying hourly solicitor rates.

## When probate is not needed at all

Not every death triggers the need for a grant. Assets held jointly (such as a jointly-owned home or a joint bank account) pass automatically to the surviving joint owner and do not form part of the estate for probate purposes. Assets written in trust, including many life insurance policies and some pension death benefits, also pass outside the estate.

If the deceased's solely-owned assets are below a financial institution's threshold and there is no property to transfer, many banks and building societies will release funds on the basis of a death certificate alone. That threshold varies: it can be as low as £5,000 or as high as £50,000 depending on the institution. Always ask.

The GOV.UK Probate Service guidance sets out when a grant is and is not required, and it is a good first stop before you commit to beginning the formal process.
