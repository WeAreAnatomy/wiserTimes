# How to choose an executor for your will

The executor of your will is the person who takes legal responsibility for sorting out your estate after you die. They apply for probate, gather your assets, pay any debts and tax, and distribute what's left to your beneficiaries. It sounds administrative, but done badly it can cause months of delay, family conflict and unnecessary cost.

Choosing the right person, or people, matters. This guide explains what the job actually involves, who is eligible, and the practical differences between appointing a family member and using a professional.

## What does an executor actually do?

The honest answer is: quite a lot, over quite a long time. The formal legal steps are only part of it.

Once someone dies, the executor has to register the death, locate the will, notify banks and other institutions, and apply for a grant of probate (assuming the estate requires one, which most do). That alone involves compiling a detailed inventory of everything the deceased owned and owed, at the date of death. HMRC will need an inheritance tax return even if no tax is actually due.

After probate is granted, the executor collects the assets into an executor's account, settles any outstanding bills and tax liabilities, and only then distributes to beneficiaries. They're also responsible for filing a final income tax return for the deceased, which trips up a lot of lay executors who aren't expecting it.

Throughout all of this, the executor has a legal duty of care to the beneficiaries. If they make an error that costs the estate money, they can be held personally liable. That isn't a theoretical risk. It does happen.

In my experience, people consistently underestimate how time-consuming the role is. For a fairly modest estate with a house, a couple of bank accounts, and a pension, it's realistic to expect thirty to fifty hours of work spread over the best part of a year.

## Who is eligible to be an executor?

Almost any adult can act as executor under the law of England and Wales. There is no requirement for legal knowledge, financial qualifications, or any prior experience of estates. The only firm restrictions are age (they must be 18 or over) and mental capacity at the time probate is applied for.

A beneficiary can be an executor. A spouse, child, sibling or close friend can all act. So can a solicitor, a bank trust department, or a specialist probate firm. You can appoint up to four executors, though two is the most common arrangement for estates that include property.

Scotland and Northern Ireland have their own rules on executors, and the terminology differs slightly. If you're based in Scotland, you'll be looking at "executors-nominate" and the confirmation process rather than probate. For the purposes of this article, I'm focusing on England and Wales.

## Professional executor or someone you trust?

This is where most people get stuck, and there's no single answer that works for everyone.

A professional executor (a solicitor, licensed probate practitioner, or bank trust department) brings expertise and removes the burden from your family at a difficult time. They know the process, they carry professional indemnity insurance, and they won't be grieving while they're filling in HMRC forms. The downside is cost: professional executors typically charge between 1% and 4% of the estate's gross value, plus VAT, plus disbursements. On a £500,000 estate, that could easily be £10,000 to £20,000, taken before your beneficiaries receive a penny.

Some solicitors also offer a fixed fee for straightforward estates. It's worth asking. And some firms, Farewill being a well-known example on the online end of the market, offer probate services at a flat rate that can be significantly cheaper than traditional solicitors for simpler estates.

A lay executor, typically a family member or close friend, costs the estate nothing beyond their actual out-of-pocket expenses. They may also have a much more personal understanding of your wishes and your family. The risks are real, though. Lay executors can make costly mistakes through inexperience. They may find themselves in the middle of family disputes. And they're taking on a substantial time commitment at a point when they may already be dealing with bereavement and practical upheaval.

One arrangement worth knowing about: you can appoint a family member as executor and then instruct them to engage a solicitor for the legal and administrative work. The executor retains control and legal responsibility but pays a professional to do the heavy lifting. This is sometimes called "executor-led probate" and it tends to cost less than giving a solicitor full executor authority.

:::callout
**Watch out for bank executor clauses.** Some older wills appoint a high street bank's trust department as executor. Banks including Barclays and NatWest have historically charged fees well above the market rate for this service. If you have an older will with a bank named as executor, it's worth reviewing whether that still makes sense.
:::

## How many executors should you appoint?

Appointing two executors is generally sensible. It means that if one predeceases you, or is unable to act, there's still someone authorised to deal with the estate without the family having to apply to the court.

Where property is involved, two executors can also be useful practically: both need to sign for certain transactions.

Appointing more than two is possible but rarely necessary. Four people agreeing on every decision is slower than two. If you have a complex estate, a large family, or specific reasons for wanting more oversight, three can make sense. Beyond that, the coordination overhead usually outweighs the benefit.

It's also worth naming a substitute executor in your will, someone who steps in if your first choice can't or won't act. This doesn't cost anything extra and it can save significant difficulty later.

## How do you decide who to ask?

Start by thinking about the person's practical qualities rather than their emotional closeness to you. A trustworthy executor needs to be organised, able to communicate clearly with banks, solicitors and HMRC, and willing to handle paperwork and deadlines over an extended period.

They also need to be available. An adult child who lives abroad and works long hours may be your obvious choice emotionally, but the role will be much harder for them to fulfil. Geographic distance isn't a legal barrier, but it does add friction to a process that already has plenty.

Consider the dynamics between your executors and your beneficiaries. If you're appointing one child as executor and they'll be distributing assets to other children, is that a role that will create resentment or suspicion, regardless of how fairly they act? This is more common than people expect and it's worth being realistic.

Also, ask them first. It sounds obvious, but a number of people are named in wills without ever having been told. An executor has the right to renounce the role before they take any action on the estate. If the person you've named didn't know, didn't want the job, and renounces, your estate may end up with no active executor at all.

## Does the size or complexity of your estate change things?

Yes, considerably. For a simple estate (a bank account, some savings, no property, clear beneficiaries) a capable family member can usually manage the process with modest professional help at specific points. HMRC and GOV.UK both publish guidance for lay executors, and services like Farewill provide step-by-step support.

A more complicated estate shifts the calculation. Business interests, overseas assets, contested debts, multiple properties, trusts within the will, or a situation where beneficiaries are likely to disagree: any of these make professional executor involvement much more worth the cost. A solicitor who specialises in probate isn't a luxury in those circumstances.

Age UK publishes useful plain-English guidance on probate and executor duties for those who want a fuller overview. For anything beyond a straightforward estate, a solicitor who is a member of the Society of Trust and Estate Practitioners (STEP) is worth seeking out.

:::callout
**Executor and power of attorney are different roles.** A lasting power of attorney (LPA) only operates while you're alive. It ends at death. Your attorney under an LPA has no authority over your estate unless they're also named as executor in your will. If you're putting both documents in place, make sure the right people are named for each job. Priya's guides on LPAs cover this in more detail.
:::

## Should you review your executor choice over time?

Your will isn't a document you write once and file away. The person you chose as executor at 55 may be less suitable at 75 if their own health has changed, if they've moved abroad, or if your relationship has deteriorated. It's reasonable to review your will, and your executor appointments specifically, every five years or whenever your circumstances change significantly.

Divorce is one situation worth flagging: in England and Wales, divorce automatically revokes any appointment of a former spouse as executor (as well as any gifts to them). Separation without a formal divorce does not have the same effect. If you've separated, updating your will is worth doing promptly.

- q: Can an executor also be a beneficiary?

- q: Does an executor get paid?
  a: A lay executor (a family member or friend) is not automatically entitled to payment beyond out-of-pocket expenses. A professional executor, such as a solicitor or bank, will charge a fee, which comes out of the estate. Always check what those fees are before appointing them.
- q: How long does being an executor take?
  a: For a straightforward estate, expect six to twelve months from death to final distribution. A larger or more complicated estate, one involving property, business interests or multiple beneficiaries, can run to two years or longer.
