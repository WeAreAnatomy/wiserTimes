# Driving with a disability in the UK: what help is available

Disability doesn't automatically mean the end of your driving life. For many people, the right combination of medical clearance, vehicle adaptations and professional assessment means they can keep driving safely for years. The key is knowing which steps to take, in which order, and where the genuine limits lie.

This guide covers the DVLA notification rules, how a specialist driving assessment works, vehicle adaptations that make a real difference, and the Motability scheme for those who qualify.

---

## What are the DVLA medical rules for disabled drivers?

The starting point is disclosure. If you develop a health condition or disability that could affect your driving, you're legally required to tell the DVLA. That includes conditions as varied as Parkinson's disease, a recent stroke, epilepsy, severe arthritis in your hands, insulin-treated diabetes and significant visual impairment.

The GOV.UK medical conditions tool (available at gov.uk/health-conditions-and-driving) lets you check whether your specific condition must be declared. The DVLA will then review your licence, sometimes asking for a GP report, a consultant's letter, or an eyesight test. In some cases they'll impose a short-term licence of one, two or three years rather than the standard renewal period, with a reassessment at the end.

Failing to declare a notifiable condition is a criminal offence. It also risks invalidating your car insurance. In my experience, people worry that declaring a condition means they'll lose their licence immediately, but that's rarely what happens. The DVLA's job is to assess fitness, not to remove licences wholesale.

If you hold a Group 2 licence (lorry or bus), the standards are stricter, and many conditions that are manageable for ordinary car driving will lead to a revocation of the higher licence.

## How does a MIDAS driving assessment work?

MIDAS stands for Mobility Information and Driving Assessment Services. It's the professional framework used by Regional Driving Assessment Centres (RDACs) across the UK to evaluate drivers with disabilities, older drivers experiencing difficulties, and anyone returning to driving after illness or injury.

There are around 20 RDACs in England, Scotland and Wales, most based at NHS rehabilitation units or mobility centres. You don't need a GP referral to book one, though your GP or consultant can refer you if they have concerns.

A typical assessment runs in two stages. First, a specialist occupational therapist carries out a clinical assessment of your physical and cognitive abilities: strength, range of movement, reaction times, vision. Then comes the on-road element, where you drive with a qualified driving instructor who holds a specialist disability qualification, observed by the OT. The car used is usually equipped with dual controls.

Afterwards, you receive a written report covering your current fitness to drive, any adaptations that would help, and a recommendation about whether further training is needed. The report isn't a legal document, but it carries real weight with the DVLA, with insurers, and with your family.

Costs run between £150 and £250 at most centres, depending on location and the complexity of the assessment. Some local authorities will subsidise or fully fund an assessment for residents, so it's worth asking your council's adult social care team before you book.

:::callout
**Finding your nearest RDAC:** The Forum of Mobility Centres (mobility-centres.org.uk) has a postcode search tool. Most centres have waiting times of two to eight weeks, so book early rather than waiting until a condition worsens.
:::

## What vehicle adaptations are available?

This is where the practical options open up considerably. Modern vehicle adaptations cover an enormous range of needs, from relatively simple additions to significant mechanical modifications.

**For hand and arm difficulties:** Spinner knobs (fitted to the steering wheel to allow one-handed turning) are cheap and widely available. Hand controls for the accelerator and brake are more involved, replacing foot pedal operation for drivers with leg or foot conditions. Joystick controls exist for those with very limited limb function, though these require extensive assessment and training.

**For leg and foot difficulties:** Left-foot accelerators allow drivers who can't use their right leg to use the left foot for acceleration without risk of confusing the pedals. Pedal extensions raise the pedals for shorter drivers or those who can't fully flex at the hip.

**For getting in and out:** Swivel seats are one of the more popular adaptations I see in practice. They rotate the front seat outward so you can enter and exit without twisting your spine or putting weight through a painful hip. Several manufacturers offer them, including Elap and CarAdapter, and they can usually be fitted to most mainstream cars.

For wheelchair users, access ramps and internal docking systems allow the wheelchair to be secured inside the vehicle. Some people choose to drive from their wheelchair using purpose-built controls; others transfer to an adapted seat.

**For vision and cognitive needs:** Wider mirrors, reversing cameras and parking sensors may sound like standard car accessories, but for drivers with reduced peripheral vision or slower processing speed, they move from nice-to-have to genuinely useful. A MIDAS assessor will flag which of these apply to your situation.

Adaptations should always be installed by an approved adaptor. Your RDAC can provide a list. Poorly fitted adaptations, or adaptations chosen without a professional assessment, can be more dangerous than driving without them.

## How does the Motability scheme work?

The Motability scheme allows people who receive certain disability benefits to use those payments to lease a car, scooter or powered wheelchair. It's funded through a partnership between the government and Motability Operations, a not-for-profit company.

To qualify, you need to be in receipt of:

- The higher rate mobility component of Disability Living Allowance (DLA)
- The enhanced rate mobility component of Personal Independence Payment (PIP)
- Armed Forces Independence Payment
- War Pensioners' Mobility Supplement

If you qualify, you hand over your mobility allowance (or part of it) in exchange for an all-inclusive lease that covers insurance, servicing, breakdown cover and tyres. You choose a car from the Motability fleet, which includes vehicles from most mainstream manufacturers. Some models require an advance payment on top of the allowance; others are available at no extra cost.

Adaptations are where the scheme becomes particularly useful. Motability has a network of approved adapters and will fund standard adaptations at no charge. More complex adaptations, such as a powered tailgate or full hand controls, may require a contribution, but Motability offers grants toward these costs. You apply through the scheme at the time of ordering your vehicle.

One practical note: the lease runs for three years, so the choice of vehicle matters. If your condition is changing, talk to your RDAC before committing to a particular model, so the car you choose can realistically accommodate adaptations you might need further down the line.

The Motability scheme doesn't cover everyone who has a disability. If you receive the standard rate of PIP mobility rather than the enhanced rate, you won't qualify. Age UK and Citizens Advice can help you check your benefit entitlement if you're unsure.

For a broader look at aids and adaptations available to people with mobility difficulties, see our [guide to mobility aids and home adaptations](/mobility-aids/).

## What financial help exists outside Motability?

If you don't qualify for Motability, driving adaptations can be expensive. A full set of hand controls fitted professionally costs roughly £1,500 to £3,000 depending on complexity. Swivel seats range from £300 to £800.

A few routes are worth knowing:

The **Disabled Facilities Grant (DFG)** is primarily for home adaptations, but it can in theory cover equipment that enables independent living. In practice, councils rarely fund vehicle adaptations through the DFG, though it's worth asking your local authority's occupational therapy team.

**VAT relief** applies to many vehicle adaptations if you have a disability. You pay no VAT on adaptations supplied to a disabled person for their own use. The supplier will ask you to complete a VAT exemption form; this isn't means-tested, and it applies to the adaptation itself even if the car purchase is subject to standard VAT.

**Motability grant scheme:** Even if you don't qualify for the main Motability lease scheme, the charity does provide one-off grants in some circumstances. The scheme's website has a grants section worth checking.

Some local charities and disability organisations also offer small grants toward vehicle adaptation costs. Your local RDAC will often have information about what's available in your area.

## When should you think about stopping driving?

This is the part people find hardest to raise, and I understand why. Driving represents independence, and for many people in rural areas or without nearby family, it's also genuinely necessary for day-to-day life.

That said, there are signs that suggest it's time for a proper review, if not an immediate stop. Unexplained scrapes or minor collisions, getting lost on familiar routes, difficulty judging gaps in traffic, or family members quietly expressing concern are all things worth taking seriously. They don't mean you must stop driving, but they do mean an honest assessment is overdue.

A MIDAS assessment is, in this context, a more useful conversation than one with your GP. GPs assess medical conditions; driving assessors assess actual driving performance. The two aren't the same.

If the assessment confirms that continued driving isn't safe, the assessor can also help you think through alternatives: community transport schemes, local authority transport for older people, taxi subsidies available in some areas, and whether a powered mobility scooter or electric wheelchair could restore some of the independence that driving currently provides.

Giving up driving is not a small thing. But giving it up with a plan in place is much less frightening than doing it in a crisis.
