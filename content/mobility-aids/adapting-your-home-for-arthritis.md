# Practical home adaptations if you have arthritis

Arthritis doesn't affect any two people in exactly the same way, but the pattern I see most often in people's homes is this: the big things are manageable, and the small, repetitive things are exhausting. Turning a tap. Getting the lid off a jar. Pushing yourself up from the toilet. Done once, these are trivial. Done a dozen times a day with inflamed, stiff joints, they grind people down.

The good news is that most of these problems have practical solutions, many of them inexpensive, several of them free through the council.

## Where should you start?

A free occupational therapy (OT) assessment from your local council is the right first step for most people. I'd say this not because I'm an OT, but because it genuinely changes what people do. A good community OT will walk through your home with you, spot the hazards you've stopped noticing, and arrange for the council to supply basic aids directly. That can mean grab rails installed by a handyperson service, a raised toilet seat, or a bath board, all without you spending a penny.

To request one, contact your local council's adult social care team. Your GP can also refer you, and in my experience that can shorten the wait. While you're waiting for the assessment, the rest of this guide will give you a clear sense of what's out there.

:::callout
**Not sure where to start with the council?** Versus Arthritis has a helpline on 0800 5200 520, and Age UK can help you work out which services you're entitled to. Neither will leave you on hold for forty minutes and then read from a script.
:::

## What can you change in the kitchen?

The kitchen is usually where people feel the sharpest loss of independence. Gripping, twisting and lifting are all things arthritis attacks.

**Lever taps** are the single most effective kitchen adaptation for most people with hand or wrist arthritis. A standard crosshead or capstan tap requires you to grip and rotate. A lever tap needs only a downward press with the side of your hand or your forearm on a bad day. Lever tap converters (essentially a replacement head that fits over your existing fittings) cost from around £15 and involve no plumbing. NRS Healthcare stocks several options. If you're renting, these are non-invasive enough that most landlords won't object.

**Jar openers** matter more than people admit. Electric jar openers require almost no grip at all; a One Touch automatic opener costs around £20 and sits on the counter. A simpler rubber disc opener costs under a pound and is surprisingly effective for moderate arthritis. I'd suggest having both.

Long-handled kitchen tools (reaching aids, kettle tippers, angled utensils) reduce the need to stretch or twist. A kettle tipper holds the kettle in a cradle so you tilt it to pour rather than lifting it. Brands like Uccello make versions that are actually well-designed rather than institutional-looking, which matters when the object is sitting on your worktop every day.

A few other kitchen adjustments worth considering:

- Pull-out larder shelves or a lazy Susan reduce the need to reach deep into cupboards
- A spike board (suction-fixed to the worktop) holds food steady while you peel or cut single-handed
- Lightweight pots and pans. This sounds obvious, but people often don't replace heavy cast-iron cookware until someone points it out

## How do you make the bathroom safer?

Bathrooms carry the highest falls risk of any room in the house, and arthritis compounds that by reducing both grip strength and balance. Wet floors and limited mobility are a difficult combination.

**Grab rails** are the foundation. A rail beside the toilet, one at bath entry and exit, and a rail in the shower cubicle cover the four highest-risk moments. The council will often supply and fit grab rails following an OT assessment. If you're self-funding, you'll typically pay £30 to £80 per rail for a good-quality stainless steel version from NRS Healthcare or similar suppliers, plus fitting costs if you're not comfortable drilling into tiles yourself.

Position matters. A rail that's too low, or fixed to a stud wall without the right anchor, is worse than no rail at all. If you have any doubt about the fixing, pay someone to fit it properly.

**Raised toilet seats** reduce the distance your knees and hips have to travel, which is significant for people with lower-limb arthritis. A standard raised seat (adding 5 to 10 cm) clips onto most standard toilet pans without tools. Versions with arms give you something to push up from. These cost between £30 and £70 from mainstream stockists including NRS Healthcare and Boots. Your OT may be able to supply one directly.

For people who find wiping difficult due to shoulder or elbow arthritis, a Closomat toilet or a bidet attachment is worth knowing about. A retrofit bidet seat (such as those made by Bio Bidet or Brondell) fits to most standard pans for around £100 to £300 and removes the need for significant reaching altogether.

**Shower adaptations** vary enormously depending on your bathroom. A fixed fold-down shower seat, a handheld showerhead, and a non-slip mat are the starting point. Moving to a full wet room (level-access shower, no threshold) is a more significant project that usually requires a Disabled Facilities Grant or self-funding; your council OT can advise on eligibility.

## What about moving around the house?

Stairs are an obvious concern, but people often overlook the smaller obstacles: door handles that require gripping and rotating, heavy fire doors, thick carpet that slows a walking frame, or a bath step at the front door.

**Lever door handles** replace round knobs and require only a downward press. Replacing handles throughout a house typically costs £15 to £40 per door for the handle itself, plus fitting if needed. This is one of the higher-impact changes you can make for rheumatoid arthritis affecting the hands, and it's something most people can manage themselves with a screwdriver and thirty minutes.

**Handrails on both sides of the stairs** are often overlooked. Most homes have one rail on one side. If your stronger or less-affected side happens to be on the wrong side on the way down, that single rail is almost useless. A second handrail is inexpensive and often something a local handyperson service will fit. Ask your council whether they have a linked service; many do.

If the stairs themselves are becoming a serious obstacle, a stairlift may be worth considering. Stannah and Handicare are the two largest UK manufacturers; both offer straight and curved models and will carry out a home survey. This [guide to stairlifts on Wiser Times](/mobility-aids/stairlift-buyers-guide/) covers the options in more detail.

## Are there aids for the living room and bedroom?

People often invest in kitchen and bathroom adaptations and leave the rooms they spend most of their time in unchanged. That's worth revisiting.

**Furniture raisers** lift chair and sofa legs by 5 to 10 cm, reducing the effort required to stand up. They're inexpensive (around £15 for a set) and non-destructive. A riser-recliner chair is the more comprehensive version: it lifts you to a near-standing position at the press of a button. These start at around £500 from suppliers like HSL and Repose and are worth considering if getting up from chairs has become genuinely difficult.

**Bedroom adaptations** for arthritis often focus on bed height and dressing. Bed raisers work similarly to furniture raisers. A bed lever (a handled rail that slots under the mattress) helps with sitting up. For dressing, long-handled shoe horns, elastic laces, button hooks and dressing sticks are unglamorous but practical. NRS Healthcare has a wide catalogue of these and will post items out quickly.

Light switch extensions and touch-sensitive bedside lamp controls are small changes that matter at the end of a day when your hands hurt.

## What if you want to make bigger changes?

Some adaptations, such as a ground-floor bathroom, a through-floor lift, or full wet room conversion, are major building works. These are almost never funded entirely from the council's discretionary aids budget, but the Disabled Facilities Grant (DFG) can help.

In England, the DFG is means-tested and offers up to £30,000 (£36,000 in Wales) toward major adaptations. You apply through your local council; an OT referral is generally required as part of the process. The assessment considers both your clinical need and your financial circumstances. It's worth applying even if you're uncertain whether you qualify, because the thresholds are not as restrictive as many people expect.

Your OT can support your application and, in many cases, write the referral letter that triggers it.

:::callout
**A useful check before any major work:** contact your local council's Disabled Facilities Grant team before appointing a builder. The grant comes with specific requirements about the work and the contractors used. Commissioning work before approval can affect your eligibility.
:::

## What about aids for hands and wrists specifically?

This area is often underserved in general guides. For people whose arthritis is centred in the hands, fine motor tasks (writing, using a phone, cooking) are the main difficulty.

Wide-grip pens and pencils reduce the pinch required to write. Touchscreen styluses remove the need for precise fingertip control. Key turners (a handle that clips around a key) make locking and unlocking a door significantly easier and cost under £10. These are the sorts of aids that an OT assessment will flag, but that you can also source yourself quickly through NRS Healthcare or Amazon.

Voice-controlled smart home devices (Amazon Echo, Google Nest) reduce the need to operate physical switches, taps and controls. For some people this is a minor convenience; for others with severe hand arthritis it materially changes how manageable home life feels. There's no single right answer.
