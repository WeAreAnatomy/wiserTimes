# What is an unpaid carer and what support is available?

If you help a partner, parent, sibling or friend to manage daily life because of illness, disability, frailty or mental health difficulties, you are almost certainly an unpaid carer. No certificate or formal title is involved. Around 5 million people in the UK are in this position, according to Carers UK, and a significant number of them have never used that word to describe themselves. That matters, because the support available to unpaid carers in the UK is genuinely useful, and most of it goes unclaimed.

This guide explains what unpaid caring means in practice, who qualifies for what, and how to get the ball rolling.

---

## What counts as unpaid caring?

Unpaid caring covers an enormous range of situations. At one end sits a daughter who moved her mother into the spare room after a stroke and now manages medication, personal care, meals and hospital appointments. At the other sits a husband who drives his wife to dialysis three times a week and picks up her prescription. Both are carers. Both have the same right to support.

The common thread is this: you are doing something regularly, without pay, that the person couldn't safely or comfortably manage without you. That includes physical care like bathing, dressing or medication management, but also emotional support, financial administration, or simply being present so that the person isn't unsafe alone.

Age UK puts it plainly: if you're providing unpaid support to a family member or friend who could not cope without that help, you're a carer.

---

## Why do so many people not realise they're carers?

Partly because caring tends to creep up. A husband starts collecting a prescription. Then he takes over the cooking. Then he's managing GP appointments, organising hospital transport, and doing the laundry, all while still thinking of himself as simply doing what any spouse would do.

There's also a cultural reluctance, particularly among people in their sixties and seventies, to identify as someone who needs support themselves. Caring feels like love or duty, not a category that comes with rights attached.

But here's what changes when you do identify as a carer: you gain access to a carer's assessment, potential financial support, respite options, and in some circumstances, priority for NHS health checks. None of that is available if you don't come forward.

---

## What is a carer's assessment and how do you get one?

A carer's assessment is a free conversation with your local authority's adult social care team about how caring is affecting your life. Not the person you care for, you. Your sleep, your health, your ability to work or socialise, your own plans.

It's a legal right under the Care Act 2014, regardless of how many hours you provide, how much the person you care for earns, or whether you live with them. The local authority cannot refuse one.

To request it, contact your local council's adult social care department directly. You can find the right number on GOV.UK by searching your council name alongside "carer's assessment". The process itself is usually a phone call or home visit with a social worker. In my experience it's far less formal than people expect, and far more useful.

What can come out of it varies: access to local carer support groups, a personal budget to cover replacement care so you can take a break, referral to NHS carer health checks, or simply having your situation recorded so that if there's a crisis, services know you exist.

:::callout
**Start here if you're unsure what to do next.** Ring your local council's adult social care team and ask for a carer's assessment. You do not need a GP referral, a diagnosis for the person you care for, or any documentation. You just need to ask.
:::

## What financial support is available to unpaid carers?

This is where it gets slightly complicated, so I'll take each element separately.

**Carer's Allowance** is the main benefit for carers of working age. As of April 2024 it pays £81.90 per week, provided you're caring for someone for at least 35 hours a week and they're receiving a qualifying disability benefit (usually Personal Independence Payment at the daily living component, or Attendance Allowance). You can't earn more than £151 net per week after allowable deductions and still claim it. Apply through GOV.UK.

One thing worth knowing: Carer's Allowance has a cliff edge. Earn £1 over the threshold and you lose the entire payment, not just the excess. If you're working part-time while caring, it's worth calculating very carefully, or asking a benefits adviser at Citizens Advice to do it with you.

**Carer's Credit** is useful if you're not eligible for Carer's Allowance, perhaps because you're already retired or not caring enough hours. It protects your National Insurance record, which matters if you haven't yet reached the 35 qualifying years needed for the full new State Pension.

**Pension Credit and Council Tax Reduction** can both include an additional element for carers in some circumstances. These are means-tested and worth checking on the government's benefits calculator or through Age UK's benefits check service, which is free.

If you're struggling financially because of your caring role and not sure where to start, Turn2us has a free online benefits calculator and grant search that's straightforward to use.

## What is respite care and how do you access it?

Respite means a break. It sounds simple, but for many carers it's one of the hardest things to arrange, partly because finding someone reliable to step in feels impossible, and partly because leaving can feel like abandonment.

A carer's assessment can trigger a funded respite arrangement. The local authority might pay for a care worker to come to the home for a few hours a week, or fund a short residential stay for the person you care for, giving you days rather than hours off.

Demand usually outstrips supply for council-funded respite. Some families end up self-funding through private care agencies. Others use the NHS Continuing Healthcare route if the person they care for has very complex health needs, though this is assessed separately and isn't guaranteed.

Carers Trust has a network of local organisations across the UK that provide everything from sitting services (a volunteer comes to stay with your relative while you go out) to day centres and emergency respite schemes. Their website has a "find support near you" function worth using.

One more option: if the person you care for attends a day centre through their own social care package, that time is effectively respite for you too, even if it wasn't framed that way.

## What home adaptations might help a carer's situation?

This sits more firmly in my professional lane. A lot of caring is physically hard work, and the right adaptations can make an enormous difference to both the carer and the person being cared for.

Grab rails beside the toilet or bath, fitted by the council after a home assessment, reduce the number of times you need to physically support someone during personal care. A ramp at the front door means you're not managing a step with a wheelchair. A raised toilet seat or a shower seat bought from NRS Healthcare for relatively little money can reduce how much physical effort each task takes.

For larger adaptations, a Disabled Facilities Grant (DFG) from the local authority can contribute up to £30,000 in England toward major works like a stairlift, a through-floor lift, or a wet room conversion. The grant is means-tested for adults but not for disabled children. An occupational therapist assessment is part of the process, and that assessment is free.

If the home isn't suitable for adaptation, or if you're a carer who has taken someone with significant needs into a property that wasn't designed for it, a housing occupational therapist can also advise on whether rehousing is a more practical route. It's not always the right answer, but it's worth raising.

You can read more about this in our [home adaptations and the Disabled Facilities Grant guide](/mobility-aids/disabled-facilities-grant/).

## Where can carers find emotional and practical support?

The organisations worth knowing about are Carers UK, Carers Trust and, if the person you're caring for is older, Age UK. All three have helplines, local groups and online resources. None of them will judge you for struggling.

Carers UK runs an online forum where carers talk to each other. It's low-key, honest, and often more useful than a helpline for the kind of "is this normal?" questions that are hard to ask out loud. They also have a free advice line on 0808 808 7777, open on Wednesdays and Thursdays.

Your GP can record your carer status on your medical record. This opens access to things like an annual health check and means that if you become unwell, the practice knows there's a dependent at home. It takes one conversation at the surgery and most practices are actively trying to identify their carer patients.

Finally, if you're employed: the right to request flexible working became a day-one right in April 2024 under the Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act. You don't need six months' service to ask. You do still need your employer to agree, but the request itself is now protected from the first day. Carers UK's website has a template letter and guidance if you're not sure how to approach it.

## Questions carers often ask

**Do I count as an unpaid carer if I only help occasionally?**
Possibly. There's no minimum number of hours that defines a carer legally, though Carer's Allowance requires at least 35 hours of care per week. Even if you help less than that, you're still entitled to a carer's assessment from your local authority.

**Can I claim Carer's Allowance if I'm already receiving a State Pension?**
You can apply, but Carer's Allowance is an overlapping benefit. If your State Pension is equal to or more than the Carer's Allowance rate, you won't receive any extra cash, though you may still get underlying entitlement that boosts other benefits.

**What is a carer's assessment and who carries it out?**
A carer's assessment looks at how caring affects your life, your health and your own needs. It's carried out by your local council's adult social care team, usually a social worker. It's free, it's your legal right, and it doesn't affect the support the person you care for receives.

**How do I get respite care arranged?**
Start with a carer's assessment. If your local authority agrees you need a break, they may fund or arrange respite, either at home through a care agency or in a short-stay residential placement. Carers Trust and Age UK also have local networks that can point you toward options your council doesn't fund.

**Is there help for carers who are also working?**
Yes. You have the legal right to request flexible working from day one of employment under rules that came into force in April 2024. Some employers also offer carer's leave. Carers UK publishes a regularly updated guide to your workplace rights worth bookmarking.
:::
