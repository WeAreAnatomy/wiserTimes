# How to prevent falls at home: a practical room-by-room guide

Around one in three adults over 65 falls at least once a year, according to the NHS. For many, the fall happens at home, somewhere familiar, somewhere they've moved around safely for decades. That's partly why home falls are so easy to dismiss until they aren't. The good news is that most home fall risks are fixable, and many fixes cost very little. This guide works through your home room by room, covering the changes that actually make a difference.

If you'd like the broader context on mobility aids and home adaptations, our [main mobility and home adaptations guide](/mobility-aids/) covers everything from stairlifts to wet rooms.

## Why does fall risk increase with age?

Balance, muscle strength and reaction time all decline gradually with age, and that process accelerates if someone has been ill, had a hospital stay, or is taking certain medications. Conditions such as Parkinson's, osteoporosis, inner-ear problems and poor vision all add to the picture. So does medication: sedatives, blood pressure drugs and some antidepressants affect stability more than many people realise.

This isn't meant to alarm. It's worth saying clearly because understanding the mechanisms helps you see why the solutions work. Better lighting helps because ageing eyes take longer to adjust to contrast changes. Grab rails help because reaction time is slower, so instinctive grab-and-recover movements don't happen as quickly. Exercise helps because it rebuilds the muscle strength and proprioception (the body's sense of its own position) that naturally reduce over time.

## How do I know which rooms are highest risk?

The bathroom and the stairs account for the largest share of home falls, in my experience. The bathroom because it combines wet surfaces, awkward movements and often inadequate grab points. The stairs because a fall there carries the most serious consequences.

That said, a proper risk picture needs a proper assessment. A local authority OT assessment is free, and it's the right first step for most people who've had a fall or feel their balance is changing. Your GP can refer you, or you can contact your local adult social care team directly. The OT will visit your home, watch how you move through it, and make specific recommendations rather than generic ones.

## What can I do in the bathroom?

Start with the floor. A wet bathroom floor is genuinely dangerous, and a non-slip mat in the bath or shower tray is a basic minimum. Look for mats with suction cups on the underside; they're more stable than rubber-backed alternatives.

Grab rails matter more than most people expect. A well-placed rail next to the toilet (at the right height for you specifically) makes sitting down and standing up far more controlled. Similarly, a rail on the wall of the bath or shower helps steady you as you step in and out. These aren't medicalised-looking additions anymore: brands such as NRS Healthcare offer rails in brushed steel and chrome that look entirely at home in a modern bathroom.

A few other changes worth considering:

- A bath board or bath seat, which allows you to sit on the edge before swinging your legs over rather than stepping over the rim
- A raised toilet seat if getting up from low seating is difficult
- A lever tap to reduce the grip and twisting required

If your bathroom needs more substantial work, a level-access shower (sometimes called a wet room) eliminates the step-over problem entirely. That's almost always self-funded, though a Disabled Facilities Grant may contribute depending on your circumstances.

:::callout
**Not sure where to start in the bathroom?** Contact your local council's adult social care team. They can arrange a free OT assessment and, in many cases, fund grab rail installation at no cost to you.
:::

## What about the stairs?

A stairlift is the obvious solution when stairs become difficult, but before you get there, some simpler changes can make a real difference.

Make sure both handrails run the full length of the staircase, not just partway. If you only have one rail, adding a second on the opposite wall is often a small job for a local handyperson and provides bilateral support. Check that the rails are properly secured; a wobbly rail can cause a fall rather than prevent one.

Lighting matters a great deal here. A poorly lit staircase is a hazard at any age, but more so when contrast sensitivity and depth perception have reduced. Motion-activated lights mean you're never starting a stair descent in the dark because you forgot to switch the light on.

Stair nosings (the contrasting strip on the edge of each step) are worth adding to any staircase where the step edges are hard to distinguish. You can buy adhesive versions for around £15-20 per staircase.

When stairs become genuinely unmanageable, a stairlift is a reasonable solution. Stannah and Handicare both offer curved and straight models, and most providers will do a home survey before you commit to anything.

## What changes help in the kitchen?

The kitchen is where people tend to over-reach, particularly for items stored in high cupboards. That combination of reaching, twisting and standing on tiptoe (or on a step) is a significant fall risk. Reorganising storage so that frequently used items sit between hip and shoulder height costs nothing.

Anti-fatigue mats near the sink and cooker reduce tiredness during prolonged standing. Slip-resistant flooring is worth considering if you're ever replacing kitchen floor, but if that's not in the budget, ensuring the existing floor is clean of grease and always dry is the more immediate priority.

A perching stool (a stool angled slightly forward to allow a semi-standing position) can help if standing for long periods is tiring. NRS Healthcare stocks several options, and some GP surgeries or OT services will trial one with you before you buy.

## How does lighting affect fall risk around the home generally?

Poor lighting is one of the most underestimated risk factors, and one of the cheapest to address. The NHS recommends at least 100 lux in areas where tasks are performed, and stairways specifically should be well lit at both the top and bottom.

Plug-in night lights on the route between the bedroom and the bathroom are straightforward to install and make a genuine difference for anyone who gets up in the night. Motion-sensor versions mean you don't need to remember to turn them on.

Replace any blown bulbs promptly. That sounds obvious, but I've visited homes where a ceiling light has been out for months because replacing it required a ladder nobody felt comfortable using. A local handyperson service (Age UK branches often run these) is worth knowing about.

## Rugs and flooring: what should I remove?

Loose rugs are one of the most consistent hazards I see in home assessments, particularly on smooth floors. The rug shifts slightly, the edge curls, and a foot catches it. If you're not ready to remove a rug entirely (and some rugs have sentimental weight as well as practical use), a non-slip underlay and double-sided tape around the perimeter reduce the risk.

Thick-pile carpets present a different problem: they can catch the front of a foot, particularly for anyone who shuffles or has limited foot clearance. Low-pile or loop-pile carpets are more forgiving.

Transition strips between different flooring types are often a trip point, especially where there's a height difference between a carpet and a tiled area. A local handyperson can usually level these out or add a smooth ramped strip.

## What role do exercises play?

A great deal, actually. Exercise isn't a nice-to-have addition to a home safety plan: it's arguably the most evidence-backed fall prevention measure available. Strengthening the muscles around the hip and knee, and improving proprioception, directly reduces the likelihood of a loss of balance becoming a fall.

The Otago Exercise Programme was developed in New Zealand but is widely used across NHS falls services here. It involves a structured set of leg strengthening and balance exercises, progressively increased, and the evidence for it reducing falls in older adults is substantial. Many NHS trusts offer it in group sessions or as a home programme.

At an everyday level, simple practices help:

- Sit-to-stand exercises from a chair (no arms, hands resting on thighs) build the quad strength that matters most for stability
- Standing on one leg while holding a worktop builds single-leg balance
- Heel-to-toe walking along a hallway (like walking a tightrope) improves dynamic balance

I'd suggest starting gradually and always near something solid you can hold. If you've had a fall or have a condition affecting your balance, ask your GP for a referral to an NHS falls prevention service before beginning a new exercise programme.

:::callout
**NHS falls services are available across England, Scotland and Wales.** Your GP can refer you, or search for your local service on the NHS website. Many services include both exercise programmes and a home assessment visit.
:::

## What footwear should I wear indoors?

This question comes up in almost every home visit I do. The answer is firmer than most people expect: proper footwear, even indoors, makes a meaningful difference.

A good indoor shoe or slipper has a thin, firm sole (not the thick cushioned type, which reduces ground-feel), a non-slip base, and a closed heel to keep the foot secure. Brands like Cosyfeet make indoor shoes specifically designed with these features and with wider fittings for swollen feet.

Walking around in socks on a smooth floor is one of the most common hazards in any home assessment. Even if the floor is dry and clear, socks provide very little friction and no ankle support.

## Frequently asked questions

**How do I get a free falls assessment at home?**
Ask your GP to refer you to your local NHS falls prevention service, or contact your local authority's adult social care team. An occupational therapist will visit and assess your home at no cost. Many areas also accept self-referrals directly through the NHS.

**Are non-slip mats enough to make a bathroom safe?**
They help, but they're rarely sufficient on their own. Grab rails positioned correctly for your height and balance make a far bigger difference. A qualified OT can advise on the right height and angle for your home specifically.

**Does the council pay for grab rails and ramps?**
Small adaptations such as grab rails and simple external ramps are frequently funded by the local authority, often at no cost to you. Larger work may qualify for a Disabled Facilities Grant. Eligibility depends on your local council and your financial situation.

**What footwear actually reduces fall risk indoors?**
Shoes or slippers with a firm, thin, non-slip sole and a closed heel. Thick-soled slippers feel comfortable but can reduce floor-feel and stability. Avoid walking in socks or bare feet, particularly on wooden or tiled floors.

**Which exercises help most with balance?**
Heel-to-toe walking, single-leg standing (near a wall or worktop for safety) and sit-to-stand repetitions from a chair are among the most practical. The NHS-backed Otago Exercise Programme has strong evidence behind it and can be done at home.
:::
