# Live-in care vs a care home: how do they compare?

Live-in care means a paid carer moves into your home and supports you there, usually on a rota with another carer covering breaks. A care home means you move into a staffed residential setting. Both can provide high levels of support, but the daily experience, cost and practical arrangements are quite different. Which works better depends on your health, your home, your finances and, frankly, your personality.

This guide walks through what each option actually involves day to day, what families typically need to arrange, and the questions worth asking before making a decision.

## What does live-in care actually look like day to day?

Your carer arrives, or is already there, when you wake up. They help with whatever you need: washing, dressing, breakfast, medication. The rest of the morning might look like your ordinary morning always did, because you are still in your own home, with your own kitchen, your own garden, your dog on the sofa.

A live-in carer is typically on duty for around ten hours a day and should have a two-hour break. They sleep at your home overnight, and while they are not expected to provide care through the night as a matter of routine, most carers will respond if you need them. (Contracts vary, so it is worth clarifying this with any agency.)

In my experience working with families through NHS community teams, the biggest adjustment is often less about the care itself and more about having someone living in the house. For people who value privacy or have always been independent, that takes getting used to. For others, the company is genuinely welcome.

Practically, you need a spare bedroom in reasonable condition. The carer is not a lodger in the traditional sense, but they do need somewhere decent to sleep and some personal space. A Victorian terrace with two bedrooms can work fine. A single-bedroom flat almost certainly cannot.

## What does a care home day look like?

Care homes vary enormously, and it is a mistake to imagine a single typical day. A good residential home runs to a rhythm: breakfast, activities, lunch, rest periods, afternoon programmes, dinner, evening wind-down. Staff are present throughout, which means help is always nearby, though it also means your schedule is shaped by the home's routines rather than entirely your own.

Nursing homes add qualified nurses to the staffing mix and are registered to care for people with more complex clinical needs: wound management, PEG feeding, complex medication regimes.

Residents have private rooms in most modern homes, sometimes with en-suite facilities. Common areas are shared. Some homes have gardens, hairdressers, visiting entertainers. Quality varies considerably: the Care Quality Commission (CQC) inspects and rates every registered care home in England, and their reports are publicly searchable at cqc.org.uk. Reading the most recent inspection report before visiting is time well spent.

:::callout
**Before you visit any care home**, look it up on the CQC website. Filter by "Outstanding" or "Good" ratings and read the full inspection report, not just the headline score. Pay particular attention to the "Safe" and "Well-led" domains.
:::

## Who does each option suit?

There is no clean answer here, but some patterns emerge.

Live-in care tends to work well for people who are strongly attached to their home and neighbourhood, couples where one partner needs care (a single carer can often support both, which changes the economics significantly), people with mild to moderate dementia who benefit from familiar surroundings, and those with pets or gardens they are not ready to leave behind.

A care home tends to suit people who need a level of clinical oversight that a single carer cannot safely provide, those whose homes are not practically adaptable (some properties really are too small or too inaccessible), people who are socially isolated at home and would benefit from the community a good home provides, and those whose families cannot carry any coordinating role.

This is not a hierarchy. Neither option is inherently better. Some people thrive in care homes and find them genuinely freeing after years of struggling at home alone. Others would find the move devastating. Honest conversations with the person who needs care, not just with family members, matter here.

## What do families need to arrange for live-in care?

More than many people expect, at least initially.

If you use an agency (firms like Helping Hands, Prestige Nursing + Care or Elder are well-established UK providers), the agency handles recruitment, payroll, holiday cover and most compliance. Your job is to brief them thoroughly, be at home for the initial introduction if possible, and stay in regular contact about how things are going.

If you employ a carer directly, the administrative load is significantly heavier. You become an employer, which means payroll, National Insurance, holiday pay, and a DBS check as a minimum. Some families manage this and save money in the process. Many find it more than they bargained for.

Either way, the home usually needs some adaptation before a carer moves in. A hospital-style bed may be easier for the carer as well as the resident. Grab rails in the bathroom, a shower seat, adequate lighting on the stairs. A local authority OT assessment (free, arranged through your council) is the sensible first step, and our [home adaptations guide](/mobility-aids/home-adaptations/) covers the practical detail.

The carer also needs feeding, warmth and a workable living arrangement. Small things matter: a decent mattress in their room, access to a kettle, somewhere to keep their food in the fridge. It sounds obvious, but agencies sometimes raise it with families because it occasionally gets overlooked.

## What do families need to arrange for a care home?

The move itself is the main logistical task, and it is not a small one. Furniture, clothing, personal items. Deciding what happens to the family home, at least in the short term. Redirecting post, managing finances, informing the GP and arranging transfer of medical records.

Many care homes ask for two to four weeks' notice if you wish to leave, which is worth knowing in advance.

Funding is the other significant arrangement. If the person moving in has assets above £23,250 (the current threshold in England; thresholds differ in Wales and Scotland), they are expected to fund their own care. Below that threshold, the local authority contributes on a means-tested basis. The NHS funds care home places for people with primarily health-based needs through Continuing Healthcare, though eligibility is assessed carefully and approval is not guaranteed.

Getting independent financial advice before committing to a care home, particularly regarding property, is sensible. This is not a decision to make quickly or without proper information.

## How do the costs compare?

Both options are expensive, and the costs have risen considerably over the past few years.

Live-in care from an agency typically runs from around £900 to £1,800 per week, depending on the level of care needed, the agency, and the region. Self-employed carer arrangements tend to be lower, but carry more administrative and legal responsibility for the family.

Residential care home fees average around £800 to £1,000 per week in England, according to figures published by Age UK. Nursing home fees are higher, often £1,000 to £1,500 or more. London and the South East tend to sit at the top of these ranges.

For a single person, the two options are broadly comparable in cost, with the choice driven more by preference and clinical need than by price. For a couple, live-in care frequently works out cheaper, sometimes substantially so.

Neither option is covered by a simple state benefit. NHS Continuing Healthcare aside, most people fund their own care, at least until assets fall to the relevant threshold.

:::callout
**A care needs assessment from your local authority is free and is the right place to start**, whether you're leaning towards live-in care or a care home. It establishes what support is needed and what funding may be available. You can request one by contacting adult social services at your council.
:::

## What questions should you ask before deciding?

A few that I'd suggest are worth sitting with, rather than rushing past.

How does the person who needs care feel about having someone in their home, or about moving out of it? Their answer matters, even if it's complicated by cognitive changes.

What does the home actually look like physically? Is there a spare room? Can the bathroom be adapted? Would the layout work for a wheelchair or walking frame?

What level of clinical care is needed now, and what might be needed in six or twelve months? Live-in care can accommodate a lot, but it has limits, and arranging a second move is disruptive and upsetting.

Who in the family can realistically take on a coordinating role? Live-in care, in particular, works better when someone is engaged and available to communicate with the agency, respond to changes, and visit regularly.

There is no formula that produces the right answer. I'd suggest speaking to a social worker or independent care adviser as well as to providers themselves. The Elderly Accommodation Counsel (at eac.org.uk) offers an independent directory and advice service that some families find useful as a starting point.
